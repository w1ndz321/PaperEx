RGI (rhamnogalacturonan-I) pectin, abundant in nature, often exhibits poor solubility due to molecular aggre-
gation  during  thermal  drying.  As  an  anionic  polysaccharide,  its  intermolecular  interactions  are  highly  pH-
sensitive  and  can  be  effectively  modulated  during  ethanol  precipitation,  which  is  conventionally  conducted
under  near-neutral  conditions.  This  study  therefore  systematically  investigated  how  ethanol  precipitation  pH
(PpH)  regulates  the  solubility  and  aggregation  behavior  of  citrus-derived  RGI-rich  pectin.  A  sharp  transition
occurred between PpH 3 and 4, where solubility dropped from 77.51% to 39.82%, accompanied by increases in
molecular weight (381.5 to 662.4 kDa). Microstructural analyses showed that acidic precipitation (PpH  = 1–3)
produced  loose,  porous  architectures  favorable  for  hydration.  Intermolecular  interactions,  fluorescence  probe
and thermal analyses further revealed that enhanced hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions dominated
aggregation under higher PpH  conditions. This study highlights precipitation pH adjustment as a simple, non-
chemical strategy to improve the solubility of RGI-rich pectin, providing practical guidance for its processing
and utilization.

Pectin,  a  complex  polysaccharide  predominantly  found  in  the  pri-
mary cell walls and middle lamellae of higher plants, is well known for
its  gelling,  stabilizing,  and  thickening  properties  (Reichembach  &  de
Oliveira Petkowicz, 2021; Zhang et al., 2025). It consists of three main
domains:  homogalacturonan  (HG),  rhamnogalacturonan  I  (RGI),  and
rhamnogalacturonan II (RGII) (Chandel et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024).
Among these, RGI is particularly notable for its repeating disaccharide
backbone  [→2)-α-L-Rhap-(1  →  4)-α-D-GalpA-(1→],  which  are  heavily
substituted with neutral sugar side chains such as arabinan, galactan,
and arabinogalactan (Cui et al., 2024). RGI is widely distributed in plant
materials,  particularly  in  citrus  processing  by-products,  making  it  an
attractive resource for industrial utilization (Chandel et al., 2022; Niu
et al., 2024). Its unique structure also underpins its superior bioactivity
and functionality, which have drawn increasing interest for use in food,
pharmaceutical, and cosmetic industries (Chen et al., 2024; Jing et al.,

Despite its significant presence in nature, the industrial application
of RGI pectin remains constrained, primarily due to its poor solubility
after drying (del Amo-Mateos et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025). The poor
solubility of RGI pectin is primarily attributed to thermal aggregation.
During dehydration—particularly following ethanol precipitation—RGI
molecules tend to form dense macromolecular assemblies due to their
low  charge  density  and  flexible  side  chains,  which  favor  extensive
hydrogen  bonding  and  hydrophobic  interactions  (An  et  al.,  2026;  Bu
et al., 2022; Lan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020, 2025). These aggregated
structures exhibit  limited  solubility  in  aqueous  solutions, limiting  the
applicability of RGI pectin in food and pharmaceutical systems where
solubility is essential for availability (Assifaoui et al., 2024; Du et al.,
2023).

Our  previous  studies  have  attempted  to  improve  the  solubility  of
RGI-rich  pectin  by  blending  it  with  other  negatively  charged  poly-
saccharides (Xu et al., 2025). This strategy introduces steric hindrance

A simpler and more industrially viable alternative is to control the
aggregation  behavior  of  RGI  by  adjusting  the  precipitation  pH  (PpH),
defined as the pH of the extract prior to ethanol addition (Kalapathy &
Proctor, 2001). In conventional pectin extraction, the extract is usually
acidic and is neutralized before precipitation. Because RGI is an anionic
polysaccharide, its degree of ionization and inter-chain associations are
highly responsive to pH, particularly in the low-dielectric environment
induced by ethanol (Pang et al., 2024). Previous studies on HG and sugar
beet  pectins  suggest  that  PpH  can  affect  molecular  conformation,  hy-
dration, and aggregation during drying (Fu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2016,
2017;  Yang  et  al.,  2018,  2025),  and  anecdotal  observations  in
citrus-based RGI preparations also imply that PpH  influences solubility
and particle morphology (Hu et al., 2021). However, systematic mech-
anistic  understanding  of  how  PpH  regulates  RGI  aggregation  remains
limited,  particularly  regarding  the  roles  of  hydrogen  bonding,  hydro-
phobic  interactions,  and  molecular  packing.  It  can  therefore  be  hy-
pothesized that pH conditions during processing, especially the ethanol
precipitation step prior to drying, play a critical role in determining the
aggregation state and solubility of RGI-rich pectin.

This study aims to systematically investigate the influence of PpH (1-
7) on the thermal aggregation and solubility behavior of RGI-rich pectin
extracted from citrus processing by-products. Comprehensive analyses,
including  molecular  weight  distribution,  microstructure,  and  assess-
ment of intermolecular interactions, were performed using techniques
such  as  Fourier-transform  infrared  (FTIR)  spectroscopy,  differential
scanning calorimetry (DSC), and zeta potential measurement. This work
not only advances understanding of RGI aggregation mechanisms but
also proposes a process-oriented strategy to improve its functional sol-
ubility without chemical modification. These insights contribute to the
effective  utilization  of  citrus  processing  residues  and  support  the
development of RGI-based ingredients in water-based food systems.

2.2. Preparation and management of samples

2.2.1. Preparation of RGI-rich pectin samples at different ethanol
precipitation pH levels

RGI-rich pectin was extracted from citrus peel powder using an acid-
assisted low-temperature method. Briefly, citrus peel powder was mixed
with hydrochloric acid solution adjusted to the HCl solution (pH = 1)
maintaining a solid-to-liquid ratio of 1:20. The suspension was stirred at
40
C for 1.5 h and then cooled to room temperature. The extract was
filtered through a 400-mesh filter cloth to remove insoluble materials,
and the supernatant was collected.

their precipitation pH (PpH = 1–7), and all procedures were conducted in
triplicate. The mixture was left undisturbed for 2 h at room temperature,
filtered through a 400-mesh cloth, washed once with absolute ethanol to

2.2.2. Preparation of interaction-modulated pectin samples

The extraction procedure and conditions were the same as described
above.  After  centrifugation  and  filtration  to  obtain  the  supernatant,
sodium  chloride  (NaCl),  urea,  and  guanidine  hydrochloride  (Gu-HCl)
were added to the pectin extract to investigate the roles of electrostatic
interactions, hydrogen bonding, and hydrophobic interactions, respec-
tively. Specifically, NaCl was added at a concentration of 0.4% (w/v),
while  urea  and  Gu-HCl  were  each  added  at  1%  (w/v),  based  on  the
volume of the pectin extract. The pH of each solution was then adjusted
accordingly,  followed  by  ethanol  precipitation  and  subsequent  pro-
cessing as previously described.

2.8. Bulk density

Absolute  density  refers  to  the  actual  solid  density  of  a  material,
excluding  any  interparticle  voids,  whereas  bulk  density  includes  the

2.6. Microscopic observation of the dissolution process

The dissolution behavior of RGI-rich pectin was monitored using an
optical imaging method adapted from previous studies, with modifica-
tions to ensure stable imaging during water addition (Xu et al., 2025). To
prevent  disruptions  caused  by  changes  in  focal  length—commonly
encountered  in  conventional  upright  microscopy—an  inverted  micro-
scope (DMi1, Leica, Germany) was employed. This setup allowed water
to be introduced to the sample without altering the distance between the
objective lens and the observation field.

A small quantity of the pectin powder was evenly distributed on a
microscope  slide,  and  a  drop  of  deionized  water  was  added  using  a
precision syringe. Real-time imaging was conducted at room tempera-
ture, with images captured at 1-s intervals for a total of 10 min. The
observations were carried out using a 10 × objective lens in combination
with a 10 × eyepiece, providing a total magnification of 100 × . This
approach  enabled  detailed  visualization  of  the  dynamic  dissolution
process and provided qualitative insights into water–pectin interactions
and structural changes upon hydration.

3. Results and discussions

3.1. Solubility and aggregation behavior of RGI-rich pectin at different
precipitation pH

The dried pectin samples exhibited distinct morphologies across the
PpH gradient. Low-PpH samples (PpH = 1–3) appeared fluffy and loosely
packed, while high-PpH samples (PpH ≥4) formed dense, rigid solids with
markedly  reduced porosity (Fig.  1A). This pronounced  morphological
transition  coincided  with  a  progressive  increase  in  pectin  yield,  sug-
gesting that higher PpH enhances recovery efficiency. However, the same
conditions  appear  to  facilitate  tighter  molecular  packing  during  pre-
cipitation and drying, which in turn drives irreversible aggregation and
markedly  impairs  solubility.  Similar  effects  of  pH  on  pectin  network
structure  and  porosity  have  been  reported,  where  lower  pH  favours
more open, porous morphologies whereas higher pH conditions produce
denser, closely packed networks, likely due to altered charge screening
and chain associations (Souza Almeida et al., 2021).

Quantitative solubility measurements revealed a pronounced decline
between PpH  3 and 4, dropping from 77.51% to 39.82%, which paral-
leled  marked  differences  in  dissolution  behavior.  Although  the  pH  of
redissolved  dispersions  differed  among  PpH  groups  (Table  S1),  cross-

Collectively, these findings indicate that acidic precipitation condi-
tions  favor  the  formation  of  loosely  associated  pectin  structures  with
high aqueous solubility, whereas precipitation near neutral pH induces
the assembly of compact aggregates, thereby markedly reducing solu-
bility.  This  behaviour  is  consistent  with  pH-dependent  self-assembly
mechanisms described for other natural polysaccharides, where changes
in charge density  and hydration state govern aggregate structure and
dissolution performance (Asimakopoulou et al., 2024).

3.2.4. Microstructure of RGI-rich pectin under different precipitation pH

The microstructural characteristics of RGI-rich pectin under different
PpH  conditions  are  visualized  in  Fig.  4.  Notably,  samples  prepared  at
lower  PpH  exhibited  a  well-defined,  honeycomb-like  porous  network
with relatively thin pore walls and a more open architecture (Fig. 4A–C).
Within  this  structure,  pectin  chains  appeared  loosely  organized  in  a
filamentous  arrangement.  This  increased  structural  openness  likely
promoted  stronger  interactions  between  hydrophilic  groups  and  sur-
rounding  water  molecules,  thereby  enhancing  the  solubility  of  the
pectin.

As PpH increased (≥4), the microstructure gradually transitioned to a
more compact and collapsed morphology. The pore size decreased, and
the  network  became  thicker  and  more  irregular,  indicating  tighter
intermolecular association and enhanced aggregation. Similar structural
densification  has  been  reported  in  pectin  systems  undergoing  pH-

induced  conformational  rearrangement  and  aggregation  (Kastner
et  al.,  2017).  Such  structural  densification  is  consistent  with  the
increased  Mw  and  reduced  Rz  values  discussed  in  Section  3.2.1,
reflecting the formation of more tightly packed aggregates.

The micrographs in Fig. 4 therefore corroborate the conclusion that
PpH  modulates  the  aggregation  state  of  RGI-rich  pectin,  where  acidic
conditions favor a loosely packed network structure, while higher PpH
promotes structural compaction and reduced solubility.

3.2.5. Rheological properties of RGI-rich pectin

The  rheological  behavior  of  polysaccharide  solutions  is  generally
governed  by  multiple  factors,  including  molecular  weight,  concentra-
tion, and intermolecular aggregation tendencies (Cui et al., 2000). As
shown in Fig. 5A, all samples exhibited typical shear-thinning behavior,
indicating  that  the  weak  interaction  networks  formed  among  pectin
molecules  were  progressively  disrupted  under  external  shear.  This
observation  aligns  with  previous  reports,  in  which  weak  interactions
between pectin molecules were found to govern shear-thinning behavior
(Peng et al., 2016).

Notably, for samples that produced visible insoluble sediment after
redissolution (PpH 4–7), viscosity measurements were conducted on the
centrifuged  supernatants  (Section  2.11),  thereby  reflecting  the  flow
properties  of  the  soluble  fraction  rather  than  the  whole  dispersion.
Under  this  standardized  protocol,  the  apparent  viscosity  decreased
markedly with increasing PpH (Fig. 5A): samples precipitated at PpH 1–3
showed substantially higher viscosity across the entire shear-rate range,
whereas  samples  from  PpH  5–7  exhibited  much  lower  viscosity  and
converged to similarly low values at high shear. This trend agrees with
the  PpH-dependent  solubility/aggregation  results,  suggesting  that  pre-
cipitation under acidic conditions yields a larger effective soluble frac-
tion and a more entangled/interactive soluble phase, while higher PpH

3.3. Intermolecular interactions governing aggregation

3.3.1. Electrostatic interactions: zeta potential analysis

As RGI pectin is an anionic polysaccharide, its ionization state—and
hence  its  electrostatic  behavior—can  be  strongly  influenced  by  pH
(Rezvani & Goli, 2024). As shown in Fig. 6B, the absolute value of the
Zeta  potential  increased  with  increasing  PpH,  with  a  noticeable  shift
occurring around pH = 4. This behavior is attributable to the progressive
deprotonation  of  carboxyl  groups  when  the  system  pH  exceeds  their
pKa, leading to enhanced negative surface charge. Generally, a higher
absolute value of Zeta potential indicates improved colloidal stability,
which in the case of pectin would suggest better solubility. However, the
absolute Zeta potential values did not directly correlate with the solu-
bility data. This discrepancy may be due to the fact that Zeta potential
measurements were conducted on the supernatant portion of the sam-
ple,  thereby  excluding  the  insoluble  precipitates.  Poorly  soluble  or
highly aggregated components likely settled during sample preparation
and were not represented in the measurement, resulting in biased sta-
bility readings for only the dissolved fraction.

with rising PpH, indicating a higher degree of aggregation. These find-

3.3.2. Selective disruption analysis of intermolecular interactions

To further dissect the molecular forces underlying the pronounced
solubility loss observed at high PpH, we evaluated the effects of sodium
chloride (NaCl), urea, and guanidine hydrochloride (Gu-HCl) on pectin
aggregation behavior. These reagents selectively disrupt different non-
covalent  interactions:  NaCl  screens  electrostatic  repulsion  via  ionic
shielding, urea disrupts hydrogen bonding while also acting as a good
solvent for non-polar residues, and Gu-HCl serves as a potent chaotrope
capable  of  weakening  both  hydrogen  bonding  and  hydrophobic
interactions.

groups remain protonated (pKa ≈3.5) and uncharged, rendering NaCl
shielding effects minimal (Han et al., 2017; Kalapathy & Proctor, 2001;
Pieczywek  et  al.,  2021).  As  pH  increases,  carboxyl  group  ionization
enhances  negative  charge  density  and  thus  the  potential  for  ionic
shielding by NaCl; however, the resulting decrease in solubility indicates
that electrostatic screening alone is not sufficient to prevent association
and  precipitation  in  this  system.  Notably,  aggregation  may  already
initiate in the aqueous phase (especially for poorly soluble fractions),
and  hot-air  drying  further  involves  progressive  dehydration  that  con-
centrates the residual aqueous phase. Under such dehydration/concen-
tration conditions, ionic strength increases and electrostatic screening
may be amplified, which could facilitate closer chain–chain approach
and promote aggregation (Wang et al., 2019). Therefore, the NaCl effect
here  is  interpreted  as  an  indirect  mechanistic  implication  for
drying-stage  association  rather  than  a  direct  observation  of  chain
packing during drying.

In  contrast,  urea  and  Gu-HCl  markedly  altered  precipitation
morphology across the entire PpH  range. Samples remained floating at
the liquid surface and displayed an open, multi-porous gel-like structure
regardless  of  PpH.  Urea  can  directly  disrupt  internal  hydrogen  bonds
within polysaccharide chains, destabilizing their native helical confor-
mations and promoting chain unfolding (Diener et al., 2019; P´erez et al.,
2000;  Zdunek  et  al.,  2021).  It  also  interacts  with  water  molecules,
reducing their structural organization and hydration capability, while
improving the solvation of non-polar moieties (Jing et al., 2022; Zhang
et  al.,  2019).  Gu-HCl,  a  stronger  chaotropic  agent,  competes  for
hydrogen bond acceptor sites and simultaneously reduces hydrophobic
associations, thereby enhancing compatibility of pectin with water (Lu
et al., 2022; Sun & Arntfield, 2012).

Solubility  assays  (Fig.  7A)  revealed  that  both  urea  and  Gu-HCl
treatments  increased  pectin  solubility  across  all  PpH  levels,  with  Gu-
HCl showing slightly greater efficacy. The consistent improvement in-
dicates  that  the  solubility  decline  at  high  PpH  is  primarily  driven  by
intramolecular hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions, which
promote irreversible aggregation during thermal drying. The partial and
pH-dependent effects of NaCl further support that electrostatic forces are
secondary contributors in this system, in agreement with zeta potential
findings.

flected  by  changes  in  I1/I3  and  CAC.  In  the  uniform  concentration
experiment,  pyrene  fluorescence  spectra  were  recorded  for  pectin  so-
lutions prepared at different PpH values and normalized (Fig. 8A), while
the corresponding I1/I3 ratios were plotted as a function of PpH (Fig. 8B).
The  I1/I3  ratio  displayed  a  “decrease–increase”  trend  with  increasing
PpH,  reaching  its  minimum  near  PpH  = 4.  This  indicates  that  as  PpH
increased from 1 to 4, intermolecular hydrophobic interactions among
pectin chains progressively strengthened, placing pyrene in a less polar
and  more  hydrophobic  environment  (Li  et  al.,  2025).  This  result  is
consistent with the previously identified solubility drop and structural
densification threshold at PpH  = 3–4. At higher PpH  (6–7), I1/I3  values
exhibited a slight rebound, but still remained lower than those at PpH =
1–2.  Considering  the  concurrent  decreases  in  solubility,  increases  in
molecular  weight,  and  higher  packing  density,  this  rebound  does  not
reflect weakened hydrophobicity. Rather, it suggests that aggregation
became more compact and coarse, thereby limiting probe access to in-
ternal hydrophobic sites and causing the apparent increase in I1/I3.

In  the  concentration-gradient  experiment  (Fig.  8C),  the  PpH  = 1
sample  showed  a  slight  increase  in  I1/I3  values  within  the  range  of
0.05–0.10  mg/mL,  followed  by  a  decline  beyond  0.10  mg/mL.  This
suggests that pectin molecules at this PpH gradually assembled through
intermolecular  hydrophobic  groups,  forming  aggregates  with  internal
hydrophobic  microdomains,  with  a  critical  aggregation  concentration
(CAC) of approximately 0.11 mg/mL. By contrast, the PpH  = 7 sample
exhibited a monotonic decline in I1/I3  values with increasing concen-
tration (from 1.84 to 1.38), implying that hydrophobic microdomains
were already  present at very  low concentrations, with  pyrene readily
accessing  these  regions.  Its  CAC  was  therefore  estimated  to  be  below
0.05  mg/mL.  These  findings  align  with  previous  reports  that  pectin
forms  hydrophobic  regions  through  intermolecular  forces,  promoting
the  migration  of  pyrene  molecules  into  these  domains  and  thereby
reducing the I1/I3 ratio (Cheng et al., 2018; Pan et al., 2025).

Overall, pyrene fluorescence results demonstrate that precipitation
pH  regulates  intermolecular  non-covalent  interactions  in  pectin,  with

hydrophobic forces being central to solubility and aggregation. Acidic
precipitation  produced  loose  structures  with  few  hydrophobic  micro-
domains, whereas near-neutral conditions favored compact aggregates
with stronger hydrophobic interactions and reduced solubility.

4. Conclusions

In  this  study,  we  demonstrated  that  PpH  critically  modulates  the
thermal  aggregation  behavior  of  RGI-rich  pectin.  Although  some
primary-structure-related indicators (e.g., monosaccharide composition
and  DM)  showed  statistically  significant  variations  among  PpH  treat-
ments  (Table  1 and  Fig.  2),  the  major  PpH-dependent  changes  were
manifested as a pronounced reduction in solubility at PpH  ≥ 4, accom-
panied by an increase in apparent molecular weight, a decrease in mo-
lecular dimensions, and a denser internal morphology, suggesting that
aggregation  rather  than  backbone  alteration  dominated  the  observed
behavior. Mechanistically, pectin aggregation at high PpH is driven by a
synergistic  combination  of  enhanced  hydrophobic  interactions  and
intensified hydrogen bonding. Cationic shielding reduces electrostatic
repulsion, allowing closer association of pectin chains. At the same time,
the increased exposure of hydrophobic regions and the strengthening of
hydrogen bonding further facilitate aggregation. These findings provide
