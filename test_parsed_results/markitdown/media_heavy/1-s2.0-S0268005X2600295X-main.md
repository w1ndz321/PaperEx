1. Introduction

Whey  protein  (WP),  rich  in  branched-chain  essential  amino  acids,
consists  primarily  of  β-lactoglobulin  (~65%),  α-lactalbumin  (~25%),
and  bovine  serum  albumin  (~8%).  As  a  by-product  of  cheese
manufacturing, WP is available as concentrate (WPC), isolate (WPI), and
hydrolysate (WPH) forms, making it a versatile biopolymer (Tang et al.,
2024; Triani & Foegeding, 2019; Zhu, 2024). These proteins are widely
incorporated into pharmaceutical and food products, including nutra-
ceuticals,  dietary  supplements,  beverages,  baked  goods,  and  dairy
(Setiowati et al., 2020; Wagner et al., 2020), due to their multiple health
benefits such as muscle maintenance, blood sugar control, inflammation

reduction, and heart health (Kadam et al., 2018; Madureira et al., 2007;
Zhu,  2024),  along  with  its  excellent  gelling,  emulsifying,  and
film-forming properties (Eissa et al., 2004; Eissa &  Khan, 2005; Setio-
wati et al., 2020).

Several factors, including pH, temperature, ionic strength and con-
centration  influence  WP  denaturation  and  gelation,  altering  the  solu-
bility, rheology, texture, and functionality (Giroux et al., 2020; Kessler &
Beyer, 1991; Vardhanabhuti & Foegeding, 1999). Moreover, the struc-
tural and functional behavior of WP is highly dependent on processing
conditions.  While  the  basic  mechanisms  of  thermal  denaturation  and
aggregation are well established (Donovan & Mulvihill, 1987; Tanford,
1968; Wagner et al., 2020), specific heating profiles and environmental

design (Behrouzain et al., 2020).

Galactomannans,  a  group  of  polysaccharides  made  up  of  a  linear
chain of β-1,4-linked mannopyranosyl mannose units with branches of
α-1,6-linked galactopyranosyl galactose units, are used extensively with
WP.  While  all  galactomannans  are  similar  in  their  base  architecture,
they vary in their galactose-to-mannose (G/M) ratios. Guar gum (GG),
derived  from  guar  beans  (Cyamopsis  tetragonoloba),  is  a  natural  gal-
actomannan  polysaccharide  with  a  G/M  ratio  of  ~0.5  (Mahammad
et al., 2007). It is widely used as food thickener, stabilizer, and edible
coating  due  to  its  non-ionic  and  environmentally  friendly  properties
(Salehi et al., 2024; Tayal & Khan, 2000; Tripathi et al., 2024). Locust
bean gum (LBG) obtained from the seed endosperm of the carob tree
(Ceratonia siliqua) has a typical G/M ratio of 0.25. The high mannose
content  and  low  galactose  content  distinguish  LBG  from  other
commercially  available  galactomannans,  including  GG  (Khoobbakht
et al., 2024; Prajapati et al., 2013; Sana et al., 2024). LBG and GG are
suitable  for  a  wide  range  of  applications  due  to  their  biodegradable
nature and versatility in modifying product  properties (Copetti et  al.,
1997; Silva et al., 2016).

While  several  studies  have  examined  interactions  between  whey
proteins and polysaccharides such as pectin (Beaulieu et al., 2001; Lutz
et al., 2009; Raei et al., 2018), arabic gum (Klein et al., 2010; Weinbreck
et al., 2003, 2004), xanthan gum (Bertrand & Turgeon, 2007; Bryant &
McClements,  2000;  Laneuville  et  al.,  2000),  carrageenan  (Lizarraga
et al., 2006; Mleko et al., 1997; Weinbreck et al., 2004), and tara gum
(Hern´andez  Hern´andez  et  al.,  2020;  Huamaní-Mel´endez  et  al.,  2021;
Sittikijyothin  et  al.,  2007),  the  most  prevalent  interest  remains  on
protein-polysaccharide  interactions  involving  WPI  + GG  and  WPI  +
LBG,  owing  to  their  widespread  use.  However,  the  existing  literature
presents conflicting findings regarding the macroscopic impact of these
galactomannans, particularly concerning concentration thresholds. For

concentrations under realistic processing conditions (i.e., simultaneous
mechanical  shear  and  thermal  treatment).  By  deliberately  selecting
commercially  relevant  formulation  ratios,  this  work  aims  to  resolve
these literature discrepancies and provide actionable, predictive insights
directly applicable to industrial food design.

In particular, the synergistic behavior of the binary mixtures under
simultaneous  mechanical  shear  and  thermal  treatment—conditions
commonly  encountered  in  industrial  processing—remains  largely  un-
explored. An understanding of the relationship between microstructural
organization  and  macroscopic  rheological  behavior  in  these  mixed
biopolymers also needs further probing. This study addresses these gaps
by  investigating  the  interaction  mechanisms  in  two  separate  binary
systems—whey  protein  isolate  (WPI)-guar  gum  (GG)  and  WPI-locust
bean  gum  (LBG)  and  comparing  them  to  a  control  whey  protein
isolate  (WPI)  system.  By  focusing  on  shear-induced  thermal  denatur-
ation, in which denaturation time, shear-rate during denaturation and
temperature cycling are parameterized, we examine the way each gal-
actomannan  independently  modulates  protein  microstructure  and  the
resulting rheological properties.

The novelty of this work lies in coupling rheological measurements
with confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) and isothermal titra-
tion calorimetry (ITC). Specifically, CLSM combined with fast Fourier
transform  (FFT)  analysis  correlates  rheological  responses  with  micro-
structural phase behavior, while ITC provides thermodynamic insights
into the underlying interaction mechanisms. Collectively, these findings
advance the fundamental  understanding of protein-polysaccharide in-
teractions  under  shear  and  provide  practical  guidance  for  industries
seeking to tailor the formulation and processing of WPI-based products.

2. Materials and methods

2.1. Materials

Whey  protein  isolate  (WPI)  (Ultra  9100;  97  wt%  protein  content)
was obtained from Grande Custom Ingredients Group (Fond du Lac, WI,
USA). Guar gum (GG) (88.58 wt% polysaccharide content) and locust

were of analytical grade and used as received unless otherwise specified.

Quantitative assessment of the protein network microstructure was
performed using ImageJ software (NIH, USA) (Cordoba & Daly, 2019).
Initially, a confocal image of the whey protein samples were processed
by applying a grayscale threshold, which separated the protein features
from the background based on pixel intensity. This conversion enhanced
contrast  and  segmented  the  image  into  distinct  regions,  making  the
underlying  microstructural  patterns  easier  to  identify  and  analyze
(Broeke et al., 2015, Shihan et al., 2021). Following thresholding, im-
ages were processed using Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to convert them
into the frequency domain. The mid-range frequencies were isolated by
selecting inner and outer circle diameters of 10 and 300 pixels, respec-
tively,  which  removed  low-frequency  background  intensity  and
high-frequency noise while preserving structural information (Fig. S2).
An  inverse  FFT  was  then  applied  to  return  the  image  to  the  spatial
domain.  This  approach  emphasized  the  frequency  components  most
relevant to the protein's structural features, providing insight into the
periodicity and arrangement of the protein aggregates (Cordoba & Daly,
2019;  Ockleford  et  al.,  2002).  In  parallel,  particle  analysis  was  con-
ducted to quantitatively assess the protein network. For this, the open
binary function was applied to smooth object boundaries and eliminate
isolated  pixels,  after  which  the  ImageJ  particle  analysis  tool  was
employed to characterize and compare the microstructural features in
greater detail (Shihan et al., 2021).

3.2. Effect of gelation time on WPI, WPI + GG, and WPI + LBG systems

The  final  properties  of  protein-polysaccharide  systems  denatured
depend  on several factors, including the  temperature and  duration of

mains. This was reflected in the pronounced shear-dependent rise in Gʹ.
Overall, while shear governs the structural trend across all systems, the
presence of polysaccharides amplifies biphasic reinforcement, through
viscosity enhancement in GG or interfacial bridging in LBG, ultimately
dictating the final viscoelastic properties.

3.5. Interaction mechanism between WPI and GG/LBG

The interaction between WPI, GG, and LBG was investigated using
isothermal  titration  calorimetry  (ITC).  The  ITC  setup  consisted  of  an
adiabatic chamber with a sample cell containing WPI and a reference
cell  filled  with  acetate  buffer,  both  maintained  at  pH  5.  Incremental
additions of GG or LBG (also at pH 5) were introduced into the sample
cell, and the resulting heat changes from each injection were recorded as
thermograms.  Fig.  8a  presents  the  raw  heat  peaks  for  all  titrations,
including GG and LBG into WPI, buffer into WPI, and GG and LBG into
buffer.  The  thermograms  show  that  the  heat  of  dilution  for  gums  in
buffer was significantly smaller than the heat changes observed in the
other titrations. The total heat change for each titration was obtained by
integrating the heat peaks and calculating the enthalpy change per mole
of titrant, as shown in Fig. 8b and c. To isolate the binding interactions,
the heat of dilution (from buffer into WPI and gums into buffer) was
subtracted from the total interaction heat, as described in Equation (1).
In Fig. 8b and c, the term GG/LBG-WPIinteraction  refers to the enthalpy
change prior to correction for dilution effects, whereas GG/LBG-WPI-
binding represents the enthalpy change attributable solely to gum-protein
binding, after accounting for dilution.

The results in Fig. 8b and c shows that the heat of binding between
WPI and either GG or LBG is negligible, with enthalpy changes close to
zero. This indicates the absence of significant binding interactions be-
tween WPI and the gums. The exothermic heat observed in the system
instead arises primarily from buffer addition to WPI or gum addition to
WPI,  with  both  processes  producing  nearly  identical  values.  These
findings  suggest  that  during  titration,  denatured  WPI  undergoes  self-
association,  regardless  of  whether  gums  or  buffer  are  introduced.  A
similar phenomenon has been reported for β-glucan titrated into casein
protein (Sarantis et al., 2021). Accordingly, ITC measurements provide
direct  evidence  that WPI  at  its  isoelectric  pH does  not associate  with
neutral polysaccharides such as GG and LBG. This lack of association
reflects thermodynamic incompatibility, which drives phase separation
(Hadian et al., 2016; Turgeon & Laneuville, 2009). In such systems, two
non-interacting  macromolecules  segregate  into  distinct  immiscible
aqueous phases, one enriched in protein and the other in polysaccharide.
These  results  are  consistent  with  the  previously  discussed  interaction
mechanisms of WPI with GG and LBG, reinforcing our interpretation of
their behavior under these conditions.

4. Conclusions

This study investigated the behavior of whey protein isolate (WPI)
when combined with guar gum (GG) or locust bean gum (LBG) under
varying  thermal  denaturation  time  while  simultaneously  exposed  to
different shear rates. Material properties of these binary systems were
probed  at  three  different  length  scales,  macro,  micro  and  molecular

levels using rheology, confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) and
isothermal  titration  calorimetry  (ITC),  respectively.  Of  the  three
different denaturation times (10, 15 and 20 min) explored at 80oC, 15
min was found to maximize gel strength. Different shear rates (2, 10 and
(cid:0) 1) applied for this time duration revealed that while both GG and
20 s
LBG enhanced the rheological performance of WPI, they did so through
distinct  mechanisms.  LBG  promoted  shear-dependent  gel  modulus,

yielding bi-continuous, phase-separated structures, whereas GG resulted
in  formation  of  homogeneous,  elastic  networks  with  higher  elastic
moduli across all shear conditions. CLSM confirmed these contrasting
microstructural features, underscoring the complementary roles of GG
and LBG in tailoring WPI-based gels for enhanced functionality.

Interestingly, the gelation mechanism of WPI remained unchanged
in  the  presence  of  either  GG  or  LBG,  as  observed  from  temperature-
dependent  measurements  of  the  elastic  (G′)  modulus.  Such  a  result
was consistent with both gums exhibiting negligible (i.e., same) heat of
binding with WPI as revealed by ITC experiments. Reheating, however,
led to irreversible structural modification possibly through protein ag-
gregation  and  rearrangement,  underscoring  the  impact  of  thermal
cycling  on  gel  stability  and  functionality.  Collectively,  these  results
elucidate how galactomannan type governs the microstructural evolu-
tion  of  WPI  gels  under  concurrent  temperature  and  shear  variations;
conditions that are relevant in industrial processing, thereby providing
valuable  insights  for  designing  food  formulations  with  tailored
properties.
