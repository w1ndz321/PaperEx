Background: In recent years, tissue engineering has experienced rapid development, with bioscaffolds emerging
as  a  focal  point  of  research  due  to  their  favorable  bioactivity,  biocompatibility,  and  capacity  to  provide  me-
chanical  support  for  cellular  growth.  The  bioscaffolds  have  great  potential  in  tissue  regeneration.  However,
conventional natural scaffolds and polymer scaffolds pose risks of immunogenicity, while also face challenges in
mimicking the in vivo microenvironment and the biochemical and mechanical properties of natural organs/tis-
sues,  which  collectively  limit  their  repair  capability.  The  development  of  decellularized  extracellular  matrix
(dECM)  technology  offers  a  viable  solution  to  these  challenges,  demonstrating  considerable  potential  for
advancing organ and tissue regeneration.
Technology: This reviews the classification of dECM, outlines various current methods for its preparation, and
comprehensively examines its latest advances in tissue repair and regenerative medicine, including applications
in skin, bone, nerve, heart, lung, liver, and kidney tissues.
Results: This  review  systematically  examines  recent  advances  in  dECM  production  and  regenerative  medicine
applications.  We  classify dECM subtypes,  detail contemporary  decellularization  protocols,  and highlight  their
biomedical  utility.  Superior  biocompatibility  substantially  mitigates  post-transplant  immune  rejection  risk,
underscoring strong clinical translation potential for tissue engineering.

2.2. Scaffolds derived from plants

Currently, dECM derived from animal tissues and organs remain the
most widely used in tissue engineering [46,47], while plant-based dECM
are  emerging  as  promising  alternative  [48].  Plant  cells  are  mainly
composed of cell walls and protoplasts [49], with the plant cell wall play
an equivalent role in maintaining tissue morphology and contributing to
the formation of plant organs [50,51]. The topological similarities be-
tween plant cell walls and the ECM of animal tissues are striking, with
their naturally layered tubular network systems effectively mimicking
vascular networks. This creates an ideal three-dimensional environment
for transporting nutrients and enabling cell migration. As a long-chain
polysaccharide,  cellulose  forms  the  fundamental  fibrous  framework
that imparts mechanical strength and rigidity to plant tissues [52,53].
dECM derived from natural cellulose have been positively demonstrated
to  support  in  vitro  growth  of  mammalian  somatic  cells  for  up  to  12
weeks. Notably, the vast structural diversity across plant species allows
plant-derived scaffolds to recapitulate a wide range of tissue architec-
tures, thereby enabling the mimicry of different human organs [54].

Plant-derived dECM have the advantage of being suitable alternative
biomaterials. In addition to mimicking the structural features of animal
tissues  and  organs,  plant-derived  dECM  offer  several  advantages,
including  superior  biocompatibility,  lower  production  costs  [55],  For
example, Ju et al. developed a decellularized apple scaffold by preparing
decellularized  apple  scaffolds.  Then  these  scaffolds  were  used  as  a
porous cellulose skeleton for 3D culture of mammalian cells and were

applied  to  the  culture  of  human  mesenchymal  stromal  cells  (MSCs)
(Fig.  3a)  [56].  Fischer  et  al.  developed  a  cochlear  tree  decellularized
scaffold, and used human dental pulp stem cells (hDPSCs) for the scaf-
fold's  re-cellularization  (Fig.  3b)  [57].  The  scaffold  demonstrated
excellent  mechanical  properties  and  degradability,  presenting  great
potential  in  the  field  of  regenerative  dentistry  [58].  Gaudette  et  al.
prepared  a  dECM  from  parsley.  Due  to  the  preservation  of  its  innate
vascular system, these scaffolds exhibit inherent permeability, providing
a cost-effective, biochemically rich, and sustainable scaffold material for
tissue engineering applications (Fig. 3c) [59]. Feinberg et al. developed
a  plant-derived  scaffold  for  regenerative  dentistry  by  decellularizing
parsnip tissue (Fig. 3d) [60]. Additionally, the outer white portion of
shallots  were  found  to  possess  aligned  microstructures  capable  of
guiding the differentiation of C2C12 cells into aligned myotubes [61].

2.3. Scaffolds derived from cells

Cell-derived  dECM  have  certain  advantages  in  terms  of  structural
and compositional diversity [62,63]. This type of scaffold is cultured in
vitro, and different species of cells can secrete the corresponding specific
ECM [64]. When a large amount of ECM is secreted, it can be harvested
and processed through decellularization techniques to remove cellular
components while preserving the structural and biochemical integrity of
the ECM process [65]. The resulting cell-derived natural ECM scaffolds
closely  mimic  the  native  3D  microenvironment,  providing  a  highly
biomimetic  platform  for  the  subsequent  recellularization  [66].  These
scaffolds  contain  key  skeletal  proteins  such  as  elastin,  laminin,  and
glycosaminoglycans  (GAGs),  which  not  only  maintain  cellular  archi-
tecture  but  also  support  cell  growth  and  differentiation,  thereby
enhancing the scaffold's functionality in tissue engineering applications.
Natural ECM also contains many bioactive molecules, such as growth
factors,  matrix  metalloproteinases  (MMP),  and  metalloproteinase  in-
hibitors (TIMP). These active biomolecules can promote cell adhesion as
well as proliferation, and also promote the secretion of new EC [67].

3. Preparation method of dECM

dECM usually involve decellularization of entire organs and tissues,
whereby all cells as well as immunogenic substances are removed from
the  organ  or  tissue  by  physical,  chemical,  and  biological  methods,
leaving only the intact ECM structure [76]. Nowadays, there is no fixed
treatment protocol for tissue and organ decellularization process, and
the decellularization protocol is largely related to the specificity of the
tissue or organ, such as the species, the age, and the type and size of the
organ  [77].  These  retained  ECM  components  are  necessary  for  cell
reattachment, growth, proliferation, and differentiation on the scaffold.
Therefore, the researchers involved have developed physical, chemical,
biological, and a combination of all three approaches.

3.1. Physical methods

Physical  methods  of  decellularization  are  designed  to  disrupt  cell
membranes and remove cell contents, such as ultrasound, shock, elec-
troshock,  supercritical  CO2,  and  freeze-thaw  cycles.  In  recent  years,
many studies have utilized physical methods as an adjunct to enhance
the efficiency of decellularization protocols, demonstrating their feasi-
bility and effectiveness in preserving ECM integrity.

3.1.2. Supercritical CO2  method

Supercritical CO2 is used to remove cells from tissues by utilizing the
special  physicochemical  properties  of  supercritical  CO2.  Supercritical
CO2 refers to CO2 maintained at high temperature and pressure, where it
has both liquid-like and gas-like properties. This unique state enables it
to  penetrate  solid  materials  without  solvent  contamination  [78].  By
applying heat and pressure, the CO2 gas could penetrate into organs or
tissues.  During  the  decellularization  process,  the  CO2  gas  does  not
remain in the organ or tissue, and does not introduce toxic chemicals or
solvent contamination. Moreover, the CO2 gas itself is non-toxic. Under
the  action  of  supercritical  CO2,  the  cells  are  removed  and  the  ECM
structure is preserved. Nowadays, supercritical CO2  has been success-
fully applied to decellularize human adipose tissue, rat heart, porcine
cornea,  porcine  pericardium and  other tissues, becoming  a  promising
strategy for decellularization.

3.1.3. Perfusion

When  larger  tissues  or  organs  need  decellularization,  perfusion
method is often used to remove cells from tissues and preserve the ECM
and  scaffolding  structure  of  tissues.  This  approach  involves  inserting

cannulas into both ends of an organ to establish a perfusion pathway
through  its  native  vascular  network.  Decellularization  solutions  or
enzymatic  agents  are  then  circulated  via  this  pathway  to  effectively
remove cellular components while preserving the ECM and native tissue
architecture. This method is particularly advantageous for the decellu-
larization of large and complex tissues, as it ensures efficient, uniform
cell removal and maximal retention of the organ's 3D structural integ-
rity.  However,  the  efficiency  of  perfusion-based  decellularization  is
mainly  determined  by  the  relevant  parameters.  For  example,  factors
such as pressure, temperature, and flow rate of the fluid during perfusion
can determine  the rate of cellular removal. In addition, the choice of
perfusion route (the organ's artery or portal vein) and the direction of
fluid  flow (forward flow or reverse flow) can also impact  decellulari-
zation  outcomes.  Optimization  of  these  variables  is  essential  for
achieving  consistent  and  reproducible  decellularization  while  main-
taining the mechanical and biochemical properties of the ECM.

This method allows the decellularized fluid to enter the interior of
the  organ  quickly  and  uniformly,  thus  removing  cellular  components
from the organ by dissolving cell membranes and cellular components
For example, Verstegen et al. used continuous perfusion to decellularize
a  pig  liver  (Fig.  5a)  [79].  After  the  decellularization  of  pig  liver  by
perfusion,  the  liquid  established  a  circulation  in  the  organ,  and  the
cellular components were taken away by the perfusion liquid. And the
whole process of perfusion didn't destroy the 3D structure of the organ,
the scaffolding structure and extracellular matrix were preserved rela-
tively intact, which provided a good inoculation platform for subsequent
recellularization. Wan et al. used the perfusion method to decellularize
the organ with Triton X (Fig. 5b) [80]. And the complete decellulari-
zation was achieved in 16 h. The perfusion method has a great advan-
tage  over  static  immersion  by  enabling  uniform  distribution  of  the
decellularization  solution  throughout  the  entire organ.  This  approach
facilitates more rapid and consistent penetration of the solution into the
internal regions of the tissue, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency
and  effectiveness  of  the  decellularization  process.  In  addition,  small
tissues  can  also  be  decellularized  efficiently  by  perfusion.  Yang  et  al.
prepared adipose dECM with vascular tips by perfusing adipose tissues
for 24 h in a specific bioreactor, and a transparent, low-immunogenic
adipose  decellularized  extracellular  matrix  was  obtained.  Wang  et  al.
prepared a decellularized extracellular matrix of porcine liver through

extracellular matrix is usually used in the treatment of large organs. It
has been shown that the perfusion method is usually used for decellu-
larization of large organs in order to achieve the optimal efficiency of
decellularization.

3.2. Chemical methods

Chemical  decellularization  refers  to  the  destruction  of  cellular
structures through a series of chemical agents, including: hypertonic and
hypotonic solutions, acidic and alkaline solutions, organic solvents, and
eluents.

3.2.1. Eluting agent decellularization

Eluents are typically categorized as either ionic or non-ionic based on
their chemical properties. A common non-ionic eluent is Triton X-100

compromise  the mechanical  strength of  the scaffolds.  Besides, the  re-

fibronectin in the ECM. The residual SDS exhibits substantial cytotox-

3.2.2. Acid and base

Acid- and base-mediated decellularization is a process that utilizes
acidic or alkaline solutions to remove cellular components by disrupting
ECM adhesion molecules  [84]. Similar to detergent-based  decellulari-
zation,  these  chemical  treatments  can  compromise  dECM  integrity.
Acidic solutions, induce decellularization by altering extracellular pH,

thereby  destabilizing  cellular  membranes  and  membrane-associated
proteins  [85,86].  The  acidic  environment  disrupts  cell-matrix  in-
teractions, including integrin- and calreticulin-mediated adhesions, ul-
timately  leading  to  cell  detachment  from  the  dECM  [87].  Alkaline
solutions, including sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and other strong bases,
modify  the  pH-dependent  binding  between  cell  membranes  and  the
ECM. The alkaline conditions interfere with electrostatic interactions on
the cell surface, promoting cell dissociation. In a comparative study by
Fathi et al., NaOH and SDS were evaluated for ovarian tissue decellu-
larization,  respectively  [88].  Histological  and  quantitative  analyses
demonstrated  that  NaOH  treatment  more  effectively  preserved  tissue
architecture and supported post-recellularization follicular reconstruc-
tion  compared  to  SDS.  Despite  their  efficacy,  excessive  acid  or  base
exposure can induce cytotoxic effects, including cellular apoptosis and
functional  impairment.  Moreover,  prolonged  treatment  or  elevated
concentrations  may  also  degrade  ECM  components,  particularly

diminished growth factor bioactivity [89]. Consequently, precise opti-

3.2.3. Hypertonic and hypotonic solutions

Osmotic decellularization utilizing hypertonic or hypotonic solutions
mediates  cellular  removal  through  transmembrane  osmotic  pressure
differentials, inducing successive cycles of cell swelling, shrinkage, and
eventual membrane rupture [90]. Mechanistic studies reveal that: hy-
pertonic solutions elicit cellular dehydration and volumetric reduction
while simultaneously facilitating cytoplasmic protein extraction; while
hypotonic  treatment  promotes  nuclear  membrane  destabilization  and
genomic DNA efflux through osmotic lysis [91]. Notably, osmotic ap-
proaches  are  predominantly  employed  as  ancillary  components  in
combinatorial decellularization protocols due to their limited efficacy
when used in isolation [92].

3.3. Biological methods

Biological methods mainly separate or detach cells by breaking down
the  intercellular  connective  material  or  ECM  with  enzymes  such  as
trypsin,  nuclease,  lyase,  collagenase.  Different  enzymes  have  specific
degradation effects on different cellular components.

Trypsin  has  emerged  as  the  predominant  enzymatic  agent  for
decellularization, functioning through selective proteolysis of cell sur-
face  proteins  [93].  Trypsin-treated  dECM  demonstrate  excellent  cyto-
compatibility  for  subsequent  recellularization  and  are  frequently
combined  with  physical  and  chemical  methods  to  achieve  enhanced
decellularization  efficacy  [94,95].  However,  prolonged  enzymatic
exposure  may  induce  structural  alterations  in  dECM,  potentially
compromising  its  biomechanical  properties  and  overall  integrity.
Therefore,  precise  optimization  of  trypsin  incubation  parameters  is
crucial  to  maintain  the  structural  integrity  of  tissue  engineering  scaf-
folds while ensuring complete cellular removal. Nucleases, particularly
deoxyribonuclease  (DNase)  and  ribonuclease  (RNase),  constitute
another  critical  class  of  decellularization  enzymes.  These  enzymes
mediate complete nucleic acid clearance through phosphodiester bond
hydrolysis in DNA and RNA molecules [96]. Similar to trypsin, nuclease
treatment  requires  careful  temporal  regulation  to  prevent  nonspecific
ECM degradation while ensuring thorough removal of genetic material
[97].

Although  no  universal  standard  protocol  for  cell  decellularization
currently exists, all existing methods aim to thoroughly remove cells and
nuclear  material  to  eliminate  immunogenicity  while  maximizing  the
preservation of the natural extracellular matrix structure and bioactive
components  [98].  Physical  approaches  apply  forces  such  as  pressure,
temperature,  or  mechanical  agitation  to  disrupt  and  dislodge  cells.
Freeze-thaw cycles are commonly employed as an initial pretreatment,
whereas perfusion is typically applied to larger intact organs such as the
heart, liver, or kidney. Although physical methods avoid chemical res-
idues  and  offer  high  biosafety,  the  applied  mechanical  stresses  risk
damaging  the  ECM  ultrastructure  and  often  fail  to  achieve  complete
decellularization when used alone [31].

Chemical  methods  use  specific  reagents  to  dissolve  cellular  mem-
branes, break down nucleic acids and remove lipids. The choice of re-
agent can be adjusted according to the type of tissue. For instance, SDS is
typically used for dense tissues such as the dermis or tendon, whereas
Triton  X-100  is  more  effective  for  softer  tissues  like  adipose  tissue.
Despite  being  highly  efficient  and  capable  of  targeting  particular  cell
components,  this  approach  can  result  in  the  presence  of  residual
chemicals and cause cytotoxicity. It can also damage important extra-
cellular  matrix  elements,  including  GAGs  and  collagens.  Biological
methods, primarily enzymatic treatments, selectively degrade intercel-
lular junctions, cellular contents, and nucleic acids. These are frequently
incorporated  as  final  steps  in  decellularization  protocols  due  to  their

mild processing conditions and minimal ECM disruption [99]. However,
excessive enzymatic treatment can degrade certain ECM proteins, and
enzymes may lose activity under suboptimal conditions [14].

In  summary,  no  single  decellularization  strategy  is  universally
applicable; each approach presents distinct advantages and limitations.
The selection of an appropriate method depends on tissue characteristics
such as type, density, and intended application. Optimized decellulari-
zation  protocols  typically  integrate  physical,  chemical,  and  biological
techniques in a complementary manner to maximize cell removal while
preserving ECM integrity and bioactivity.

3.4. Combined methods

Current  decellularization  approaches  employ  physical,  chemical,
and biological methods, each with distinct advantages and limitations
[100–102].  While  these  techniques  can  effectively  remove  cellular
components,  they  invariably  cause  varying  degrees  of  ECM  damage.
Single-method strategies often prove insufficient for complete decellu-
larization, necessitating combined protocols for optimal results [103].
Modern decellularization strategies typically combine physical, chemi-
cal and  biological methods. For example, dECM that are only treated
with Triton X-100 and do not undergo freeze-thaw cycles retain up to
40 % residual DNA. It is notable that physical processing followed by
mild non-ionic surfactant treatment significantly reduces the amount of
residual cellular components and genetic material in the dECM [104].
Physical  methods  such  as  freeze-thaw  cycles  or  perfusion  utilize  me-
chanical forces to disrupt cell membranes and increase matrix porosity,
facilitating the efflux of cellular contents and significantly enhancing the
subsequent  penetration  of  chemical  agents  into  the  tissue.  Chemical
approaches, including the use of detergents like SDS or Triton X-100,
build upon this physical pretreatment by selectively solubilizing lipids
and membrane proteins, thereby efficiently removing cellular material
from  tissues  or  organs.  While  these  chemicals  pose  risks  of  residual
toxicity and potential damage to GAGs and collagens, such adverse ef-
fects can be mitigated by optimizing their concentration and exposure
duration. Biological methods, primarily employing nucleases or trypsin,
serve  as  a  final  purification  step  by  specifically  degrading  residual
nucleic  acids  after  chemical  decellularization,  thereby  minimizing
immunogenic remnants. This step benefits from the substrate accessi-
bility  achieved  through  prior  physical  and  chemical  treatments,
ensuring  efficient  clearance  of  genetic  material  while  preserving  the
integrity and bioactivity of the ECM [105]. Combination protocols have
been extensively optimized to effectively remove nuclei and DNA while
minimizing  ECM  damage.  Freeze-thaw  cycling  proves  particularly
valuable by reducing exposure time to chemical and enzymatic reagents,
thereby preserving ECM integrity and limiting toxic residue accumula-
tion. Kim et al. successfully applied this integrated approach for renal
decellularization,  combining  freeze-thaw  cycles,  Triton  X-100,  and
nuclease  treatment  to  produce  low-immunogenicity  renal  dECM  with
minimal cellular residue [106]. Despite these advances, tissue-specific
standardization  remains  challenging.  Optimal  decellularization  pro-
tocols must be empirically determined for different organs and tissues.
The ultimate goal is to produce intact dECM that support cell adhesion,
proliferation, and differentiation while meeting the structural demands
of complex organs.

4.2. Bone diseases

Bone, the primary structural component of the vertebrate musculo-
skeletal  system,  serves  critical  mechanical  and  protective  functions
through  its  unique  mineralized  ECM  [117,118].  In  contrast,  skeletal
muscle  possesses  intrinsic  regenerative  capacity,  sufficient  to  repair
minor injuries but inadequate in cases of volumetric muscle loss (VML).
VML defined as traumatic defects exceeding 20 % of total muscle mass,
represents a clinically intractable condition characterized by irreversible
loss of regenerative potential [119,120].

Typically resulting from high-energy trauma, oncologic resection, or
degenerative  pathologies,  VML  induces  profound  fibrotic  remodeling,
permanent functional impairment and significantly diminished quality
of life [121]. In certain cases, these defects also necessitate adjunctive
interventions  for  bone  regeneration  [122].  Current  therapeutic  para-
digms,  particularly  autologous  muscle  transplantation,  remain  subop-
timal due to donor site morbidity and inadequate functional recovery
[123].  Consequently,  there  is  an  urgent  clinical  need  for  alternative
strategies. Tissue engineering has emerged as a leveraging biomimetic
scaffolds, cell-based therapies, and bioactive factor delivery systems to
overcome  the  limitations  of  conventional  treatments  and  promote
functional muscle regeneration.

dECM  have  emerged  as  particularly  promising  platforms  in  tissue
engineering due to their unique capacity to recapitulate the native tissue
microenvironment. These scaffolds not only provide structural support
for cellular regrowth, proliferation, and differentiation but also retain
critical bioactive components essential for osteogenesis, including key
growth factors and trace elements that collectively orchestrate bone cell
development and function [124]. dECM represents a naturally derived
reservoir  enriched  with  osteogenic  bioactive  components,  wherein
constituents such as GAGs and collagens facilitate cellular proliferation
and  differentiation  [125].  Bone-specific  dECM  acts  as  a  reservoir  for

dECM  neutralizes  the  localized  acidic  environment  caused  by  PLGA
degradation,  reducing  chemical  irritation  to  macrophages  [147].  As
previously  described,  dECM  derived  from  natural  tissues  contains
bioactive signaling molecules capable of guiding macrophages toward
an anti-inflammatory phenotype. M2 macrophages secrete factors like
IL-10 and TGF-β, promoting angiogenesis and tissue remodeling. This
combination of natural and synthetic materials leverages the advantages
of both, enabling targetedrepair of complex tissue injuries such as SCI
[148,149].

body, orchestrating systemic oxygen and nutrient delivery through its
precisely coordinated contractile activity. This vital pump, composed of
specialized cardiomyocytes, valvular structures, and coronary vascula-
ture [150–152], maintains cellular homeostasis by continuously cycling
between systole and diastole to circulate blood through pulmonary and
systemic  circuits  [153].  Its  critical  role  extends  beyond  perfusion  to
include  thermoregulation  and  metabolic  balance,  as  the  circulatory
system distributes thermal energy throughout the body. Cardiovascular
diseases (CVDs), particularly myocardial infarction, represent the lead-

4.5. Lung diseases

Among  respiratory  diseases  causing  death  worldwide,  chronic
obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is one of the most common and
challenging  condition  [164,165].  While  lung  transplantation  remains
the  sole  therapeutic  option  for  end-stage  pulmonary  pathologies,  this
approach  faces  significant  limitations  including  critical  donor  organ
shortages that fail to meet current clinical demand [166]. Furthermore,
transplant  recipients  face  lifelong  risks  of  immune  rejection,  necessi-
tating chronic immunosuppression with its associated complications. In
response  to  these  challenges,  tissue  engineering  has  pioneered  the
development of decellularized lung scaffolds as a potential alternative to
whole-organ  transplantation  [167,168].  These  engineered  constructs
maintain the native 3D architecture and ECM composition of pulmonary
tissue, thereby preserving essential biomechanical properties including
appropriate viscoelasticity and structural integrity [169]. This preser-
vation of native ultrastructure and mechanical characteristics positions
decellularized lung scaffolds as a promising platform for addressing both
the  scarcity  and  immunological  constraints  of  conventional  lung
transplantation.

Decellularized lung scaffolds offer unique advantages for pulmonary
regeneration by preserving not only the organ's intricate 3D architecture
but also its complete vascular network, including the original arterial
and venous systems. This preservation enables the scaffolds to maintain
critical biomechanical properties while supporting revascularization to
achieve functional gas exchange. Heise et al. developed an innovative
hybrid gel system that incorporated lung-derived ECM to better reca-
pitulate  the  native  pulmonary  microenvironment  for  studying  cell-
substrate  interactions  in  disease  models  (Fig.  11a)  [170].  Their  plat-
form significantly advanced the application of decellularized lung ECM
by  enhancing  its  biofidelity  to  human  lung  tissue  while  retaining
essential  bioactive  components.  Complementary  work  by  Moharana
et  al.  systematically  compared  adult  (AECM)  and  neonatal  (NECM)
lung-derived matrices, revealing that neonatal-derived dECM exhibited
superior  regenerative  potential,  particularly  for  COPD  applications
(Fig. 11b and c) [171]. The study findings indicate that key bioactive
substances  in  dECM,  such  as  collagen,  show  no  significant  changes
compared  to  pre-decellularization  levels.  These  results  confirm  that
bioactive substances promoting cell growth remain preserved in dECM,
ensuring  its  long-term  therapeutic  efficacy  [11].  These  engineered
scaffolds  successfully  mimic  the  natural  structure  of  the  alveoli  and
airways. This guides the alignment and growth of cells, which is critical
for  restoring  the  capacity  for  gas  exchange.  Regarding  safety,  dECM
offers  an  ideal  alternative  for  lung  tissue  engineering.  By  removing
cellular  components  and  substances  that  trigger  immune  rejection,  it
demonstrates  excellent  biocompatibility  and  low  immunogenicity.
dECM  also  mimics  the  biochemical  composition  of  lung  tissue,  pro-
moting the growth of therapeutic cells. Furthermore, the collagen pre-
served in dECM, which comprises around 60 % of pulmonary ECM, is
essential  for  mechanical  strength  and  cell  adhesion.  This  ensures  the
long-term functionality of pulmonary dECM within the lung [172]. In

summary,  as  a  dynamic  microsystem,  dECM  not  only  provides  the
physical  scaffold  required  for  lung  tissue  regeneration  but,  more
importantly, recreates key microenvironmental signals involved in lung
development  and  repair.  This  establishes  the  material  foundation  for
ultimately  achieving  functional  lung  regeneration.  This  characteristic
positions dECM as a critical bridge connecting existing therapeutic ap-
proaches to lung regeneration [173].

4.6. Liver regeneration

The liver, serving as the body's primary metabolic organ, performs
essential  physiological  functions  including  xenobiotic  detoxification,
bile synthesis, glycogen storage, and plasma protein production [174,
175]. End-stage liver failure represents a critical medical condition with
profound  mortality,  demonstrating  acute  fatality  rates  of  60–70  %
despite current therapeutic interventions [176,177]. While orthotopic
liver  transplantation  (OLT)  remains  the  sole  definitive  treatment,  the
severe shortage of donor organs results in significant waitlist mortality,
driving the development of liver tissue engineering as a viable thera-
peutic alternative [178]. dECM have emerged as particularly promising
platforms for hepatic regeneration by preserving the organ's intricate 3D
architecture, including its characteristic vascular networks (portal vein,
hepatic artery, and hepatic vein) and biliary structures [179,180]. These
scaffolds maintain liver-specific ECM components and bioactive factors
that collectively support hepatocyte repopulation, vascularization, and
functional restoration [181]. Tao et al. advanced this field through the
development  of  a  multifunctional  biocomposite  system,  combining
thrombin-fibrinogen-dECM  hydrogels  with  3D-printed  hyaluronic
acid/gelatin/sodium  alginate  scaffolds  incorporating  N-acetylcysteine
(NAC)-functionalized  gold  nanoclusters,  demonstrating  enhanced
regenerative capacity through synergistic integration of structural and
biochemical cues (Fig. 12a) [182].

In addition, Tao et al. developed 3D-printed artificial liver lobules
using  MSCs  spheroids  and  their  potent  extracellular  vesicle (EV)  pro-
tective effects. A decellularized porcine liver-derived extracellular ma-
trix (PLdECM) hydrogel scaffold exhibits significant anti-inflammatory
effects.  A  hexagonal  bio-inspired  scaffold  was  3D-printed,  and  MSC
spheroids were co-cultured with HUVECs on the scaffold, demonstrating
excellent  hepatocyte  differentiation  capacity.  After  transplanting  this
bio-scaffold into CCl4-induced acute liver failure (ALF) mice, the artifi-
cial  liver  lobule  (ALL) effectively  alleviated  excessive  oxidative  stress
and  inflammatory  storms,  reduced  tissue  necrosis,  and  promoted
cellular regeneration, demonstrating significant clinical translation po-
tential (Fig. 12b) [41].

By  injecting  stem  cells  into  dECM  to  achieve  recellularization,
functional liver tissue grafts were successfully constructed. These grafts
maintain key liver functions in vitro and effectively support hepatocyte
survival and metabolic activity in vivo experiments [174]. In a signifi-
cant  advancement,  Li  et  al.  engineered  a  multifunctional  platform
comprising biomimetic copper oxide nanozymes (Cu NZs)-incorporated
PLGA  nanofibers  (Cu  NZs@PLGA)  combined  with  dECM  hydrogel
(Fig. 12c) [183]. This innovative system demonstrated dual therapeutic
mechanisms:  the  Cu  NZs@PLGA  component  effectively  scavenged
excessive reactive oxygen species (ROS) during early ALF pathogenesis,
while  the  dECM  hydrogel  provided  an  optimal  3D  microenvironment
that enhanced the hepatic functionality of adipose-derived MSCs-gen-
erated hepatocyte-like cells (HLCs) [184]. These collective findings not
only validate dECM-based approaches as clinically viable strategies for
ALF treatment but also highlight their translational potential through
synergistic  integration  of  antioxidative  protection  and  ECM-mediated
tissue remodeling.

5. Conclusion

Among various scaffold types utilized in tissue engineering, dECM
derived  from human  and  animal  tissues  or  organs  have  garnered  sig-
nificant attention. Through physical, chemical, and biological decellu-
larization techniques, cellular components and immunogenic materials
are effectively removed while preserving the native ECM composition
and 3D architecture. These obtained dECM closely mimics the natural
cellular  microenvironment,  providing  essential  biochemical  and  me-
chanical cues that support cell adhesion, proliferation, differentiation,
and ultimately the repair and regeneration of damaged tissues and or-
gans. Currently, dECM platforms have evolved from simple tissue con-
structs to complex whole-organ frameworks, finding broad applications
in  treating  cardiovascular  diseases,  liver  failure,  skin  wounds,  and
chronic  pulmonary  fibrosis,  among  other  conditions.  Their  superior
biocompatibility, low immunogenicity, and preservation of structural-
