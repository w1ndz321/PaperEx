24 Abstract
25 Emulsion gels with tunable textures are promising carriers for hydrophobic food
26 ingredients and have been widely used in the food industry. However, the fabrication
27 of emulsion gels frequently depends on isolated food components, such as
28 polysaccharides and proteins, or on chemically or physically modified derivatives.
29 These methods typically involve labor-intensive extraction, purification, or
30 modification steps, which limit their scalability and practical application. Herein, a
31 straightforward and scalable strategy was developed to produce property-tailored
f
o
32 emulsion gels by ball-milling a mixture of Spirulina, water, and oil. The effects of
o
33 Spirulina biomass content and water-to-oil volume ratio (S) on the microstructure,
r
p
34 stability, and rheological behavior were investigated. Emulsion gels with an S of 0.3
-
35 demonstrated the highest level of stability,e while the biomass content of Spirulina
r
36 exerted an insignificant effect on the properties. During ball milling, the cellular
P
37 structure of Spirulina is disrupted, resulting in the release of phycocyanin and
l
38 polysaccharides. The polysaccaharide-induced depletion effect promotes the enrichment
n
39 of phycocyanin at the oil–water interface, thereby facilitating emulsion gel formation.
r
40 The emulsion gel couuld be used as a bio-ink for 3D printing, and optimal printability
o
41 was achieved at S = 0.3. In addition, the emulsion gels were used for sesamol delivery,
J
42 and their bioaccessibility was almost twofold that of the bulk oil. Moreover, the ball-
43 milling strategy was effective for stabilizing a range of vegetable oils, indicating its
44 broad applicability. Overall, the whole-Spirulina biomass approach provides a
45 straightforward, universal, and scalable route for designing functional emulsion gels for
46 advanced food applications.
47 Keywords: Emulsion gel, 3D printing, Depletion attraction, Sesamol,

50 1. Introduction
51 Oil-in-water emulsion gels (O/W) are soft, solid–like structures in which oil
52 droplets are immobilized either within a three-dimensional crosslinked polymer
53 network (emulsion-filled gels) or within a network formed by aggregated oil droplets
54 themselves (particulate emulsion gels) (Niu, Zhang, Mujumdar, & Li, 2026; Shu,
55 McClements, Luo, Liu, & Ye, 2025). Emulsion gels have attracted considerable
56 attention in the field of food science due to their ability to encapsulate, protect, and
57 control the release of bioactive compounds (Song, Li, Shang, Wang, & Tan, 2023; H.
f
o
58 T. Wang, Y. D. Sun, Y. T. Hou, & M. Q. Tan, 2025). The preparation of emulsion gels,
o
59 however, typically depends on isolated food components, such as polysaccharides or
r
p
60 proteins, or on chemically or physically modified derivatives. These approaches often
-
61 require labor-intensive extraction, purificatioen, or modification processes, which limit
r
62 their scalability and practical application. Importantly, the growing consumer demand
P
63 for sustainable, clean-label foods has spurred significant interest in emulsion gels made
l
64 from natural ingredients with aminimal processing, underscoring the need to develop
n
65 novel biopolymer gelation strategies.
r
66 Spirulina, a bluue-green microalga (Cyanobacterium), is well known for its high
o
67 nutritional value and sustainability and has been widely used in foods as a dietary
J
68 supplement or whole-food ingredient (Nakamoto, Assis, de Oliveira, & Braga, 2023;
69 Villaro-Cos, Acien, Lafarga, & Sanchez, 2024). Proteins in Spirulina, particularly the
70 water-soluble phycocyanin, have demonstrated strong emulsifying ability and have
71 been extensively employed to stabilize emulsions. On this basis, Spirulina protein-
72 based emulsion gels have been developed for applications such as fat replacement (J.
73 X. Guo, Huang, Gu, & Meng, 2025) and three-dimensional food printing (J. X. Guo,
74 Gu, & Meng, 2024). In addition, emulsion gels constructed from Spirulina protein
75 isolates and carboxymethyl chitosan have been designed for curcumin delivery (Mao,
76 et al., 2025), while Spirulina protein-chitosan complexes have been reported to stabilize
77 Pickering emulsions for astaxanthin encapsulation (R. G. Liu, Li, Zhou, & Tan, 2023).
78 However, these approaches typically require protein isolation, chemical modification,
79 or cross-linking reactions involving non-food-grade reagents, which limit their
3
80 scalability and broader application in food products. Recently, the direct utilization of
81 whole biomass has emerged as a promising strategy for emulsion and emulsion gel
82 preparation, offering a more sustainable and simplified alternative. For example, whole
83 Gracilaria biomass has been directly employed as an emulsifier to fabricate emulsions
84 for astaxanthin delivery and fat replacement (Shao, et al., 2026), highlighting the
85 potential of whole-biomass-based approaches for the development of clean-label and
86 sustainable emulsion gels.
87 Proteins and polysaccharides are the most widely used food-grade biopolymers for
f
o
88 the construction of emulsion gels (Zhi, et al., 2023). Conventional emulsion gel
o
89 preparation procedures involve separately adding proteins or polysaccharides. In
r
p
90 contrast, whole-biomass-based approaches involve the simultaneous release of cellular
-
91 compounds, mainly proteins and polysacceharides, during high-energy processing,
r
92 which enables their direct participation in emulsion gel formation. Proteins function as
P
93 emulsifiers due to their high surface activity. In contrast, polysaccharides typically
l
94 contribute indirectly to the emaulsion gel formation process by increasing continuous-
n
95 phase viscosity or inducing depletion attraction. Recent results have demonstrated that
r
96 cooperative interactuions between proteins and polysaccharides can substantially
o
97 enhance emulsion stability and promote gel network formation (Kim, et al., 2017; Tong,
J
98 et al., 2023), particularly in particulate emulsion gels. In the case of Spirulina, the whole
99 biomass has been employed in the preparation of emulsion gels. Depletion attraction
100 has been proposed as a potential underlying mechanism; however, experimental
101 evidence supporting it remains limited (Feng, et al., 2023a). As a result, the specific
102 mechanisms governing protein–polysaccharide cooperation, particularly when both
103 components originate from a single natural source, remain to be fully elucidated.
104 Ball milling is a straightforward and scalable method for processing various food
105 formulations. The freeze–thaw stability of κ-carrageenan-based emulsion gels could be
106 improved by ball milling (Xu, et al., 2025). It is important to note that by adjusting the
107 ratios of the raw materials, the characteristics of food formulations prepared via ball
108 milling can be easily controlled, indicating that the ball milling method offers a
109 straightforward approach to modifying product properties. The rheological properties
4
110 of oleogels prepared by ball milling can be effectively tuned by regulating the water
111 content of the initial formulation (G.-S. Wang, et al., 2022). Furthermore, the high shear
112 forces generated during ball milling enabled the one-step preparation of emulsion gels
113 from soy protein isolate (SPI) hydrogels, wherein gel formation was regulated by the
114 depletion-induced attraction effect mediated by konjac glucomannan (Z. Yang, et al.,
115 2025). In recent years, the development of 3D-printable emulsion gels for functional
116 ingredient delivery has attracted growing interest (Niu, et al., 2026), creating demand
117 for processing methods that are both scalable and controllable. Compared with other
f
o
118 methods such as high-pressure homogenization or ultrasonication, ball milling offers a
o
119 practical approach that meets these requirements because it can be readily scaled for
r
p
120 larger production volumes. In addition, key processing parameters, such as milling time
-
121 and rotation speed, can be easily adjusted eto control the properties of the resulting
r
122 emulsion gels. Despite these advances, systematic investigation into the preparation of
P
123 emulsion gels via ball milling whole biomass, particularly those that simultaneously
l
124 enable 3D printability and funactional compound delivery, remains limited and merits
n
125 further study.
r
126 In the present sutudy, 3D-printable emulsion gels were developed by subjecting a
o
127 mixture of Spirulina biomass, oil, and water to direct ball milling. A systematic
J
128 investigation was conducted into the effects of the mass fraction and water content of
129 Spirulina on the microstructure, stability, rheological properties, and 3D printing
130 performance of emulsion gels. Furthermore, the formation mechanism of Spirulina-
131 based emulsion gels was examined. To this end, Spirulina polysaccharides and
132 phycocyanin were isolated to elucidate their respective contributions to gel network
133 formation. Sesamol, a naturally occurring antioxidant with a broad spectrum of
134 biological activities, was incorporated into the emulsion gels, and its bioaccessibility
135 was evaluated. This approach, which combines whole-biomass-derived formulation
136 with a scalable processing method, offers new insights into the design of sustainable
137 emulsion gels for functional food applications.

293 3.1 Microstructure of emulsion gels
294 Emulsion gels were prepared by ball milling Spirulina powder with oil and water
295 (Fig. 1A). The visual appearance of the mixtures after ball milling was strongly
296 influenced by the water-oil ratio (S). Formulations with S values between 0.1 and 0.2
297 displayed fluid-like behavior and flowed upon tube inversion (Fig. 1B), indicating the
298 absence of a continuous gel network. Whereas when the S increased to the range of
299 0.3–0.5, the fluid mixture transformed into self-standing emulsfion gels, suggesting a
o
300 self-supporting three-dimensional network was formed (S. Wang, et al., 2020).
o
301 However, a further increase in S to 0.6 led to viscous frlow, which may be attributed to
p
302 excessive dilution of the interfacial stabilizer in the aqueous phase, thereby weakening
-
e
303 the gel network (Feng, et al., 2023b). Optical microscopy revealed no obvious oil
r
304 droplets at S values between 0.1 aPnd 0.2 (Fig. 1C). Furthermore, these samples
305 exhibited fluid-like behavior upon tube inversion (Fig. 1B), which precludes the
l
a
306 formation of a bi-continuous emulsion gel at these concentrations. Taken together, these
n
307 results suggest that a structured emulsion gel was not formed at S values below 0.3. In
r
u
308 contrast, well-defined oil droplets were observed in samples with S values between 0.3
o
309 and 0.6. Moreover, the average oil droplet diameter in emulsion gels increased from
J
310 1.9±0.55 μm to 6.5±2.14 μm as S increased from 0.3 to 0.6 (Fig. 1C and Fig. 1D).
311 Increasing S reduces droplet–droplet interactions and lowers the bulk viscosity of the
312 system. As a result, stress is transmitted less efficiently to the droplet interface,
313 weakening droplet breakup and leading to larger average oil droplet sizes.
314 Simultaneously, dilution of Spirulina-derived stabilizers at the oil–water interface
315 promoted droplet coalescence, highlighting that both continuous-phase viscosity and
316 stabilizer availability govern droplet size in Spirulina-based emulsion gels. Emulsion
317 gels can be classified into emulsion-filled gels and particulate gels. In emulsion-filled
318 gels, a continuous gel matrix forms the primary load-bearing network, with emulsion
319 droplets dispersed within it. In contrast, particulate gels are formed when the emulsion
320 droplets themselves act as the structural units (Dickinson, 2012). Since ball milling does
12
321 not convert Spirulina into a continuous gel (Fig. S1), the resulting emulsion gels in this
322 case is considered a particulate gel.

503 sweep tests showed that the G′ remained consistently higher than the G′′ across the
504 entire frequency range (Fig. 5C). This predominance of G′ over G′′ confirms the
505 formation of a robust, solid-like gel network in the PSP–PC emulsion, similar to that
f

r
514 in the formation andu mechanical integrity of the emulsion gel.
o
515 Polysaccharides generally exhibit weak surface activity and therefore do not
J
516 induce emulsion formation via direct adsorption at the oil–water interface; instead, their
517 contribution is likely due to depletion attraction. To verify this hypothesis, time-
518 dependent oil–water interfacial tension measurements were performed (Fig. S7).
519 Spirulina polysaccharides (PS) showed weak interfacial adsorption, as the equilibrium
520 interfacial tension decreased only slightly to 21.5 mN/m at 1 mg/mL. In contrast,
521 phycocyanin (PY) exhibited strong surface activity, reducing the equilibrium interfacial
522 tension to 12.5 mN/m at 0.1 mg/mL, consistent with substantial adsorption at the
523 interface. Notably, in the presence of 0.5 mg/mL PS, the adsorption kinetics of PC were
524 markedly accelerated, and the equilibrium interfacial tension was further reduced to
525 10.8 mN/m. This enhancement is attributed to polysaccharide-induced depletion
526 attraction, which promotes PC accumulation at the oil–water interface and facilitates
527 emulsion gel formation. It is notable that the formation of emulsion gel was not solely
22
528 driven by depletion attraction. This enhancement is likely driven by a synergistic effect:
529 while polysaccharide-induced depletion attraction promotes the initial clustering of PC-
530 coated droplets, the accumulation of PC at the interface provides necessary electrostatic
531 repulsion to prevent coalescence. Additionally, the increased viscosity of the water
532 continuous phase provides a structural framework that further facilitates the formation
533 of a stable, macroscopic emulsion gel.

r
p

r
540 stability to the emulsion gel. Importantly, although the non-adsorbed PS and proteins
P
541 remaining in the bulk phase do not form a self-supporting hydrogel at these
l
542 concentrations (Fig. 5A), they astill play a crucial role by increasing the viscosity of the
n
543 continuous phase, thereby promoting the assembly of oil droplets into a more robust
r

545 3.5 3D-printed properties of emulsion gels
J
546 To evaluate the 3D printability of Spirulina emulsion gels, cylindrical structures
547 were fabricated using a commercial extrusion-based 3D printer. The result suggested
548 that emulsion gels with S = 0.3–0.4 exhibited excellent printability, as evidenced by the
549 successful fabrication of cylindrical structures (Fig. S8). Furthermore, the printing
550 accuracy (Fig S8 1B) and printing stability (Figure S8 C) were evaluated. Emulsion
551 gels with S of 0.3 yielded a higher printing accuracy (99.57±0.39%), closely matching
552 the designed dimensions, whereas emulsion gels with S of 0.4 resulted in a slightly
553 lower accuracy (92.62±1.06%). Regarding printing stability, emulsion gels with S of
554 0.3 demonstrated nearly perfect structural stability (99.77±0.18%) over 30 min,
555 indicating excellent resistance to post-printing deformation. In comparison, emulsion
556 gels with S of 0.4 showed a markedly lower stability (85.93±1.54%), confirming its
557 weaker ability to maintain structural integrity after printing. In extrusion-based 3D
23
558 printing, printability is primarily governed by the rheological properties of the 3D
559 printing ink, including shear-thinning behavior, yield strength, and structural recovery.
560 Shear-thinning behavior facilitates smooth extrusion through narrow nozzles of the 3D
561 printer, while adequate viscosity and elastic modulus are required to maintain shape
562 fidelity after extrusion. Emulsion gels with S = 0.3 displayed rheological characteristics
563 conducive to 3D printing, including higher viscosity, G′, and yield strength (Fig. 2A,
564 B), suggesting sufficient mechanical stability to support deposited layers. Thixotropic
565 recovery is another key parameter for printing fidelity, as rapid structural rebuilding
f
o
566 after shear cessation is necessary to prevent filament collapse and oil migration. As
o
567 shown in Fig. S9 and Table S2, the emulsion gels with S = 0.3 exhibited both high initial
r
p
568 G′ and the highest thixotropic recovery (93.34 ±0.64%) among the samples. A further
-
569 increase in S to 0.6 led to a pronounced decerease in recovery (16.02 ±0.08%), which
r
570 adversely affected printing stability. Recovery values exceeding 70% are generally
P
571 considered sufficient to maintain shape fidelity during extrusion-based printing (Patel,
l
572 et al., 2014; Tang, Gao, & Taang, 2023; D. Zhang, et al., 2024), which explains the
n
573 superior printing performance observed for emulsion gels with S = 0.3.

601 4. Conclusions
602 In conclusion, a simple and scalable strategy was established to produce property-
603 tailored emulsion gels by ball-milling a mixture of Spirulina, water, and oil. The water-
604 to-oil ratio (S) was the main factor influencing the properties of the prepared emulsion
605 gel. Rheological and microstructure results indicate that an increase in S from 0.3 to 0.6
606 resulted in a reduction in the viscosity of the emulsion gel, an increase in the size of the
607 oil droplets, and eventual disruption of the gel structure of the emulsion gel when S
608 reached 0.6. The emulsion gel with S = 0.3 was the most stable, retaining its structure
609 for over 100 days. Mechanistic studies shown that ball milling leads to the release of
610 phycocyanin and polysaccharides from Spirulina cells, which is key to the formation
611 of emulsion gels. Polysaccharides promote the adsorption of phycocyanin at the oil-
612 water interface through the depletion effect and promote the formation of a three-
25
613 dimensional gel network with high viscosity, high elasticity and structural integrity. In
614 vitro digestion demonstrated that the emulsion gel supported sustained release of
615 sesamol and increased its bioaccessibility by more than twofold. This approach
616 provides a scalable, whole-biomass strategy for designing sustainable functional
617 emulsion gels with tunable properties.
