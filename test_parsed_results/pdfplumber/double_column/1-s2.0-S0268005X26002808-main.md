3.2.2. FTIR analysis of RGI-rich pectin at different precipitation pH
The FTIR spectra of RGI-rich pectin samples prepared under different
PpH conditions are shown in Fig. 2A. All seven samples exhibited similar
spectral profiles, corroborating the monosaccharide composition results
and indicating that PpH did not introduce new characteristic functional
groups into the pectin, although some primary-structure-related indices
were found to vary significantly among treatments. Notably, the hy-
droxyl absorption band near 3400 cm (cid:0) 1 exhibited broadening, gradually
overlapping with the C–H stretching region. This spectral change in-
dicates a weakening of hydrogen bonding between water molecules and
pectin chains, likely accompanied by an increase in intramolecular
hydrogen bonding within the pectin structure (Wang et al., 2019). This
shift is consistent with enhanced molecular aggregation. In addition,
minor variations were observed in the absorption bands around 1740
cm (cid:0) 1 and 1630–1600 cm (cid:0) 1, corresponding to ester and carboxyl group
vibrations, respectively. The degree of methylesterification (DM%) was
quantified from the integrated areas of the 1740 and 1630 cm (cid:0) 1 bands
(Fig. 2B). As shown in Fig. 2B, DM% decreased significantly with
increasing precipitation pH, with a pronounced drop between PpH 2–4
and then reaching a relatively stable level at higher PpH (PpH ≥4). This
result indicates that mild de-esterification may occur under higher PpH
conditions during ethanol precipitation, thereby increasing the propor-
tion of de-esterified carboxyl groups (Yang et al., 2018).

With increasing PpH, the balance of intermolecular interactions
progressively shifts. The effective hydration of pectin chains is weak-
ened in the ethanol–water medium due to reduced solvent polarity and
competitive interactions with ethanol, leading to diminished polymer-
–solvent interactions. This reduction in hydration facilitates closer chain
proximity and promotes the exposure of hydrophobic domains, thereby
strengthening hydrophobic interactions. Simultaneously, hydrogen
bonding within and between pectin side chains becomes more pro-
nounced. The combined enhancement of hydrophobic interactions and
inter-chain hydrogen bonding drives the expulsion of solvent and
entrapped air from inter-chain spaces, reduces chain spacing, and pro-
motes inter-chain association, ultimately resulting in the formation of a
denser aggregated structure.
Although increasing PpH induces ionization of carboxyl groups into
–COO-, which would typically introduce electrostatic repulsion, this
effect is substantially attenuated by ionic shielding from counterions (e.
+
g., Na introduced during pH adjustment with NaOH) present in the
system. As a result, electrostatic repulsion does not prevent chain
approach, allowing aggregation to proceed under the dominance of
hydrophobic interactions and hydrogen bonding.
