Abstract
24
Emulsion gels with tunable textures are promising carriers for hydrophobic food
25
ingredients and have been widely used in the food industry. However, the fabrication
26
of emulsion gels frequently depends on isolated food components, such as
27
polysaccharides and proteins, or on chemically or physically modified derivatives.
28
These methods typically involve labor-intensive extraction, purification, or
29
modification steps, which limit their scalability and practical application. Herein, a
30
straightforward and scalable strategy was developed to produce property-tailored
31
emulsion gels by ball-milling a mixture of Spirulina, water, and oil. The effects of
32
Spirulina biomass content and water-to-oil volume ratio (S) on the microstructure,
33
stability, and rheological behavior were investigated. Emulsion gels with an S of 0.3
34
demonstrated the highest level of stability, while the biomass content of Spirulina
35
exerted an insignificant effect on the properties. During ball milling, the cellular
36
structure of Spirulina is disrupted, resulting in the release of phycocyanin and
37
polysaccharides. The polysaccharide-induced depletion effect promotes the enrichment
38
of phycocyanin at the oil–water interface, thereby facilitating emulsion gel formation.
39
The emulsion gel could be used as a bio-ink for 3D printing, and optimal printability
40
was achieved at S = 0.3. In addition, the emulsion gels were used for sesamol delivery,
41
and their bioaccessibility was almost twofold that of the bulk oil. Moreover, the ball-
42
milling strategy was effective for stabilizing a range of vegetable oils, indicating its
43
broad applicability. Overall, the whole-Spirulina biomass approach provides a
44
straightforward, universal, and scalable route for designing functional emulsion gels for
45
advanced food applications.

1. Introduction
50
Oil-in-water emulsion gels (O/W) are soft, solid–like structures in which oil
51
droplets are immobilized either within a three-dimensional crosslinked polymer
52
network (emulsion-filled gels) or within a network formed by aggregated oil droplets
53
themselves (particulate emulsion gels) (Niu, Zhang, Mujumdar, & Li, 2026; Shu,
54
McClements, Luo, Liu, & Ye, 2025). Emulsion gels have attracted considerable
55
attention in the field of food science due to their ability to encapsulate, protect, and
56
control the release of bioactive compounds (Song, Li, Shang, Wang, & Tan, 2023; H.
57
T. Wang, Y. D. Sun, Y. T. Hou, & M. Q. Tan, 2025). The preparation of emulsion gels,
58
however, typically depends on isolated food components, such as polysaccharides or
59
proteins, or on chemically or physically modified derivatives. These approaches often
60
require labor-intensive extraction, purification, or modification processes, which limit
61
their scalability and practical application. Importantly, the growing consumer demand
62
for sustainable, clean-label foods has spurred significant interest in emulsion gels made
63
from natural ingredients with minimal processing, underscoring the need to develop
64
novel biopolymer gelation strategies.
65
Spirulina, a blue-green microalga (Cyanobacterium), is well known for its high
66
nutritional value and sustainability and has been widely used in foods as a dietary
67
supplement or whole-food ingredient (Nakamoto, Assis, de Oliveira, & Braga, 2023;
68
Villaro-Cos, Acien, Lafarga, & Sanchez, 2024). Proteins in Spirulina, particularly the
69
water-soluble phycocyanin, have demonstrated strong emulsifying ability and have
70
been extensively employed to stabilize emulsions. On this basis, Spirulina protein-
71
based emulsion gels have been developed for applications such as fat replacement (J.
72
X. Guo, Huang, Gu, & Meng, 2025) and three-dimensional food printing (J. X. Guo,
73
Gu, & Meng, 2024). In addition, emulsion gels constructed from Spirulina protein
74
isolates and carboxymethyl chitosan have been designed for curcumin delivery (Mao,
75
et al., 2025), while Spirulina protein-chitosan complexes have been reported to stabilize
76
Pickering emulsions for astaxanthin encapsulation (R. G. Liu, Li, Zhou, & Tan, 2023).
77
However, these approaches typically require protein isolation, chemical modification,
78
or cross-linking reactions involving non-food-grade reagents, which limit their
79
Journal Pre-proof
4
scalability and broader application in food products. Recently, the direct utilization of
80
whole biomass has emerged as a promising strategy for emulsion and emulsion gel
81
preparation, offering a more sustainable and simplified alternative. For example, whole
82
Gracilaria biomass has been directly employed as an emulsifier to fabricate emulsions
83
for astaxanthin delivery and fat replacement (Shao, et al., 2026), highlighting the
84
potential of whole-biomass-based approaches for the development of clean-label and
85
sustainable emulsion gels.
86
Proteins and polysaccharides are the most widely used food-grade biopolymers for
87
the construction of emulsion gels (Zhi, et al., 2023). Conventional emulsion gel
88
preparation procedures involve separately adding proteins or polysaccharides. In
89
contrast, whole-biomass-based approaches involve the simultaneous release of cellular
90
compounds, mainly proteins and polysaccharides, during high-energy processing,
91
which enables their direct participation in emulsion gel formation. Proteins function as
92
emulsifiers due to their high surface activity. In contrast, polysaccharides typically
93
contribute indirectly to the emulsion gel formation process by increasing continuous-
94
phase viscosity or inducing depletion attraction. Recent results have demonstrated that
95
cooperative interactions between proteins and polysaccharides can substantially
96
enhance emulsion stability and promote gel network formation (Kim, et al., 2017; Tong,
97
et al., 2023), particularly in particulate emulsion gels. In the case of Spirulina, the whole
98
biomass has been employed in the preparation of emulsion gels. Depletion attraction
99
has been proposed as a potential underlying mechanism; however, experimental
100
evidence supporting it remains limited (Feng, et al., 2023a). As a result, the specific
101
mechanisms governing protein–polysaccharide cooperation, particularly when both
102
components originate from a single natural source, remain to be fully elucidated.
103
Ball milling is a straightforward and scalable method for processing various food
104
formulations. The freeze–thaw stability of κ-carrageenan-based emulsion gels could be
105
improved by ball milling (Xu, et al., 2025). It is important to note that by adjusting the
106
ratios of the raw materials, the characteristics of food formulations prepared via ball
107
milling can be easily controlled, indicating that the ball milling method offers a
108
straightforward approach to modifying product properties. The rheological properties
109
Journal Pre-proof
5
of oleogels prepared by ball milling can be effectively tuned by regulating the water
110
content of the initial formulation (G.-S. Wang, et al., 2022). Furthermore, the high shear
111
forces generated during ball milling enabled the one-step preparation of emulsion gels
112
from soy protein isolate (SPI) hydrogels, wherein gel formation was regulated by the
113
depletion-induced attraction effect mediated by konjac glucomannan (Z. Yang, et al.,
114
2025). In recent years, the development of 3D-printable emulsion gels for functional
115
ingredient delivery has attracted growing interest (Niu, et al., 2026), creating demand
116
for processing methods that are both scalable and controllable. Compared with other
117
methods such as high-pressure homogenization or ultrasonication, ball milling offers a
118
practical approach that meets these requirements because it can be readily scaled for
119
larger production volumes. In addition, key processing parameters, such as milling time
120
and rotation speed, can be easily adjusted to control the properties of the resulting
121
emulsion gels. Despite these advances, systematic investigation into the preparation of
122
emulsion gels via ball milling whole biomass, particularly those that simultaneously
123
enable 3D printability and functional compound delivery, remains limited and merits
124
further study.
125
In the present study, 3D-printable emulsion gels were developed by subjecting a
126
mixture of Spirulina biomass, oil, and water to direct ball milling. A systematic
127
investigation was conducted into the effects of the mass fraction and water content of
128
Spirulina on the microstructure, stability, rheological properties, and 3D printing
129
performance of emulsion gels. Furthermore, the formation mechanism of Spirulina-
130
based emulsion gels was examined. To this end, Spirulina polysaccharides and
131
phycocyanin were isolated to elucidate their respective contributions to gel network
132
formation. Sesamol, a naturally occurring antioxidant with a broad spectrum of
133
biological activities, was incorporated into the emulsion gels, and its bioaccessibility
134
was evaluated. This approach, which combines whole-biomass-derived formulation
135
with a scalable processing method, offers new insights into the design of sustainable
136
emulsion gels for functional food applications.

3.1 Microstructure of emulsion gels
293
Emulsion gels were prepared by ball milling Spirulina powder with oil and water
294
(Fig. 1A). The visual appearance of the mixtures after ball milling was strongly
295
influenced by the water-oil ratio (S). Formulations with S values between 0.1 and 0.2
296
displayed fluid-like behavior and flowed upon tube inversion (Fig. 1B), indicating the
297
absence of a continuous gel network. Whereas when the S increased to the range of
298
0.3–0.5, the fluid mixture transformed into self-standing emulsion gels, suggesting a
299
self-supporting three-dimensional network was formed (S. Wang, et al., 2020).
300
However, a further increase in S to 0.6 led to viscous flow, which may be attributed to
301
excessive dilution of the interfacial stabilizer in the aqueous phase, thereby weakening
302
the gel network (Feng, et al., 2023b). Optical microscopy revealed no obvious oil
303
droplets at S values between 0.1 and 0.2 (Fig. 1C). Furthermore, these samples
304
exhibited fluid-like behavior upon tube inversion (Fig. 1B), which precludes the
305
formation of a bi-continuous emulsion gel at these concentrations. Taken together, these
306
results suggest that a structured emulsion gel was not formed at S values below 0.3. In
307
contrast, well-defined oil droplets were observed in samples with S values between 0.3
308
and 0.6. Moreover, the average oil droplet diameter in emulsion gels increased from
309
1.9±0.55 μm to 6.5±2.14 μm as S increased from 0.3 to 0.6 (Fig. 1C and Fig. 1D).
310
Increasing S reduces droplet–droplet interactions and lowers the bulk viscosity of the
311
system. As a result, stress is transmitted less efficiently to the droplet interface,
312
weakening droplet breakup and leading to larger average oil droplet sizes.
313
Simultaneously, dilution of Spirulina-derived stabilizers at the oil–water interface
314
promoted droplet coalescence, highlighting that both continuous-phase viscosity and
315
stabilizer availability govern droplet size in Spirulina-based emulsion gels. Emulsion
316
gels can be classified into emulsion-filled gels and particulate gels. In emulsion-filled
317
gels, a continuous gel matrix forms the primary load-bearing network, with emulsion
318
droplets dispersed within it. In contrast, particulate gels are formed when the emulsion
319
droplets themselves act as the structural units (Dickinson, 2012). Since ball milling does
320
Journal Pre-proof
13
not convert Spirulina into a continuous gel (Fig. S1), the resulting emulsion gels in this
321
case is considered a particulate gel.

509

511
rapid network-reformation kinetics post-shearing. Collectively, these results
512
demonstrate that both phycocyanin and Spirulina polysaccharides play essential roles
513
in the formation and mechanical integrity of the emulsion gel.
514
Polysaccharides generally exhibit weak surface activity and therefore do not
515
induce emulsion formation via direct adsorption at the oil–water interface; instead, their
516
contribution is likely due to depletion attraction. To verify this hypothesis, time-
517
dependent oil–water interfacial tension measurements were performed (Fig. S7).
518
Spirulina polysaccharides (PS) showed weak interfacial adsorption, as the equilibrium
519
interfacial tension decreased only slightly to 21.5 mN/m at 1 mg/mL. In contrast,
520
phycocyanin (PY) exhibited strong surface activity, reducing the equilibrium interfacial
521
tension to 12.5 mN/m at 0.1 mg/mL, consistent with substantial adsorption at the
522
interface. Notably, in the presence of 0.5 mg/mL PS, the adsorption kinetics of PC were
523
markedly accelerated, and the equilibrium interfacial tension was further reduced to
524
10.8 mN/m. This enhancement is attributed to polysaccharide-induced depletion
525
attraction, which promotes PC accumulation at the oil–water interface and facilitates
526
emulsion gel formation. It is notable that the formation of emulsion gel was not solely
527
Journal Pre-proof
23
driven by depletion attraction. This enhancement is likely driven by a synergistic effect:
528
while polysaccharide-induced depletion attraction promotes the initial clustering of PC-
529
coated droplets, the accumulation of PC at the interface provides necessary electrostatic
530
repulsion to prevent coalescence. Additionally, the increased viscosity of the water
531
continuous phase provides a structural framework that further facilitates the formation
532
of a stable, macroscopic emulsion gel.
533
Overall, based on the interfacial, rheological, and microscopic analyses, the
534
formation of Spirulina emulsion gels can be described as follows (Fig. 5F). Ball milling
535
disrupts Spirulina cells, releasing phycocyanin (PC) and polysaccharides (PS).  PC
536
adsorbs at the oil–water interface, while PS promotes its accumulation through
537
depletion attraction. The resulting oil droplets act as building blocks that assemble into
538
a three-dimensional particulate gel network, imparting high viscosity, elasticity, and
539
stability to the emulsion gel. Importantly, although the non-adsorbed PS and proteins
540
remaining in the bulk phase do not form a self-supporting hydrogel at these
541
concentrations (Fig. 5A), they still play a crucial role by increasing the viscosity of the
542
continuous phase, thereby promoting the assembly of oil droplets into a more robust
543
and interconnected three-dimensional network.

3.5 3D-printed properties of emulsion gels
545
To evaluate the 3D printability of Spirulina emulsion gels, cylindrical structures
546
were fabricated using a commercial extrusion-based 3D printer. The result suggested
547
that emulsion gels with S = 0.3–0.4 exhibited excellent printability, as evidenced by the
548
successful fabrication of cylindrical structures (Fig. S8). Furthermore, the printing
549
accuracy (Fig S8 1B) and printing stability (Figure S8 C) were evaluated. Emulsion
550
gels with S of 0.3 yielded a higher printing accuracy (99.57±0.39%), closely matching
551
the designed dimensions, whereas emulsion gels with S of 0.4 resulted in a slightly
552
lower accuracy (92.62±1.06%). Regarding printing stability, emulsion gels with S of
553
0.3 demonstrated nearly perfect structural stability (99.77±0.18%) over 30 min,
554
indicating excellent resistance to post-printing deformation. In comparison, emulsion
555
gels with S of 0.4 showed a markedly lower stability (85.93±1.54%), confirming its
556
weaker ability to maintain structural integrity after printing. In extrusion-based 3D
557
Journal Pre-proof
24
printing, printability is primarily governed by the rheological properties of the 3D
558
printing ink, including shear-thinning behavior, yield strength, and structural recovery.
559
Shear-thinning behavior facilitates smooth extrusion through narrow nozzles of the 3D
560
printer, while adequate viscosity and elastic modulus are required to maintain shape
561
fidelity after extrusion. Emulsion gels with S = 0.3 displayed rheological characteristics
562
conducive to 3D printing, including higher viscosity, G′, and yield strength (Fig. 2A,
563
B), suggesting sufficient mechanical stability to support deposited layers. Thixotropic
564
recovery is another key parameter for printing fidelity, as rapid structural rebuilding
565
after shear cessation is necessary to prevent filament collapse and oil migration. As
566
shown in Fig. S9 and Table S2, the emulsion gels with S = 0.3 exhibited both high initial
567
G′ and the highest thixotropic recovery (93.34 ±0.64%) among the samples. A further
568
increase in S to 0.6 led to a pronounced decrease in recovery (16.02 ±0.08%), which
569
adversely affected printing stability. Recovery values exceeding 70% are generally
570
considered sufficient to maintain shape fidelity during extrusion-based printing (Patel,
571
et al., 2014; Tang, Gao, & Tang, 2023; D. Zhang, et al., 2024), which explains the
572
superior printing performance observed for emulsion gels with S = 0.3.

4. Conclusions
601
In conclusion, a simple and scalable strategy was established to produce property-
602
tailored emulsion gels by ball-milling a mixture of Spirulina, water, and oil. The water-
603
to-oil ratio (S) was the main factor influencing the properties of the prepared emulsion
604
gel. Rheological and microstructure results indicate that an increase in S from 0.3 to 0.6
605
resulted in a reduction in the viscosity of the emulsion gel, an increase in the size of the
606
oil droplets, and eventual disruption of the gel structure of the emulsion gel when S
607
reached 0.6. The emulsion gel with S = 0.3 was the most stable, retaining its structure
608
for over 100 days. Mechanistic studies shown that ball milling leads to the release of
609
phycocyanin and polysaccharides from Spirulina cells, which is key to the formation
610
of emulsion gels. Polysaccharides promote the adsorption of phycocyanin at the oil-
611
water interface through the depletion effect and promote the formation of a three-
612
Journal Pre-proof
26
dimensional gel network with high viscosity, high elasticity and structural integrity. In
613
vitro digestion demonstrated that the emulsion gel supported sustained release of
614
sesamol and increased its bioaccessibility by more than twofold. This approach
615
provides a scalable, whole-biomass strategy for designing sustainable functional
616
emulsion gels with tunable properties.
