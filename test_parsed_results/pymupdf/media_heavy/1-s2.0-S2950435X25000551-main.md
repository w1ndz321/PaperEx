A B S T R A C T
Over the past few decades, advancements in microfabrication and biomaterials have driven the innovation of
microfluidic technologies, significantly advancing the development of lab-on-a-chip (LOC) systems. As trans­
formative tools in precision medicine and high-throughput biomolecular analysis, LOC platforms can process and
analyze numerous samples rapidly and cost-effectively, all within compact individual devices. This review
summarizes the latest developments in the fabrication and detection technologies of LOC devices, along with
their applications in precision medicine and biomolecular analysis. Furthermore, we anticipate a future where
LOC technologies will redefine the landscape of medical diagnostics and biomolecular research, ultimately
transforming biomedical research and clinical practice.
1. Introduction
Lab-on-a-chip (LOC) is a miniaturized device that integrates mul­
tiple functions of a traditional laboratory onto a single small chip. These
functions typically encompass a range of laboratory operations, in­
cluding sample processing, chemical reactions, separation, and detec­
tion [1,2]. The core technology underlying LOC is microfluidics, which
allows for the manipulation and processing of minute fluid volumes
(10−9 to 10−18 liters) within micron-scale channels [3–5]. Microfluidic
systems were initially established in the 1950s, and have since experi­
enced continuous advancements and refinements [6]. First, the precise
distribution of small amounts of liquid in the nanoliter and picoliter
ranges has been achieved. Next, the miniaturization of gas chromato­
graphs has been realized [7]. Following this, microfluidic devices for
high-pressure liquid chromatography columns have been fabricated
[8]. Additionally, various microfluidic structures have been created
using silicon micromachining techniques [9–11].
These innovations facilitate the development of effective drug delivery
systems [12] and liquid biopsy technologies [13,14]. Collectively, these
ongoing technological advancements in microfluidic platforms have laid
the groundwork for the advancement of LOC systems [15]. In the context
of novel personalized medicine and point-of-care testing (POCT), clinical
practice has demanded more precise and efficient testing and diagnostic
methods, leading to the widespread adoption of LOC technology in high-
throughput molecular analysis [16]. For example, the Cepheid GeneX­
pert® cartridge-based PCR system has already been implemented in rapid
nucleic-acid and tuberculosis diagnosis [17], while the BioFire Fil­
mArray® multiplex PCR panel enables simultaneous detection of more
than 20 types respiratory pathogens within 75 min [18]. LOC technology
enables automated parallel processing of biological samples, which sig­
nificantly reduces the time required for data analysis compared to tradi­
tional laboratory techniques [21]. To further highlight the advantages of
LOC systems, Table 1 provides a quantitative comparison between re­
presentative LOC-based platforms and traditional laboratory techniques.
Moreover, the compatibility of LOC devices with advanced detection
methods, such as mass spectrometry, fluorescence-based assays, and
electrophoresis, has broadened their applicability across genomics, pro­
teomics, and metabolomics [22–29]. This compatibility has significantly
promoted the rapid development of LOC in precision medicine and high-
throughput biomolecular analysis.

In recent years, molecular diagnostics and treatments leveraging
LOC systems have gained significant momentum, transitioning from
research laboratories to clinical settings [30]. While several reviews
have addressed the extensively catalogued foundational fabrication
techniques or broader applications of LOC systems, there remains a
need for a critical investigation linking material innovations to real-
world diagnostic performance. This review aims to fill this gap by
providing a comprehensive overview of recent advancements in LOC
device fabrication, detection efficiency, and their applications in high-
throughput molecular diagnostics, highlighting their impact on the
future of biomedical research and clinical practice.
2. The development of LOC device fabrication
The advancement of device fabrication is a crucial factor in trans­
forming traditional bulky and costly laboratory equipment, along with
labour-intensive assays, into a miniaturized LOC system. This trans­
formation is characterized by high cost-effectiveness and efficiency. A
functional LOC typically consists of three main components: the chips,
the analyser (which includes the driving source and signal detection
device), and functional reagent kit [31]. In recent years, LOC devices
have experienced continuous developments in material selection,
manufacturing processes, and application adaptability for various ap­
plications.
2.1. Diversity in material selection
In the early stages of chip manufacturing, silicon and glass were the
primary materials used for chips due to their resistance to organic
solvents, ease of metal deposition, stable electroosmotic mobility, and
excellent thermal conductivity [32–34](Fig. 1A). However, these ma­
terials also have significant drawbacks, including brittleness, complex
thermal management that can adversely affect the stability of LOC
operations, and high processing difficulties and costs that hinder large-
scale production [38,39].
Subsequently, polymer materials emerged as the preferred choice
for microfluidic chip manufacturing, owing to their exceptional optical
transparency and ease of processing [40–42]. Common polymer mate­
rials include polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) and various thermoplastics,
such as polycarbonate (PC), polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA), poly­
styrene (PS), and polyvinyl chloride (PVC) (Fig. 1B-1C).
Recently, the chip manufacturing process has incorporated various
new materials to enhance the functionality of LOC systems. To improve
material stability within the LOC, researchers have continuously im­
plemented modification techniques on polymers and proposed new
materials [43,44]. For instance, Gokaltun et al. [45] developed new
copolymers combining polyethylene glycol and PDMS segments to
improve hydrophilicity and reduce non-specific protein adsorption
while maintaining cell biocompatibility. Rabe et al. [46] chemically
bonded biotin to the surfaces of plastic microfluidic chips to increase
the surface area of the chip and demonstrate higher capture rates of
analyte. Aubrecht et al. designed a dual-cure polymer using a thiol-ene-
epoxy formulation to improve biocompatibility and conduct various
cell-based assays [47–49]. With a growing awareness of environmental
sustainability, Zimmer et al. introduced natural polysaccharide chitosan
into the microfluidic system manufacturing process [50,51]. Ad­
ditionally, the introduction of nanomaterials into LOC devices has im­
proved the material properties and expanded application areas. Func­
tionalized Pluronic nano constructs exhibit biocompatibility, thermo-
sensitivity, and low adhesion, which can be used in medical diagnosis,
medical imaging, and the creation of miniaturized LOC. The use of
enzyme-like nanomaterials in LOC microfluidic systems can provide
effective solutions for real-time biological detection [52,53]. These in­
novations can enhance detection sensitivity and explore new possibi­
lities in LOC applications (Table 2).
2.2. Progress in LOC manufacturing process and related technologies
2.2.1. Chip manufacturing
The primary fabrication methods for chips made from silicon and
glass materials are well-established and include photolithography,
chemical etching, electrochemical etching, deep reactive ion etching, as
well as physical and chemical vapor deposition [54–63]. Compared to
silicon and glass materials, the manufacturing of polymer micro­
structures is simpler and avoids the use of dangerous etching reagents.
For instance, The main fabrication technique of PDMS is soft litho­
graphy, which involves directly pouring PDMS onto a master micro-
mold followed by a bonding process. This method is characterized by its
low cost and high flexibility. The entire fabrication process can be
completed in less than 24 h, from concept to realization [68]. Ad­
ditionally, high-volume production of thermoplastic microchips can be
achieved through techniques such as hot embossing, injection molding,
and roll pressing [69].
2.2.2. Microfluidics
The innovative microfluidics technique provides new methodologies
for the precise regulation of fluid at a miniaturized scale, while en­
abling high-throughput data acquisition (Fig. 2). This technique en­
compasses passive-driven LOC platforms relying on channel pressure
and geometric shapes, such as paper-based, [70] centrifugal and ca­
pillary microfluidics [71–75]. Emerging digital microfluidic platforms
based on electrowetting on dielectric (EWOD) automate. droplet
mixing, incubation, and segmentation by utilizing electrode activation
to enhance hydrophilicity [76,77].
LOC systems are continually advancing in the treatment of trace
liquids [78]. Another significant technological advancement in the
design of microfluidic devices is droplet microfluidic systems [79]. It
enables the manipulation and detection of samples in separate com­
partments, achieving high-accuracy and high-throughput analyses [80].

2.2.3. Optical technology
The integration of microfluidic technology with optical methods
enhances device miniaturization and detection sensitivity, broadening
applications in microanalytical chemistry and biomedical analysis.
Most microfluidic detection systems employ optical techniques, in­
cluding fluorescence, fiber optics, absorbance measurements, and
image recognition [87,88]. Combining LOC with optical sensing mini­
mizes optical losses, enhances the signal-to-noise ratio, and improves
detection sensitivity. Zanini et al. reported parallel waveguides with a
T-junction on the LiNbO3 surface for effective droplet sensing [89].
When one of these waveguides is illuminated at its input, the light can
be transmitted into the channel, contributing to more effective sensing.
The collected signal intensity can reflect the temporal detection of the
microdroplets [90]. Guo et al. [91] proposed a depth-multiplexed
ptychographic microscopy for on-chip sensing and parallel imaging.
Advanced microscopy integration has facilitated immunoassays, real-
time cell motion tracking, and single-molecule investigations, by
leveraging signals from reflected, transmitted, or scattered light waves
[92]. Sano et al. [93] demonstrated a LOC system with a distributed
feedback dye laser for highly specific viral detection through fluores­
cence labelling and optical excitation.
2.2.4. Microelectromechanical systems
Mature microelectromechanical systems (MEMS) technology is es­
sential for achieving the miniaturization of LOC systems [94–96].
MEMS-based microflow control devices can precisely regulate and de­
liver fluid flow throughout the circuit (Fig. 3). The LOC system fully
integrated with MEMS technology is capable of performing various
essential processes, including cell separation, sample transportation,
mixing, cell lysis, and electrochemical sensing [97–100].
Recently, researchers have made continuous attempts and innovations
to enhance the functionality of integrated MEMS chip laboratories and ex­
pand their application fields. Park, K et al. [101] designed MEMS-based
biochip and created portable auxiliary diagnostic tools for breast cancer. Fan
et al. [102,103] developed a 9 × 9×9 mm³ acoustic micropump based on
MEMS technology for drug delivery, weighing 1.16 g. Deng et al. [105]
designed a MEMS cantilever beam integrated with a capacitive pressure
sensor and gold nanoparticles to measure capacitance changes and detect
target genes. Bian et al. [106] fabricated a disposable electrode chip on a
polyester substrate using MEMS technology, which combines an electrode
array with a microreactor cell. Additionally, the advanced integration of
MEMS and multiple technologies have been explored. For instance, com­
bining SU-8 processing with printed circuit board MEMS (PCB-MEMS)
technology has led to an autonomous low-cost microfluidic propulsion
system [107]. Improved fabrication techniques of Carbon microelec­
tromechanical systems (C-MEMS) have enhanced the sensitivity and speci­
ficity of nanoelectronics biosensors, leading to more accurate bacterial de­
tection [108,109].
2.2.5. Three-dimensional printing
Advanced three-dimensional (3D) printing technology offers high
geometric flexibility, rapid printing speeds, and precision down to
100 nm [110]. It can generally be divided into light-based methods,
which use light to cure materials sensitive to it, and ink-based methods,
which apply inks with specific viscoelastic properties [111,112]. Re­
searchers utilized 3D printing for the production of LOC devices, en­
abling increased flexibility in employing intricate geometries and
multifunctional composites (Fig. 4) [117–119].
The promotion of 3D printing technology is driving technological
innovation in LOC systems [120]. This includes the development of
porous membranes with 7 µm pore sizes for seeding living cells, [121]
the design of a low-cost sample preparation device for filtering and
separating blood cells, [122] and the establishment of dynamic detec­
tion platforms for target biomarkers that function without manual in­
tervention [123]. Additionally, 3D printing facilitates the large-scale
production of standardized organ models that replicate physiological
conditions and supports the creation of innovative microfluidic one-
way valves and pumps [124,125]. Furthermore, N. Reiter et al. utilized
3D printing technology to fabricate microfluidic pathways and to rea­
lize magnetophoretic cell sorting [126].

2.2.6. Packaging and portability
The increasing demand for rapid on-site testing has placed sig­
nificant requirements on the packaging and portability of LOC devices.
The evolution of packaging and portability has transitioned from two-
dimensional electrode arrays to portable digital microfluidics platforms
integrated with electrochemical sensing, and ultimately to microfluidic
systems that allow droplet manipulation via smartphone controls [127].
Over the past decade, a variety of functional materials, including na­
noparticles, magnetic materials, and conductive composite polymers,
have been progressively employed to enhance the portability of wear­
able systems [132,133]. Zhao, S et al. [134] pioneered an integrated
technology for 3D wafer-level chip-scale packaging. It has a tempera­
ture-controlling system constructed from a high-density serpentine
electrical circuit on the back, minimizing environmental interference
during experiments.
2.2.7. Sensor technology
The incorporation of electrochemical detection methods into LOC
systems facilitates cost-effective and highly sensitive molecular detec­
tion (Fig. 5) [135,136]. The selection of electrode materials focuses on
the use of carbon nanomaterials, metal nanoparticles, and conductive
polymers [137–139]. Liu et al. developed an electrochemical sensor
platform for detecting C-reactive protein (CRP) using molecularly im­
printed polymers and doped tungsten disulfide [140]. LOC systems
integrated with biosensors facilitate the direct assessment of physiolo­
gical and biological parameters during cell growth [141,142]. The
combination of aptamers and biomimetic receptors with nano­
technology and LOC technologies enhances the capabilities of biosen­
sing [143].
Furthermore, biosensing platforms integrated into LOC systems de­
monstrate significant potential for nucleic acid extraction, amplifica­
tion, and rapid detection [144,145]. Liu et al. [146,147] synthesized
upconversion nanoparticles as luminescent agents for a luminescence
biosensing platform to detect virus. Asghari et al. [148] summarized the
latest developments in optical label-free biosensors, contributing to the
creation of a portable biosensing platform for POCT of viruses char­
acterized by high sensitivity. Sensor technology has also been used for
the detection of tumor biomarkers, Sinibaldi et al. [149] combined
label-free and fluorescent detection modes for the single-step detection
of epidermal growth factor receptor 2 within 20 min, benefiting breast
cancer diagnosis.
2.2.8. Automation and artificial intelligence
In the past decade, the challenge of automating microscale liquid
manipulation has been a significant obstacle hindering the development
of LOC technologies. Durrer et al. developed a robot-assisted system
comprising a robotic arm and an acoustofluidic end effector to achieve
automation [150]. Huang et al. further proposed two complementary
automation processing flows for LOC, designed to improve spatial
layout and enhance time efficiency, termed BigIntegr and FastIntegr
[151].
Artificial intelligence (AI) has the potential to control and enhance
the design of LOC systems, [152,153] enabling rapid analysis of large
datasets, the development of complex statistical models, [154] and the
use of prior experimental results to predict optimization strategies
[155–157]. Moreover, AI has been applied to process cell quantification
analysis in images, yielding results that closely align with conventional
software while demonstrating superior efficiency [158]. The in­
corporation of AI into LOC systems promises significant advantages for
high-throughput detection, novel drug discovery, and POCT [159]. The
evolution of AI and LOC is mutually beneficial, as evidenced by their
complementary progression Table 3 summarizes recent research that
integrates LOC with AI across these domains.

3. Application of LOC in precision medicine and high-throughput
biomolecular analysis
3.1. Progress in the application of LOC in high-throughput sequencing
The completion of the human genome sequence has significantly
advanced DNA sequencing in biomedicine, while the integrated de­
velopment of LOC and single-molecule techniques has further fa­
cilitated high-precision and high-throughput single-molecule analysis
within the biological sciences (Fig. 6) [160–162]. The integration of
microfluidic technology and related disciplines establishes a foundation
for high-throughput fluid handling in LOC systems [163,164]. LOC can
conduct millions of parallel biochemical reactions simultaneously and
perform nanoscale analyte capture and detection [165,166]. Moreover,
LOC has successfully integrated sample processing, template amplifi­
cation, purification, and analysis, making it
widely applicable in high-throughput nucleic acid sequencing
[167,168]. The optical fluid device developed by Soltani et al. utilizes
photon interference functions to achieve accurate high-throughput
measurements [179].
Cell-free DNA (cfDNA) refers to DNA fragments released into the
plasma due to degradation and can be found in various bodily fluids.
The concentration of cfDNA changes in response to tissue damage,
cancer, and inflammatory reactions. Monitoring cfDNA can provide
valuable insights into genomic mutations, tumor burden, drug re­
sistance, and prognosis. LOC technology facilitates the handling of
cfDNA samples, enabling high-throughput screening and accurate
quantitative analysis [180]. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) serves
as a sensitive auxiliary diagnostic tool, particularly in evaluating ther­
apeutic responses in cancer treatment through cfDNA analysis. How­
ever, preparing sequencing libraries remains a complex and time-con­
suming tasks. The widespread application of LOC offers a viable
alternative to traditional NGS library preparation. Hofmann et al. in­
troduced a commercial LOC platform, demonstrating strong result
comparability to manual operations (Pearson r = 0.94) [181]. In this
system, magnetic bead-based nucleic acid capture and washing were
integrated into a closed microfluidic cartridge, enabling automated li­
brary construction and minimizing aerosol contamination risks. Sedighi
et al. [182] developed a NanoBioArray chip capable of detecting single
nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), utilizing spiral channels for parallel
sample introduction in high-throughput analysis. The spiral micro­
channel architecture generates passive hydrodynamic sorting, allowing
uniform distribution of samples across multiple sensing sites without
additional pumps or valves. Singh et al. presented LOC integrated with

3.2. Progress in the application of LOC in single-cell analysis
The real-time quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain
reaction is the traditional approach for RNA virus detection, it re­
quires substantial investments in trained personnel, financial re­
sources, and time. This approach often obscured the genetic char­
acteristics of individual cells [186]. By contrast, LOC platforms
enable effective sequencing separation and exceptionally high-
throughput detection with minimal use of expensive samples and
reagents, thereby facilitating large-scale RNA sequencing at the
single-cell resolution [187–190].
LOC devices are extensively applied in high-throughput screening
and single-cell sequencing research, holding significant importance for
both biomedical and clinical applications [197]. Automated reagent
mixing and cell lysis reduce manual handling steps, ensuring highly
reproducible sequencing output while dramatically shortening total
experimental time. Based on droplet microfluidics, the comprehensive
workflow of LOC lysing, the addition of reagents, and quantitative
mRNA detection, making the specific mRNA expression quantification
across various cell types in just one hour [198]. In droplet-based mi­
crofluidic systems, single cells are encapsulated into picoliter-sized
droplets together with barcoded beads, allowing each cell to undergo
independent reverse transcription. Lan et al. [199] achieved high-
throughput single-cell sequencing of over 50,000 cells per run, facil­
itating the analysis of genetic diversity within various cell populations.
The chip adopts a high-density droplet generation array that con­
tinuously produces uniform microdroplets, ensuring stable single-cell
occupancy and efficient barcode labeling. Compared with conventional
microplate-based single-cell capture, this architecture uses significantly
less reagent volume while generating larger datasets in a shorter time.
By reducing the processing steps and the volume required for single-cell
sequencing, it is possible to measure more samples and genes [200].
Integrating several single-cell isolation chambers and hydrodynamic
traps, LOC can make the capture rate of single cells at almost 100 % and
minimal loss, which is especially advantageous for limited samples to
construct single-cell RNA sequencing efficiently with high quality
[201,202]. After capture, the chip performs on-chip lysis, mRNA ex­
traction, and cDNA synthesis within the same microchannel, mini­
mizing hands-on time and improving RNA recovery from rare clinical
samples. The practical application of LOC in single-cell sequencing
technology not only enhances our understanding of cell heterogeneity,
but also helps reveal the occurrence, development, and treatment of
diseases [203]. Furthermore, it can elucidate genetic interactions to
better understand the molecular basis of these processes.(Fig. 7A-7B)
When optimizing single-cell omics approaches, incorporates processes
such as single-cell encapsulation, sorting, microfluidic single-cell
sorting technologies emphasize the importance of considering cell
morphology and size [205]. Combined with data independent acquisi­
tion and mass spectrometry techniques, LOC can also be adapted for
protein analysis within single cells, providing a comprehensive plat­
form for single-cell biology [206] (Fig. 7C). In these systems, micro­
fluidic chambers permit rapid proteolysis and peptide clean-up in ex­
tremely small volumes, thereby improving mass spectrometric
sensitivity.

3.3. Progress in the application of LOC in biomarker detection
The high-throughput analysis capabilities of LOC technology are cru­
cial for enhancing the accuracy of biomarker detection [207]. LOC tech­
nology has multiple applications, providing a low-cost, rapid, accurate,
and user-friendly approach for inspection and diagnosis (Fig. 8). Tan et al.
has reported the development of a novel nanozyme system by integrating
platinum into the A layer of the V2SnC MAX phase. This innovative system

3.4. Progress in the application of LOC in other biomedical research
The processes of drug screening and development are often lengthy
and costly, heavily dependent on extensive experimental testing and va­
lidation [225]. One significant challenge in drug development is the lim­
ited miniaturization and parallelization techniques in chemistry, along
with the incompatibility of biologically active compounds synthesized
from biological screening methods. The technological development of LOC
has provided new inspiration and opportunities for innovation in biome­
dical research, particularly within the pharmaceutical industry.
The LOC constructed by Benz et al. meets the requirements for both
organic solvents and aqueous solutions related to biological screening.
Liposome formation and cell screening are conducted entirely on the
chip, making the entire process efficient, taking only three days and
requiring approximately one milliliter of solution, from library synth­
esis to cell screening [226]. This chip integrates droplet-based synthesis
chambers and micro-incubation units, allowing reaction conditions to
be precisely controlled in nanoliter volumes. The incidence of antibiotic
resistance is increasing, which affects clinical treatment decisions ser­
iously. Without labels, the innovative LOC technology allows for high-
throughput evaluation of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, as well as for the
swift identification of novel antibiotic-resistant mutants [227]. These
devices usually employ cell-trapping microstructures and optical de­
tection systems, enabling real-time monitoring of bacterial growth
curves in parallel microchambers. Such high-resolution growth pro­
filing helps distinguish resistance phenotypes faster than conventional
cultures.
LOC technology can be used to create various cell culture platforms,
simulate the in vivo tissue environment, promote cell growth, and de­
velop the in vitro models necessary for biomedical research [228,229].
Microfluidic channels can generate stable nutrient and oxygen gra­
dients, providing controllable microenvironments that mimic physio­
logical tissues more closely than static culture plates. More advanced
LOC platforms are capable of generating physiological organs and tis­
sues that closely resemble those in the human body in vitro. These
organ-on-a-chip systems, combined with high-throughput analysis
technology, facilitate RNA sequencing to evaluate gene expression,
improving the accuracy of drug screening [230]. They typically in­
corporate flexible membranes, microvascular channels, and continuous
perfusion, enabling dynamic drug exposure and recapitulation of
human organ-level responses. Caenorhabditis elegans, a type of nema­
tode, is commonly utilized as a classical model organism in biomedical
research, [231] aiding in genetic and chemical screenings, assessing the
impact of chemicals on health and behavior, and facilitating the dis­
covery of potential drug candidates [232]. The microfluidic screening
platforms of LOC significantly enhance the speed of chemical screen­
ings, enabling the observation of individual worms at various time
points to capture more nuanced phenotypes instead of relying solely on
averaged data [233]. This dramatically increases screening throughput
and resolution in drug discovery pipelines.

4. Discussion
4.1. The application advantages of LOC
LOC devices integrate advanced technologies such as microfluidics,
optical systems, MEMS, and biosensors, enabling precise manipulation
of nanoliter-sized droplets containing biochemical samples and re­
agents. LOC technology consolidates multiple laboratory processes,
including DNA sequencing, RNA sequencing, and biochemical analysis,
onto compact microfluidic platforms. The significant advantages of LOC
devices stem from their capacity for high-throughput and parallel
processing of biological samples, which facilitates rapid data acquisi­
tion and accelerates critical applications such as pathogen screening,
drug development, real-time detection, and environmental monitoring
[234].
One of the key strengths of LOC technology lies in its ability to
overcome limitations associated with traditional microfluidic systems,
such as low processing throughput and insufficient concentration fac­
tors. Recent advancements have demonstrated the potential of LOC
devices to achieve high-throughput processing while maintaining high
efficiency. For example, Xiang et al. developed a multiplex microfluidic
concentrator capable of processing up to 16 milliliters per minute,
significantly enhancing the concentration of cells from large volumes of
biological fluids [235]. This device has proven effective in isolating and
purifying rare malignant tumor cells from substantial volumes of ma­
lignant pleural effusion or peritoneal effusion, thereby providing va­
luable support for clinical decision-making.
In addition to these advancements, LOC technology has been widely
adopted in various fields due to its user-friendliness, portability, and
environmental sustainability. Recent studies have highlighted its ap­
plication in point-of-care diagnostics, where rapid and accurate results
are crucial [236]. For instance, LOC devices have been utilized for real-
time pathogen detection in resource-limited settings, demonstrating
their potential to revolutionize healthcare delivery. Furthermore, the
integration of machine learning algorithms with LOC systems has en­
abled automated data analysis, further enhancing their utility in com­
plex biological assays.
Overall, the continuous evolution of LOC technology has addressed
many of the challenges faced by earlier microfluidic systems, offering a
versatile and efficient platform for a wide range of applications. Future
research should focus on further optimizing these devices for specific
clinical and environmental applications, as well as exploring new ma­
terials and fabrication techniques to enhance their performance and
scalability.

testing and immunotherapy screening. Unlike traditional methods,
which often rely on trial-and-error approaches, LOC devices integrate
microfluidics, biosensors, and advanced imaging technologies to pro­
vide a miniaturized, high-throughput platform for personalized cancer
treatment.

limitations of conventional drug screening methods, such as low
throughput, high costs, and lengthy processing times. For example,
patients with advanced malignancies historically endured prolonged
cycles of ineffective chemotherapy before identifying an optimal
treatment regimen. LOC devices, however, facilitate rapid and accurate
drug screening by enabling tumor cell culture, drug uptake analysis,

4.2.2. Early screening and dynamic monitoring of diseases
The low cost and high efficiency of LOC devices, combined with
advanced sensor technologies, provide individual patients with the
opportunity to monitor real-time health parameters and gain a deeper
understanding of their disease status. Portable LOC devices are parti­
cularly valuable for early detection and screening, making them highly
recommended for individuals with suspected clinical symptoms, those
exposed to disease risk factors, and even for routine monitoring of
healthy individuals. LOC technology leverages its microfluidic and
biosensing capabilities to enable precise and rapid analysis of biological
samples, which is critical for applications such as liquid biopsy and
early molecular diagnostics. Unlike traditional molecular techniques,
LOC integrates engineering innovations to miniaturize and automate
complex laboratory processes, making it a powerful tool for POCT.
One of the most promising applications of LOC technology is in the
autonomous and regular screening of tumor-related biomarkers or circu­
lating tumor cells (CTCs). This capability facilitates early auxiliary diag­
nosis of cancer and allows for the assessment of disease progression during
treatment remission [240–242]. For instance, LOC devices have been
successfully applied to the detection of hepatocellular carcinoma, small
cell lung cancer, and cervical pre-cancer, among others [243–245]. By
enabling frequent and non-invasive monitoring, LOC technology helps
avoid missed optimal treatment opportunities due to poor patient com­
pliance. Additionally, immunoassay-based LOC systems have been devel­
oped for the analysis of serum biomarkers such as Clara cell protein 16,
which is useful for early screening of lung injury and identification of
early-stage silicosis in workers exposed to silica dust [246]. LOC tech­
nology also plays a critical role in pathogen monitoring, with applications
ranging from the detection of human papillomaviruses (HPV) to bacteria
such as Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Escherichia
coli [247–249]. The integration of LOC with electrochemical detection has
further expanded its utility, particularly in the development of wearable
devices. For example, flexible skin-wearable LOC devices can analyze
biomarkers such as lactate and glucose in sweat, transmitting data wire­
lessly to mobile devices. Real-time monitoring of sweat glucose levels of­
fers new possibilities for non-invasive dynamic monitoring and manage­
ment of diabetes [250,251].

technology are expected to revolutionize the management of chronic

4.2.3. Comprehensive multi-omics analysis
LOC can simultaneously detect thousands of gene expressions, pro­
teins, or metabolites, enabling researchers to quickly obtain large-scale
biological information essential for discovering potential biomarkers,
disease mechanisms, and therapeutic targets. The innovative tech­
nology of single-cell omics analysis based on LOC offers high-
throughput, high sensitivity, and low cost. This empowers the ex­
ploration of cellular heterogeneity and provides distinct insights into
cellular diversity across different levels of the molecular biology fra­
mework [254].
In general, the convergence of LOC platforms with single-cell multi-
omics analytics has revolutionized high-content biomolecular profiling
through three fundamental technological advancements. It includes mi­
croscale fluidic manipulation enabling deterministic cell capture, modular
reaction chamber arrays permitting parallelized molecular processing, and
integrated detection modalities supporting multiplexed readouts [255].
The integration of multi-omics information can provide more complex
biomedical information. For instance, Xu et al. successfully integrated
transcripts acquired through high-throughput sequencing with proteins
identified via fluorescence immunoassay, encoding the physical cell lo­
cation within a digital sequencing space [201]. Similarly, Lamanna et al.
combined digital LOC, laser cell lysis, and AI-powered image processing to
examine single-cell genomes, transcriptomes, and proteomes within a
heterogeneous population, achieving detection characteristics related to
single nucleotide variations [256]. Using a comprehensive analysis of
transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics, Danoy et al. revealed the
differentiation pattern of liver cells and explored the complex mechanism
of liver cell regeneration [257].
By combining the high-throughput data acquisition capabilities of
LOC with the integrated advantages of multi-omics analysis, re­
searchers can better understand the relationships between genes, the
environment, and phenotypes, leading to enhanced insights into the
origins and development of diseases.

4.3. The challenges of commercialization and promotion
The main biotechnological fields poised to benefit commercially from
LOC technologies are gene sequencing and POCT. The rising demand for
rapid virus nucleic acid testing due to infectious diseases has driven the
development of mature and representative LOC applications [258]. In the
past two decades, there have been numerous scholarly publications on
LOC concept validation, however, the commercial promotion and appli­
cation of this technology have been hindered to some extent [259]. The
fundamental barriers to commercialization may be attributed to three
main aspects: the complexity of manufacturing, the lack of standardized
quality control protocols for clinical-grade production, and the fragmen­
tation of the supply chain in sourcing microfluidic components.
To facilitate commercialization, it is crucial to optimize LOC per­
formance, improve the standardization and integration of LOC pro­
ducts, and simultaneously lower manufacturing costs [260]. Enhancing
the versatility of fluidic devices through expanded interoperability with
varied substrates, reaction parameters, and operational environments
constitutes a critical research frontier. Contemporary strategies devel­
oped by academic and industrial entities to achieve reconfigurable
fluidic architectures demonstrate significant progress in addressing
these multifaceted engineering requirements [261]. Zhou et al. [262]
reported a microparticle manipulation platform designed for LOC,
which utilizes friction-driven dielectric electrophoresis, making it safer
and more portable for commercial-scale production. Nguyen et al.
[263] improved the microcolumn structure of tissue microfluidic chip
channels, where the optimized circular microcolumns showed excellent
barrier effects for cell seeding. Moreover, manufacturing cylindrical
shapes is faster and easier than traditional hexagons. These efforts will
aid manufacturers in creating market-competitive products, success­
fully launching them into the market, achieving favourable returns on
investment, and ultimately enabling high-performance diagnostic so­
lutions to be widely applied to solve practical clinical problems.
5. Conclusions
In summary, the LOC has made significant strides in the realms of
precision medicine and high-throughput biomolecular analysis over the
past two decades, driven by advancements in manufacturing materials
and innovative process technologies. Strengthening validation and ap­
plication research of LOC technology in clinical settings will promote its
transition from laboratory to practical clinical applications. These ad­
vancements will facilitate large-scale standardization and commercia­
lization of LOC, thereby expanding its applications in healthcare, early
disease screening, targeted therapies, and prognosis monitoring. By
continuously addressing challenges and exploring new development
directions, chip laboratory technology will play an increasingly vital
role in precision medicine and high-throughput molecular analysis.
