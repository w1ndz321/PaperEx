ABSTRACT
Single-atom materials with well-defined microstructures offer unique opportunities for revealing electromagnetic energy
dissipation mechanisms. However, research on the optimization of local electronic states to achieve dielectric–magnetic
collaborative losses remains rare. Herein, a dipole–spin synergistic regulation was realized in cobalt single-atom (Co-SA) absorbers
through atomic-scale coordination engineering. Experimental and theoretical analyses revealed that asymmetric coordination
facilitates enhanced dipole polarization, thereby improving dielectric loss, while the low-spin to high-spin transition increases the
magnetic moment, resulting in strengthened magnetic loss. This dielectric–magnetic synergistic regulation constructs superior
atomic-level absorption centers, enabling outstanding electromagnetic wave absorption (EWA) with a minimum reflection loss of
−54.87 dB and an effective absorption bandwidth of 5.36 GHz. This work demonstrates a scalable approach for the precise design
and optimization of high-performance EWA materials and offers a new insight into the relationships between the single-atom
coordination environment and the EWA performance.

Introduction
High-performance electromagnetic wave absorption (EWA)
materials are regarded as functional materials of high strategic
relevance due to their crucial roles in safeguarding information
security and ensuring the stable operation of critical equipment
[1–5]. In essence, electromagnetic waves (EMWs) represent
an energy form generated by the coupling of electric and
magnetic fields and propagating through space [6, 7]. Therefore,
it is widely accepted that the synergistic interplay between
dielectric loss and magnetic loss constitutes the fundamental
mechanism for high-efficiency electromagnetic attenuation [8].
However, conventional EWA materials typically feature complex
multicomponent and multiscale systems, and current design
strategies remain largely dependent on simple combinations
of dielectric and magnetic constituents [9]. This limited
understanding of the intrinsic energy dissipation pathways
has obscured the underlying principles of electromagnetic
attenuation and fundamentally restricted further performance
enhancement.
Metal single atoms (SAs) implanted on nitrogen-doped carbon
matrices provide an ideal platform for elucidating the intrinsic
correlations between atomic-level structures and electromagnetic
properties, owing to their well-defined and tunable coordination
configurations [10, 11]. Regarding dielectric-loss mechanisms,

experimental and theoretical studies have demonstrated that the
interactions between metal SAs and neighboring N/C atoms
disrupt local electronic symmetry and generate abundant electric
dipoles, which give rise to dipole polarization responses in
the microwave range [12]. Increasing the density of SAs can
effectively enhance high-frequency dipolar polarization loss, yet
excessive loading readily induces cluster formation [13]. As
cluster size increases, additional and complex loss mechanisms
emerge, complicating the clarification of structure–property rela-
tionships [14]. In terms of magnetic-loss mechanisms, hybridiza-
tion between the d orbitals of magnetic SAs and p orbitals of
the N-doped carbon matrix produces more stable spin magnetic
moments compared with magnetic nanoparticles [15]. Enhancing
the spin magnetic moment can effectively increase magnetization
strength and boost natural-resonance loss in the low-frequency
region [16]. Nevertheless, most reported SAs adopt an M–N4 coor-
dination configuration with D4h symmetry, resulting in partially
symmetric dipole polarization that restricts further enhancement
of dielectric loss. Moreover, strong-field N ligands induce high
crystal field splitting energy, favoring d-electron pairing and
yielding a low spin magnetic moment [17]. Hampered by the lack
of strategies for the synergistic regulation of electric dipole and
spin magnetic moments, the intrinsic coupling between dielectric
and magnetic losses remains unresolved.
Fundamentally, both electric dipole moments and spin magnetic
moments in SAs systems lie in the local electronic structure of
the metal center. Specifically, the dipole moment arises from
the asymmetric charge distribution between the metal atom
and its neighboring ligands, and can be engineered by tuning
the electronegativity [18, 19], coordination number [20], and
geometric configuration [21, 22] of the coordinating atoms. The
spin magnetic moment is dictated by the number of unpaired
electrons in the d orbitals of the metal center, which is strongly
influenced by the ligand field strength of coordinated atoms
[23]. Therefore, rational engineering of the local coordination
environment offers a viable strategy for achieving the synergistic
regulation of dielectric and magnetic losses in SAs absorbers.
Herein, we propose an asymmetric coordination strategy to syn-
ergistically modulate the electrical dipole and spin states of cobalt
single-atom (Co-SA) absorbers, thereby enabling the coordinated
regulation of dielectric and magnetic losses. Specifically, axial
Cl ligands are introduced into the first coordination shell, while
planar S dopants are incorporated into the second coordination
shell of Co-N4 structure. This dual modulation breaks the dipole
symmetry and redistributes local charge, markedly enhancing
polarization loss. Meanwhile, the introduction of weak-field Cl
and S ligands reduces the crystal field splitting energy, increasing
the number of unpaired electrons in the Co d-orbitals (i.e.,
inducing a transition from a low-spin to a high-spin state),
thereby enhancing the magnetic loss. Benefiting from this dual
regulation, the Co-SA absorber exhibits exceptional EWA perfor-
mance across both the low-frequency C-band and high-frequency
Ku-band, with a minimum reflection loss (RLmin) of −54.87 dB
and an effective absorption bandwidth (EAB) of 5.36 GHz. This
work realizes atomic-scale optimization of localized electronic
states within SA absorbers and provides a new pathway for
elucidating the coupling mechanisms between dielectric and
magnetic losses.

Results
2.1
Synthesis and Morphology of Single-Atom Co
Absorbers
As illustrated in Figure 1a, the Co-SA absorber (ClCoSNC)
was synthesized via a salt-assisted strategy. Briefly, CoSO4,
dicyandiamide, glucose, and NaCl were dissolved in water and
freeze-dried to ensure homogeneous dispersion of Co ions and
organic precursors within the NaCl matrix. The obtained solid
was subsequently annealed at 900◦C under an argon atmosphere.
During this process, glucose was carbonized to form intercon-
nected graphene frameworks among the salt crystals, affording
a 3D Co-SA absorber/NaCl composite. The thermal expansion
of NaCl facilitated nanosheet intergrowth at particle boundaries,
while the confinement effect of the salt domains enhanced carbon
yield [24]. Once the annealing temperature surpassed the melting
point of NaCl (800.7◦C), molten NaCl promoted the coordination
of Cl with Co atoms. Simultaneously, the thermal decomposition
of CoSO4 released sulfur species, which were incorporated into
the carbon framework and established long-range coordination
with Co-SA. Subsequent acid etching removed the NaCl template,
yielding a 3D honeycomb-like architecture. A detailed syn-
thetic protocol is provided in the Experimental Section. Notably,
this synthesis process can be proportionally scaled up (Figure
S1), indicating its potential for practical fabrication of EMW
absorbers.
On this basis, we propose a design concept of electronic structure
and spin-state modulation through atomic-level coordination, as
schematically illustrated in Figure 1b. By introducing an axial
Cl ligand and in-plane S dopants around the Co-SA center, a
dual weak-field hetero-coordination environment is established,
giving rise to an asymmetric local coordination structure. This
tailored configuration enables the synergistic engineering of both
dipole enhancement and spin-state modulation at the same
atomic site, thereby overcoming the inherent limitations of the
conventional symmetric M–N4 motif and constructing a highly
efficient SA absorber with simultaneously strengthened dielectric
and magnetic loss capabilities.
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron
microscopy (TEM) images (Figure 1c; Figures S2–S6) reveal that
the sample possesses reveal a 3D honeycomb-like morphologies
with interconnected open pores. This 3D honeycomb framework
provides multiple scattering pathways and prolonged propaga-
tion lengths for EMWs, while heteroatom doping introduces
abundant defect sites and dipolar responses into the carbon
matrix [25]. High-resolution high-angle annular dark-field scan-
ning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF-STEM) further
shows numerous isolated bright spots (∼0.2–0.3 nm) uniformly
distributed across the carbon matrix (Figure 1d), indicating
the successful anchoring of Co-SA on the designed N-doped
carbon matrix. This result is consistent with the characterization
obtained from powder X-ray diffraction (XRD, Figure S7) and
Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR, Figure S8). More-
over, elemental mapping by energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy
(EDS, Figure 1e) further confirms the effective incorporation of N,
S, and Cl elements into the carbon matrix combined with isolated
Co atoms. These results not only validate the proposed design

2.2
Structure Characterization and Simulation
Microstructure and surface chemical properties of the Co-SA
absorbers were systematically investigated. The local coordina-
tion structures of Co species were confirmed by X-ray absorption
near-edge spectroscopy (XANES). As shown in Figure 2a, the
absorption edges of CoNC, ClCoNC, and ClCoSNC lie between
those of CoO and Co foil, indicating that the oxidation state
of the isolated Co atoms falls between Co0 and Co2+. Fourier
transform extended X-ray absorption fine structure (FT-EXAFS)
spectra (Figure 2b) reveal that both ClCoNC and ClCoSNC exhibit
a shifted and asymmetric Co–N4 coordination peak at ∼1.33 Å,
assignable to axial Co–Cl coordination, causing the Co center
to deviate from the graphene plane [24]. Notably, no Co–Co
scattering signal is detected in any sample, ruling out metallic
aggregation and confirming the isolated single-atom nature of Co
species, in agreement with STEM observations [23]. Moreover, the
coordination environment of ClCoSNC resolved via quantitative
EXAFS curve fitting (Figure 2c; Figures S9 and S10, Table S1)
reveals an additional peak at approximately 2.9 Å, which can
be attributed to the Co–S interaction, indicating that sulfur
atoms have entered the second coordination shell of cobalt. WT-
EXAFS analysis further resolves the backscattering paths in both
R-space and K-space, unambiguously corroborating the above
coordination assignments (Figure 2d–i).
Raman spectroscopy revealed that the ID/IG ratio of the samples
increased from 3.19 to 3.96 with the introduction of more het-

parameters, including the average defect distance (LD) and
defect density (nD), were further calculated using Equations S1
and S2 (Figure S11) [26, 27]. The incorporation of heteroatoms
decreased LD and increased nD, indicating enhanced structural
disorder and a higher density of defects. Such abundant defect

the work functions (Ф) (Figure 3c). The pristine NC exhibits the
highest Ф (5.08 eV), corresponding to the largest energy barrier for

that stepwise modulation by Co centers and heteroatom regu-

markedly enhances the electron donor capability. This synergistic
regulation reconstructs the local electron cloud distribution and
induces a downward vacuum-level shift via interfacial dipole
polarization, as illustration schematically in Figure 3d. From
an electromagnetic perspective, the reduced Ф is expected to
promote interfacial charge transfer and polarization, thereby
potentially enhancing dipolar polarization and conductive loss
under an alternating electromagnetic field [31].

with a vibrating sample magnetometer, aiming to clarify the
influence of electronic and spin regulation on magnetic response.

coordination environment and influences magnetic anisotropy
and spin–orbit coupling [23, 34]. Notably, the combination of high

reversal, promoting magnetic relaxation and resonance processes
and thereby enhancing magnetic loss [29, 35]. By measuring
the temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibility under zero-
field cooling (ZFC) conditions (Figure 3f), the high-spin behavior

S3–S6) [36, 37]. These results demonstrate that the synergistic
effect of axial Cl and in-plane S ligands results in a higher spin
state in ClCoSNC. This higher spin state facilitates magnetic
dipole transitions and relaxation processes, thereby promoting
natural resonance, exchange resonance, and eddy current loss,
which is the primary origin of its enhanced magnetic loss
capability [38].
To elucidate the intrinsic coupling between polarization pro-
cesses and spin states in Co-SA absorbers, the electronic struc-
tures were analyzed through density functional theory (DFT)
calculations (Figure 3 g; Figures S15 and S16). In CoNC, the den-
sity of states (DOS) near the Fermi level (Ef) is sparse, indicative of
weak electronic activity and limited polarization capacity. Upon
axial Cl coordination, the DOS near Ef increases substantially,
accompanied by pronounced orbital hybridization peaks. This
redistribution of electronic density enhances carrier mobility
and local charge fluctuation, thereby strengthening polarization
processes [3, 35]. Subsequent in-plane S doping further elevates
the DOS intensity and strengthens orbital hybridization, while
simultaneously inducing a clear asymmetry between spin-up
and spin-down channels, reflecting enhanced spin splitting and
spin polarization. These findings demonstrate that modulation

2.3
Electromagnetic Performance Analysis
The electromagnetic parameters were systematically measured
to clarify the regulatory role of polarization–spin coupling in
governing EMWs attenuation. The real part of the complex
permittivity (ε’) reflects electric energy storage under an alter-
nating electric field, while the imaginary part (εʺ) represents
dielectric energy dissipation [39, 40]. As shown in Figure 4a,b,
and Figure S17, both ε’ and εʺ values of ClCoSNC are higher than
those of ClCoNC and CoNC across 2–18 GHz, indicating that
the synergistic incorporation of Cl and S increases the energy
storage ability of the carbon matrix and enhances its energy
dissipation. This dual enhancement is crucial for achieving
efficient EMWs attenuation [41]. In order to disentangle the
dielectric loss mechanisms, ε″ was separated into the polarization
loss term (εp″, Figure 4c) and the conductive loss term (εc″, Figure
S18). ClCoSNC exhibits the largest εp″ values across the entire
frequency range, reflecting its stronger polarization relaxation
capability. This enhancement can be ascribed to the deliberately
engineered local coordination structure: axial Cl coordination
and in-plane S doping synergistically introduce abundant dipolar
polarization centers into the carbon matrix. Meanwhile, the
incorporation of heteroatoms breaks the local electronic symme-
try and generates numerous defect sites, thereby strengthening
defect-induced polarization [42]. These findings, consistent with
the electronic structure analyses, demonstrate that tuning the
electronic states and coordination environment of Co-SA is an
effective route to maximize dipolar responses and dielectric loss,
thereby providing a microscopic origin for the superior EWA per-
formance of ClCoSNC. The dielectric loss tangent (tan δε = ε″/ε′)
exhibits a similar trend to ε″, with ClCoSNC consistently showing
the highest values, highlighting its superior dielectric loss capa-
bility (Figure 4c; Figure S19) [43]. In particular, its pronounced
increase above 10 GHz highlights the critical role of synergistic
heteroatom regulation in strengthening dipolar polarization and
enabling multiple relaxation pathways under alternating elec-
tric fields, thereby conferring strong high-frequency attenuation
capability.
According to Debye theory, the semicircles and linear tails in
the Cole–Cole plots correspond to polarization relaxation and
conduction loss, respectively [44]. As shown in Figure 4d and
Figure S20, ClCoSNC presents the largest number of relaxation
semicircles at 6.96, 9.12, 11.04, and 16.88 GHz, underscoring the
presence of multiple polarization relaxation processes. Mean-
while, the linear tails observed in all samples verify conduction
losses caused by electron migration within the carbon framework.
Differential charge density analysis (Figure 4e) elucidates the
electron redistribution induced by structural modulation at the
microscopic level. In ClCoNC, the theoretically uniform charge
distribution is already perturbed, leading to localized charge
accumulation and depletion. In contrast, ClCoSNC exhibits an
even more intensified non-uniformity, characterized by stronger
and more spatially extended charge-redistribution patterns. This
delocalization enhances local charge distortion, which promotes
the generation of dynamic dipole moments under alternating
fields. These findings, in conjunction with the XANES/EXAFS
results and DFT analyses, enable a comprehensive understanding
of the dielectric loss mechanisms in ClCoSNC: (i) conduction
loss, arising from enhanced charge transport pathways facilitated
by heteroatom doping; (ii) dipolar polarization, induced by axial
Co–Cl coordination and in-plane Co–S doping that introduce
abundant dipolar centers; (iii) interfacial polarization, associ-
ated with charge accumulation at heterogeneous interfaces; and
(iv) multiple scattering and reflection, originating from the
hierarchical porous framework that prolongs EMWs propagation.
The convergence of these mechanisms confirms that atomic-
level coordination engineering translates directly into tailored
dielectric responses, thereby establishing a robust microscopic-
to-macroscopic correlation for achieving efficient and broadband
EMWs attenuation.
Apart from dielectric loss, the attenuation ability of absorbers
is also strongly influenced by magnetic loss induced by high-
spin states [45]. To evaluate the contribution of magnetic
loss in Co-SA absorbers, the complex permeability (µr = µ′
– jµ″) was analyzed, where µ′ and µ″ represent the storage
and dissipation of magnetic energy, respectively (Figure 4f;
Figure S21) [46, 47]. Compared with other samples, ClCoSNC
exhibits higher µ′ and µ″ across most of the frequency range,
indicating both stronger magnetic energy storage and more
pronounced magnetic energy dissipation [48]. Magnetic loss
generally arises from domain wall motion, hysteresis loss, natural
resonance, eddy current loss, and exchange resonance [38].
However, in the GHz range, the response rate of domain walls
cannot match the oscillation frequency of EMWs, rendering
domain-wall resonance negligible. Moreover, under weak mag-
netic fields, the energy for domain reversal is insufficient to
form a distinct hysteresis loop, and hysteresis loss is therefore
negligible.
Therefore, within 2–18 GHz, magnetic loss mainly originates
from natural resonance, exchange resonance, and eddy current
loss, which can be assessed by the eddy current coefficient (C0)
(Figure 4g). In the low-frequency region (2–10 GHz), obvious
fluctuations in C0 indicate that natural resonance dominates.
At intermediate frequencies (10–15 GHz), the relatively small
fluctuations suggest that exchange resonance plays the leading
role. Moreover, at high frequencies (15–18 GHz), C0 exhibits
very limited variation, with some regions remaining constant,
implying that magnetic loss is attributed to exchange reso-
nance combined with eddy current loss. Consistent with the
permeability results, the C0 values of ClCoSNC are almost
always higher than those of ClCoNC and CoNC, confirming
its superior magnetic loss capability. The magnetic loss tangent
(tan δµ) further reveals the frequency dependence of magnetic
loss (Figure 4 h; Figure S22), where ClCoSNC shows stronger
magnetic loss in the low-frequency region, a feature that is
critical for achieving broadband EWA. This phenomenon can be
rationalized by the natural resonance frequency (Equation S7):
a higher spin state enhances the effective anisotropy constant
(Keff), while the combination of moderate Ms and low Hc

2.4
Electromagnetic Wave Absorption
Application Investigation
The reflection loss (RL) values of Co-SA absorbers were derived
from the measured electromagnetic parameters according to
the transmission line theory (Equations S8–S15) to assess their
practical EWA performance (Figure 5a–d; Figures S24 and S25).
In the frequency range of 2–18 GHz, the absorption proper-
ties of NC, CoNC, ClCoNC, and ClCoSNC were systematically
investigated at matching thicknesses of 1.0–5.0 mm. Owing
to its inherently low polarizability and magnetic permeabil-
ity, NC exhibited the poorest EWA capability (Figure S26)
[43, 49]. In contrast, the other samples demonstrated remarkable
improvements, attributed to multiple attenuation mechanisms.
Specifically, CoNC achieved an RLmin of −23.14 dB at a thickness
of 2.9 mm, along with an EAB of 4.4 GHz at 2.0 mm. Upon the
introduction of axial Cl coordination, ClCoNC exhibited superior
performance to CoNC, reaching an RLmin of −46.06 dB at 3.3 mm
and an EAB of 5.26 GHz at 2.0 mm.

with the quarter-wavelength theory and impedance matching.
According to the quarter-wavelength theory, the absorption peak
frequency is inversely proportional to the coating thickness
(Equation S13). Experimental and simulated results demonstrate
a high degree of consistency in the variation of matching thick-
ness (tm), confirming the critical role of destructive interference
in EMWs attenuation (Figure 5e). Meanwhile, the impedance
matching characteristics were further verified (Figure 5f; Figure
S27): in the Smith chart, curves closer to the center indicate
better impedance matching, while shorter trajectories imply more
consistent impedance over a broader frequency range [41, 46]. It
is evident that the impedance trajectory of ClCoSNC lies much
nearer to the center, reflecting its superior matching capability to
incident EMWs. This phenomenon can be attributed to the syn-
ergistic effect of enhanced polarization and elevated spin states,
which brings εr and µr into a more balanced magnitude, thereby
reducing the difference between the input impedance and free-
space impedance, thus further improving impedance matching
[50]. Taken together, ClCoSNC, with the coupling of multiple
polarization effects and high-spin states, achieved exceptional
EWA performance, surpassing NC, CoNC, and ClCoNC, as well as
most previously reported SA and magnetic absorbers (Figure 5g;
Table S2) [51–56].
To further evaluate the engineering applicability of Co-SA
absorbers, radar cross-section (RCS) simulations were carried
out using CST Studio Suite 2024 under open boundary con-
ditions [57, 58]. A model was constructed by coating Co-
SA absorbers onto a rectangular perfect electric conductor
(PEC) plate (200 × 200 mm). The coating thickness was
selected at the value corresponding to the RLmin, and fre-
quency sweeps were performed over 2–18 GHz. The simu-
lated results are consistent with the absorption performance
(Figure 5h), showing that the coated plates exhibit sub-
stantially reduced electromagnetic scattering. Moreover, the
suppression becomes more pronounced with stronger cou-
pling between polarization effects and high-spin states. CoNC,
ClCoNC, and ClCoSNC achieved maximum RCS reductions
of 25.67, 30.84, and 38.15 dB m2, respectively. In addition,
power loss density and the distributions of the electric and
magnetic fields (Figure 5i) indicate that energy dissipation is
mainly concentrated in the ClCoSNC model, in line with its
stronger dielectric and magnetic loss capabilities. These results
demonstrate that ClCoSNC possesses superior radar scatter-
ing suppression, effectively reducing the scattering of incident
EMWs on PEC surfaces and showing promise as an advanced
electromagnetic stealth coating. Overall, the findings confirm
that the coupling of polarization effects with high-spin states
can effectively enhance the EMW attenuation capability of
absorbers.
3
Conclusions
In summary, we develop a Co-SA absorber with an asymmet-
ric coordination configuration that bridges atomic-scale elec-
tronic regulation with macroscopic electromagnetic responses.
Through dual weak-field asymmetric coordination, the local
electronic symmetry and ligand-field strength of Co centers are
concurrently modulated, enabling the simultaneous enhance-
ment of dipole polarization and spin magnetic moments. The
resulting polarization−spin coupling establishes atomic-level
dielectric−magnetic synergy, leading to outstanding EWA per-
formance across the C and Ku bands (RLmin = −54.87 dB,
EAB = 5.36 GHz). This study provides new theoretical guid-
ance and experimental evidence at the atomic and electronic
level for understanding and designing high-performance EWA
materials.

4.1
Raw Materials
The chemicals were obtained from Macklin, including sulfuric
acid (H2SO4, 98 wt%), glucose (C6H12O6), sodium chloride (NaCl),
cobalt chloride hexahydrate (CoCl2⋅6H2O), cobalt sulfate hep-

sodium sulfate (Na2SO4), and dicyandiamide (C2H4N4, DCDA).
All reagents were used as received, without further purification.
4.2
Preparation
In the preparation, 420 g of NaCl, 42 g of glucose, 3.9354 g of
CoSO4⋅7H2O, and 46.48 g of DCDA were first dissolved in 4.2
L of deionized water under continuous stirring for 2 h. The
powder was obtained after freeze-drying of the above solution
under vacuum. Finally, the as-obtained powder was pyrolyzed
at 900◦C under a flowing argon gas for 2 h, followed by
acid etching treatment with 0.5 M H2SO4 to remove unstable
Co species and NaCl. The obtained black powder was dried
at 60◦C, and the sample was designated as ClCoSNC. Other
samples were prepared using different precursors of Co salts.
ClCoNC was synthesized in the presence of CoCl2⋅6H2O. NC
was also synthesized similarly without CoCl2⋅6H2O. CoNC was
prepared by pyrolyzing a mixture of CoPc and NC (Co con-
tent is approximately 2 wt.%) at 450◦C under flowing argon
for 1 h.
