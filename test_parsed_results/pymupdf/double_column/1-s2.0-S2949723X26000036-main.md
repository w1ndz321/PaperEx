A B S T R A C T
Currently, nanoparticles (NPs) still face challenges in achieving efficient tumor targeted delivery, especially in
balancing in vivo distribution and cellular uptake efficiency. A recent study published in Advanced Functional
Materials utilized DNA barcoding combined with high-throughput in vivo screening to systematically evaluate
the targeting of gold NPs of different shapes and sizes in tumors. The results showed that large-sized triangular
gold NPs exhibited the highest cellular uptake efficiency in vitro, and achieved significant tumor enrichment and
excellent siRNA delivery in vivo through surface modification of RGD peptides. Further photothermal experi­
ments showed that triangular particles can rapidly raise the temperature to 57 ◦C in the tumor area, achieving
effective tumor ablation. This study indicates that large triangular gold NPs possess advantages in cellular up­
take, in vivo enrichment ability, and photothermal therapy potential, providing a new strategy for precise tumor
targeted therapy. The study highlights that nanoparticle morphology plays a dominant role in governing in vivo
tumor accumulation and therapeutic outcomes.
Gold NPs have shown outstanding potential in drug delivery, pho­
tothermal therapy (PTT), and molecular diagnostics in the field of can­
cer treatment due to their excellent biocompatibility, surface
modification flexibility, and photothermal response performance [1].
This multifunctionality stems from their controllable size and
morphology, which brings significant surface plasmon resonance effects
and superior pharmacokinetic properties, such as prolonging in vivo
retention time, enhancing stability, and promoting tumor area enrich­
ment [2]. Different morphologies of gold NPs exhibit heterogeneous
cellular uptake efficiency, which may be related to differences in their
mediated endocytosis mechanisms [3]. However, most current research
still remains at the in vitro level, and even with animal experiments, the
scale is limited and lacks systematicity. In vitro screening cannot truly
reflect the distribution and targeting characteristics of gold NPs in vivo,
and may overlook potential effective topological structures (see Fig. 1).
Recently, Andy Tay's team [4] published a study titled “In Vivo
Screening of Barcoded Gold NPs Elucidates the Influence of Shapes for
Tumor Targeting” in Advanced Functional Materials, proposing inno­
vative solutions to address this issue. This study combines DNA barcode
technology [5] with high-throughput in vivo screening methods to
simultaneously evaluate the distribution and tumor targeting of multiple
gold NPs in the same animal for the first time. The research team syn­
thesized six commonly used gold NPs (spherical, rod-shaped, triangular,
40 nm and 80 nm), stabilized with PEG-5K, and functionalized the DNA
barcode on the particle surface through Au thiol bonds to achieve stable
connection. Each type of NP was administered separately in vitro (to
cells) and in vivo (to tumor-bearing mice), and their cellular uptake and
tumor enrichment were quantitatively analyzed using complementary
sequencing-, imaging-, and function-based validation approaches.
DNA barcode utilizes the binary encoding characteristics of the four
bases (A, T, C, G) of DNA to enable specific sequences to carry digital
information and be read through sequencing technology, thus becoming
a high-density quaternary carrier for information storage and labeling
[6].

tative comparison of multiple nanoparticle formulations in vivo within
the same biological system. In addition, compared with traditional
methods such as fluorescence imaging and electron microscopy, DNA
barcode has the advantages of extremely sensitive detection threshold
and easy in vivo exploration [7]. Compared with direct adsorption
methods, grafting DNA barcodes through Au thiol bonding has higher
binding energy and chemical stability, which can avoid conformational
changes and particle aggregation caused by base adsorption [8]. The
author employed this strategy to firmly functionalize DNA barcodes on
the surface of gold NPs, and verified the stability, localization charac­
teristics, and signal traceability of the barcodes through confocal laser
scanning microscopy (CLSM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM),
and NGS. This strategy enables a direct, quantitative comparison of
multiple nanoparticle formulations within the same biological context,
thereby reducing inter-animal variability and enhancing the robustness
of in vivo screening.
In vitro experiments, the authors found that large-sized triangular
gold NPs exhibited the highest cellular uptake efficiency, which con­
tradicts the conventional belief that “small-sized particles are more
likely to penetrate cell membranes”. Studies have shown that larger
sized NPs, due to their larger contact area with the cell membrane, can
promote the membrane encapsulation process, thereby improving the
efficiency of cellular internalization [9]. Nanoparticle shape influences
membrane wrapping dynamics and endocytic pathways, thereby
modulating cellular uptake efficiency. In in vivo screening, particle
shape rather than size plays a major role in tumor targeting. Spherical
gold NPs have the highest enrichment in tumors, followed by triangular
ones, while rod-shaped NPs showed the poorest tumor enrichment. This
is in sharp contrast to the result of the lowest uptake efficiency of
spherical NPs in vitro, indicating the dominant effect of shape in the
biological distribution in vivo. The author points out that this phe­
nomenon can be attributed to three main reasons: firstly, spherical
particles require lower membrane bending energy and are more easily
enveloped and penetrated by endothelial cells compared to rod-shaped
particles [10]; Secondly, spherical particles have a lower phagocytic
clearance rate by macrophages in the body, thereby prolonging circu­
lation time and increasing the probability of tumor arrival; Finally,
PEG5K modification into spherical particles provides better suspension
stability and stealth effect, further promoting their blood circulation and
tumor enrichment [11]. The inconsistency between in vitro cell uptake
and in vivo tumor accumulation highlights that the performance of
nanoparticles cannot be reliably predicted solely through in vitro ex­
periments, emphasizing the need for design strategies guided by in vivo
biological behavior.
Although in vivo experiments have shown that spherical gold NPs are
most enriched in tumors, there have been extensive studies on this topic.
The authors are more inclined to validate the well performing but less
reported rod-shaped and triangular NPs to explore their potential
application value. Through ICP-OES, the results showed that the accu­
mulation of triangular NPs in both liver and tumor was significantly
higher than that of rod-shaped NPs, consistent with NGS results. Sub­
sequently, triangular NP and rod-shaped NP were loaded onto siLuc and
applied to 4T1 luc tumor mouse models. The results showed that siRNA
delivered by triangular NP had a significantly better knockdown effect
on luciferase expression than rod-shaped NP, further confirming its
functional advantage brought about by high tumor accumulation.
Considering the excellent photothermal conversion ability of gold NPs,
selective heating and cell killing of tumor tissues can be achieved through
local illumination [12]. The author used 808-nm laser to irradiate gold
nanoparticles of different shapes and evaluate their photothermal con­
version efficiency. Under comparable irradiation conditions, the temper­
ature elevation was monitored to evaluate morphology-dependent
photothermal performance. The results showed that under the same
shape, gold NPs of different sizes exhibited similar photothermal prop­
erties; In terms of shape comparison, the photothermal efficiency of
rod-shaped and triangular NPs is superior to that of spherical NPs. Due to
the higher enrichment level of triangular NPs in tumors, the tumor tem­
perature of mice treated with triangular NPs rose to 57 ◦C within 6 mi­
nutes, which is higher than the minimum temperature of 50 ◦C required
for efficient tumor ablation according to previous research [13]. Based on
the results of in vitro experiments, the author points out that the large
triangular gold NP has both good cell uptake ability and in vivo enrich­
ment advantages in achieving efficient tumor photothermal therapy,
demonstrating its potential application value in tumor targeted photo­
thermal therapy.

To improve tumor targeting and enhance drug delivery efficiency,
researchers often introduce targeting ligands on the surface of NPs,
thereby achieving active targeting and enhancing drug delivery effi­
ciency. Among them, RGD peptides can specifically recognize the
overexpression of αvβ3 and αvβ5 integrins in various solid tumors [14].
Based on this principle, the author modified RGD peptides on the surface
of the excellent performing large triangular gold NPs to enhance their
binding and internalization with cancer cells, thereby improving tumor
accumulation and therapeutic efficacy. The experimental results showed
that the enrichment of RGD modified large triangular gold NPs in tumors
was significantly higher than that of unmodified large triangular gold
NPs, and the distribution in the liver was reduced, indicating that this
strategy can optimize the tumor targeting of NPs. More broadly, this
study reinforces the notion that nanoparticle morphology should be
considered a systems-level design parameter, whose effects emerge only
when evaluated within complex in vivo environments.
Overall, this study not only demonstrates the feasibility of DNA
barcoding systems in evaluating the tumor-targeting performance of
gold NPs of different shapes and sizes, but also emphasizes the impor­
tance of shape, surface modification, and synergistic effects of targeting
ligands. In the future, by expanding the nanoparticle library and intro­
ducing more tumor specific modifications, the in vivo distribution and
therapeutic effect of nanotherapy carriers can be further optimized,
providing new strategies for precision tumor treatment. However, the
extent to which these findings can be generalized across different tumor
types and microenvironments remains to be further explored. While
DNA barcoding methods are highly effective for comparative screening,
they may not fully capture the dynamic cell-particle interactions within
the body.
