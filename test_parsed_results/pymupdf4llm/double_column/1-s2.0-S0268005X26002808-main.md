## **1. Introduction** 

Pectin, a complex polysaccharide predominantly found in the primary cell walls and middle lamellae of higher plants, is well known for its gelling, stabilizing, and thickening properties (Reichembach & de Oliveira Petkowicz, 2021; Zhang et al., 2025). It consists of three main domains: homogalacturonan (HG), rhamnogalacturonan I (RGI), and rhamnogalacturonan II (RGII) (Chandel et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024). Among these, RGI is particularly notable for its repeating disaccharide backbone [→2)-α-L-Rhap-(1 → 4)-α-D-GalpA-(1→], which are heavily substituted with neutral sugar side chains such as arabinan, galactan, and arabinogalactan (Cui et al., 2024). RGI is widely distributed in plant materials, particularly in citrus processing by-products, making it an attractive resource for industrial utilization (Chandel et al., 2022; Niu et al., 2024). Its unique structure also underpins its superior bioactivity and functionality, which have drawn increasing interest for use in food, pharmaceutical, and cosmetic industries (Chen et al., 2024; Jing et al., 

## 2024; Wang et al., 2024). 

Despite its significant presence in nature, the industrial application of RGI pectin remains constrained, primarily due to its poor solubility after drying (del Amo-Mateos et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025). The poor solubility of RGI pectin is primarily attributed to thermal aggregation. During dehydration—particularly following ethanol precipitation—RGI molecules tend to form dense macromolecular assemblies due to their low charge density and flexible side chains, which favor extensive hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions (An et al., 2026; Bu et al., 2022; Lan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020, 2025). These aggregated structures exhibit limited solubility in aqueous solutions, limiting the applicability of RGI pectin in food and pharmaceutical systems where solubility is essential for availability (Assifaoui et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023). 

Our previous studies have attempted to improve the solubility of RGI-rich pectin by blending it with other negatively charged polysaccharides (Xu et al., 2025). This strategy introduces steric hindrance

and electrostatic repulsion to suppress molecular aggregation. However, such modifications inevitably alter the chemical composition and compromise the purity of RGI, which may be problematic in applications requiring unmodified, naturally sourced pectin. 

A simpler and more industrially viable alternative is to control the aggregation behavior of RGI by adjusting the precipitation pH (PpH), defined as the pH of the extract prior to ethanol addition (Kalapathy & Proctor, 2001). In conventional pectin extraction, the extract is usually acidic and is neutralized before precipitation. Because RGI is an anionic polysaccharide, its degree of ionization and inter-chain associations are highly responsive to pH, particularly in the low-dielectric environment induced by ethanol (Pang et al., 2024). Previous studies on HG and sugar beet pectins suggest that PpH can affect molecular conformation, hydration, and aggregation during drying (Fu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2016, 2017; Yang et al., 2018, 2025), and anecdotal observations in citrus-based RGI preparations also imply that PpH influences solubility and particle morphology (Hu et al., 2021). However, systematic mechanistic understanding of how PpH regulates RGI aggregation remains limited, particularly regarding the roles of hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interactions, and molecular packing. It can therefore be hypothesized that pH conditions during processing, especially the ethanol precipitation step prior to drying, play a critical role in determining the aggregation state and solubility of RGI-rich pectin. 

This study aims to systematically investigate the influence of PpH (17) on the thermal aggregation and solubility behavior of RGI-rich pectin extracted from citrus processing by-products. Comprehensive analyses, including molecular weight distribution, microstructure, and assessment of intermolecular interactions, were performed using techniques such as Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), and zeta potential measurement. This work not only advances understanding of RGI aggregation mechanisms but also proposes a process-oriented strategy to improve its functional solubility without chemical modification. These insights contribute to the effective utilization of citrus processing residues and support the development of RGI-based ingredients in water-based food systems. 

## **2. Materials and methods** 

## _2.1. Materials_ 

Mandarin citrus peel ( _Citrus unshiu Marc._ ) was obtained from a local citrus canning factory. (Zhejiang, China). The peel was treated with boiling water blanching and bleaching enzyme, then dried in an oven at 60[◦] C for 48 h. Dried citrus peel was ground and stored in desiccators for subsequent use. 

Carbohydrate standards, including fucose (Fuc), arabinose (Ara), rhamnose (Rha), glucose (Glc), mannose (Man), galactose (Gal) and galacturonic acid (GalA) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co. (St. Louis, Mo, USA). Trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), 3-methyl-1-phenyl-2-pyrazolin-5-one (PMP) and alcohol were sourced from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). All other reagents were of analytical grade and were obtained from commercial suppliers. 

## _2.2. Preparation and management of samples_ 

## _2.2.1. Preparation of RGI-rich pectin samples at different ethanol precipitation pH levels_ 

RGI-rich pectin was extracted from citrus peel powder using an acidassisted low-temperature method. Briefly, citrus peel powder was mixed with hydrochloric acid solution adjusted to the HCl solution (pH = 1) maintaining a solid-to-liquid ratio of 1:20. The suspension was stirred at 40[◦] C for 1.5 h and then cooled to room temperature. The extract was filtered through a 400-mesh filter cloth to remove insoluble materials, and the supernatant was collected. 

The obtained extract was divided into seven portions, and the pH of each portion was individually adjusted to 1–7 to induce ethanol 

precipitation. The resulting pectin samples were designated according to their precipitation pH (PpH = 1–7), and all procedures were conducted in triplicate. The mixture was left undisturbed for 2 h at room temperature, filtered through a 400-mesh cloth, washed once with absolute ethanol to remove residual soluble sugars and salts, and then dried in a hot air oven at 60[◦] C for 12 h. The dried pectin was ground into a fine powder using a high-speed tissue grinder for subsequent analyses. The pH of redissolved dispersions was measured at different concentrations (0.1–2.0%, w/v), and the values are summarized in Table S1. 

## _2.2.2. Preparation of interaction-modulated pectin samples_ 

The extraction procedure and conditions were the same as described above. After centrifugation and filtration to obtain the supernatant, sodium chloride (NaCl), urea, and guanidine hydrochloride (Gu-HCl) were added to the pectin extract to investigate the roles of electrostatic interactions, hydrogen bonding, and hydrophobic interactions, respectively. Specifically, NaCl was added at a concentration of 0.4% (w/v), while urea and Gu-HCl were each added at 1% (w/v), based on the volume of the pectin extract. The pH of each solution was then adjusted accordingly, followed by ethanol precipitation and subsequent processing as previously described. 

## _2.3. Determination of monosaccharide composition_ 

The monosaccharide composition of RGI-rich pectin was determined using acid hydrolysis followed by pre-column derivatization with 1- phenyl-3-methyl-5-pyrazolone (PMP), with slight modifications to (Pan et al., 2022). Briefly, pectin (2 mg) was hydrolyzed with 4 mol/L TFA at 110[◦] C for 8 h. After removal of TFA and re-dissolution, the hydrolysate was derivatized with PMP under alkaline conditions (70[◦] C, 30 min), neutralized, and extracted with chloroform to remove excess PMP. 

The monosaccharides were separated using a Waters e2695 HPLC system equipped with a UV detector (Waters 2489) and an Agilent XDBC18 column (4.6 mm × 250 mm, 5 μm) maintained at 25[◦] C. The mobile phase consisted of Solvent A (15% v/v acetonitrile in 0.05 mol/L phosphate buffer, pH 6.8) and Solvent B (40% v/v acetonitrile in 0.05 mol/L phosphate buffer, pH 6.8). The gradient elution program was as follows: 0–10 min, 0%–15% B; 10–30 min, 15%–25% B; 30–35 min, 25% B. The flow rate was set at 1.0 mL/min, and detection was performed at 250 nm with an injection volume of 10 μL. 

Monosaccharide contents were expressed as molar percentages (mol %). The relative peak areas of the PMP derivatives were corrected using response factors calculated from the mixed standard solution, enabling the quantitative determination of each monosaccharide's molar contribution to the total composition. 

## _2.4. Molecular weight determination_ 

The molecular weight and distribution of RGI-rich pectin samples were determined using size-exclusion chromatography coupled with multi-angle laser light scattering and refractive index detection (SECMALLS-RI). Pectin solutions were prepared at a concentration of 5 mg/ mL and filtered through 0.22 μm hydrophilic membranes prior to injection. 

The analysis was conducted on a Shimadzu LC-20 system equipped with a guard column (OHpak SB-G) and two size-exclusion chromatography columns (Shodex SB-806 HQ and SB-804 HQ) connected in series. A multi-angle light scattering detector (MALLS) and a differential refractive index detector (RID-20A) were used in tandem to measure the molecular parameters, including weight-average molecular weight (Mw), number-average molecular weight (Mn), and root-mean-square radius (Rg). 

The mobile phase consisted of 0.15 mol/L NaCl containing 0.02% (w/v) NaN3 to prevent microbial growth. The solution was filtered through a 0.22 μm membrane and degassed via ultrasonication for 20

## _2.5. Determination of solubility_ 

The conventional approach determines the insoluble rate by drying the precipitated insoluble matter and subsequently calculating the soluble rate. However, due to the water retention properties of pectin precipitation, we have optimized the existing formula with certain modifications and proposed a method for calculating solubility based on the solute content in the supernatant. The steps are as follows: 

A 0.5% (w/w) solution of samples was prepared in water at room temperature for 5 min under vortex mixing to determine solubility. The solution was centrifuged (7000 g, 10 min), and the solute content was determined by measuring the total solids dried at 105[◦] C in the supernatant. To verify whether solvent pH directly affects the measured solubility, a cross-dissolution control was conducted at 0.5% (w/v): PpH = 1 pectin was dissolved in deionized water and in NaHCO3 solution (pH 8.3), and PpH = 7 pectin was dissolved in deionized water and in HCl solution (pH 1.0). Centrifuged at 7000 g for 10 min, the resulting precipitate amounts after centrifugation were comparable for the same sample (Fig. S1).

## _2.6. Microscopic observation of the dissolution process_ 

The dissolution behavior of RGI-rich pectin was monitored using an optical imaging method adapted from previous studies, with modifications to ensure stable imaging during water addition (Xu et al., 2025). To — prevent disruptions caused by changes in focal length commonly encountered in conventional upright microscopy—an inverted microscope (DMi1, Leica, Germany) was employed. This setup allowed water to be introduced to the sample without altering the distance between the objective lens and the observation field. 

A small quantity of the pectin powder was evenly distributed on a microscope slide, and a drop of deionized water was added using a precision syringe. Real-time imaging was conducted at room temperature, with images captured at 1-s intervals for a total of 10 min. The observations were carried out using a 10 × objective lens in combination with a 10 × × . This eyepiece, providing a total magnification of 100 approach enabled detailed visualization of the dynamic dissolution – process and provided qualitative insights into water pectin interactions and structural changes upon hydration.

## _2.8. Bulk density_ 

Absolute density refers to the actual solid density of a material, excluding any interparticle voids, whereas bulk density includes the contribution of these voids. The sample powder was accurately weighed and gently poured into a graduated cylinder without any vibration. The loose bulk density was then calculated by dividing the mass of the powder by the volume it occupied in the cylinder (Yang et al., 2025). 

## _2.9. X-ray computed tomography (X-ray CT)_ 

The three-dimensional morphology of materials under various conditions can be effectively analyzed using X-ray computed tomography (CT), providing detailed insights into the internal structure and pore distribution without damaging the samples. High-resolution micro X-ray CT images were obtained using a SKYSCAN1272 scanner (Bruker, Germany), and porosity was calculated using ImageJ software. 

## _2.10. Field emission scanning electronic microscopy (FESEM)_ 

To evaluate the surface morphological structure, the pectin obtained after ethanol precipitation was directly redissolved in water (without oven drying), and the resulting solution was freeze-dried and subsequently sputter-coated with gold. Surface morphology was observed using a field emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM, ZEISS ULTRA Plus, Carl Zeiss, Germany). 

## _2.11. Rheological determination_ 

The steady flow measurements of samples were conducted using a controlled stress rheometer (MCR 302, Anton Paar, Austria) equipped with a parallel-plate geometry (CP50-1-SN32964) at 25[◦] C. Pectin dispersions were prepared at 2.0% (w/v). To improve repeatability and to minimize the influence of insoluble aggregates on flow measurements, the dispersions were centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min. The supernatants were carefully collected and allowed to stand overnight to release entrapped air and stabilize the soluble phase prior to rheological testing. No visible sediment was observed after centrifugation for samples at pH 1–3, whereas samples at pH 4–7 showed an insoluble sediment; therefore, viscosity measurements were conducted on the corresponding supernatants. Before each measurement, samples were gently loaded onto the lower plate and rested for 10 min to minimize shear-history effects. Apparent viscosity was recorded by sweeping the shear rate from 0.1 to 100 s[−][1 ] (Choudhury et al., 2025). 

For the visual tube test, pectin solutions (1%, w/v) were prepared using identical stirring conditions. Because samples precipitated at higher PpH may not fully dissolve, this test was designed as a qualitative comparison of dispersion behavior rather than confirmation of complete dissolution, each sample was transferred into 5 mL test tubes and subjected to standardized up-and-down shaking (10 cycles within 5 s). The tubes were then immediately placed vertically on a flat platform. Images were captured at 5 s intervals after placement to document bubble retention behavior and fluidity differences among samples.

## _2.13. Fluorescence probe analysis of hydrophobic interactions_ 

Pyrene was first dissolved in ethanol to prepare a 0.1 mM stock solution. For each assay, 60 μL of the pyrene stock solution was added to 3.0 mL of the pectin dispersion, followed by gentle shaking or inversion to ensure homogeneity. Samples were kept in the dark for 1 h to allow equilibrium partitioning of pyrene into hydrophobic microdomains. Fluorescence spectra were recorded from 350 to 500 nm with an excitation wavelength of 334 nm, using excitation/emission slit widths of 5 nm and 2.5 nm, respectively, and a scanning speed of 2400 nm/min. The intensity ratio of the first (≈373 nm, I1) to the third (≈384 nm, I3) vibronic band was calculated (I1/I3). Plots of I1/I3 versus the logarithm of pectin concentration were generated, and the critical association concentration (CAC) of each PpH sample was determined from the intersection of two linear fits corresponding to the dilute and semi-dilute concentration regimes (Zhao et al., 2025). 

## _2.14. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC)_ 

Thermodynamic properties of the samples were analyzed using a Differential Scanning Calorimeter (DSC) following previously reported procedures with minor modifications (Shi et al., 2024; Zhiqiang et al., 2021). Approximately 8 mg of each sample was accurately weighed and placed into an aluminum crucible, which was then sealed. An empty crucible was used as the reference. The samples were heated at a rate of 10[◦] C/min from 130[◦] C to 170[◦] C under a nitrogen purge. The peak temperature (TP) and enthalpy change (ΔH) were recorded during the analysis. 

## _2.15. Statistical analysis_ 

Data are presented as the mean ± standard deviation (n = 3). Statistical comparisons were performed using one-way ANOVA, with a significance level set at _p <_ 0.05. Further data analysis and graphical representations were carried out using GraphPad Prism 10 and Origin 2025. 

## **3. Results and discussions** 

## _3.1. Solubility and aggregation behavior of RGI-rich pectin at different precipitation pH_ 

The dried pectin samples exhibited distinct morphologies across the PpH gradient. Low-PpH samples (PpH = 1–3) appeared fluffy and loosely packed, while high-PpH samples (PpH ≥4) formed dense, rigid solids with markedly reduced porosity (Fig. 1A). This pronounced morphological transition coincided with a progressive increase in pectin yield, suggesting that higher PpH enhances recovery efficiency. However, the same conditions appear to facilitate tighter molecular packing during precipitation and drying, which in turn drives irreversible aggregation and markedly impairs solubility. Similar effects of pH on pectin network structure and porosity have been reported, where lower pH favours more open, porous morphologies whereas higher pH conditions produce denser, closely packed networks, likely due to altered charge screening and chain associations (Souza Almeida et al., 2021). 

Quantitative solubility measurements revealed a pronounced decline between PpH 3 and 4, dropping from 77.51% to 39.82%, which paralleled marked differences in dissolution behavior. Although the pH of redissolved dispersions differed among PpH groups (Table S1), cross-

Although some monosaccharide composition indices differed significantly among PpH groups (Table 1), the RG-I-related indicators showed no significant differences, suggesting that the overall RG-I domain characteristics were broadly preserved. Taken together, these results suggest that the marked loss of solubility was unlikely to arise primarily from extensive acid-induced degradation or major disruption of the RG-I domain at low PpH. Notably, although glucose (Glc) is not a canonical constituent of the pectin backbone, detectable Glc has been frequently reported in plant-derived (including RGI-rich) pectin preparations (Du et al., 2023), even after purification, which is generally attributed to tightly associated/co-extracted glucanor hemicellulose-derived components and/or polymeric carbohydrate fragments that are not readily removed by routine purification such as dialysis. Therefore, the Glc content here is interpreted as co-existing neutral-sugar components rather than a change in the pectin backbone composition. Collectively, these findings underscore the pivotal role of PpH in regulating the aggregation behavior of RGI-rich pectin, which in turn affects its solubility and functional performance in the final product. 

_3.2.2. FTIR analysis of RGI-rich pectin at different precipitation pH_ 

The FTIR spectra of RGI-rich pectin samples prepared under different PpH conditions are shown in Fig. 2A. All seven samples exhibited similar spectral profiles, corroborating the monosaccharide composition results and indicating that PpH did not introduce new characteristic functional groups into the pectin, although some primary-structure-related indices were found to vary significantly among treatments. Notably, the hydroxyl absorption band near 3400 cm[−][1 ] exhibited broadening, gradually – overlapping with the C H stretching region. This spectral change indicates a weakening of hydrogen bonding between water molecules and pectin chains, likely accompanied by an increase in intramolecular hydrogen bonding within the pectin structure (Wang et al., 2019). This shift is consistent with enhanced molecular aggregation. In addition, minor variations were observed in the absorption bands around 1740 cm[−][1 ] and 1630–1600 cm[−][1] , corresponding to ester and carboxyl group vibrations, respectively. The degree of methylesterification (DM%) was bands quantified from the integrated areas of the 1740 and 1630 cm[−][1 ] (Fig. 2B). As shown in Fig. 2B, DM% decreased significantly with increasing precipitation pH, with a pronounced drop between PpH 2–4 and then reaching a relatively stable level at higher PpH (PpH ≥4). This result indicates that mild de-esterification may occur under higher PpH conditions during ethanol precipitation, thereby increasing the proportion of de-esterified carboxyl groups (Yang et al., 2018).

## _3.3.1. Electrostatic interactions: zeta potential analysis_

## _3.3.2. Selective disruption analysis of intermolecular interactions_ 

To further dissect the molecular forces underlying the pronounced solubility loss observed at high PpH, we evaluated the effects of sodium chloride (NaCl), urea, and guanidine hydrochloride (Gu-HCl) on pectin aggregation behavior. These reagents selectively disrupt different noncovalent interactions: NaCl screens electrostatic repulsion via ionic shielding, urea disrupts hydrogen bonding while also acting as a good solvent for non-polar residues, and Gu-HCl serves as a potent chaotrope capable of weakening both hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions.

In both the control and NaCl-treated groups, samples at PpH = 1-3 appeared as buoyant, highly porous gel-like aggregates, whereas at PpH ≥4 the precipitates transitioned into compact granular or flocculent sediments that readily settled, with complete sedimentation at PpH = 6-7. This morphology is consistent with the reduced electrostatic repulsion expected at low pH, where most carboxyl groups remain protonated (pKa ≈3.5) and uncharged, rendering NaCl shielding effects minimal (Han et al., 2017; Kalapathy & Proctor, 2001 Pieczywek et al., 2021). As pH increases, carboxyl group ionization enhances negative charge density and thus the potential for ionic shielding by NaCl; however, the resulting decrease in solubility indicates that electrostatic screening alone is not sufficient to prevent association and precipitation in this system. Notably, aggregation may already initiate in the aqueous phase (especially for poorly soluble fractions), and hot-air drying further involves progressive dehydration that concentrates the residual aqueous phase. Under such dehydration/concentration conditions, ionic strength increases and electrostatic screening – may be amplified, which could facilitate closer chain chain approach and promote aggregation (Wang et al., 2019). Therefore, the NaCl effect here is interpreted as an indirect mechanistic implication for drying-stage association rather than a direct observation of chain packing during drying. 

In contrast, urea and Gu-HCl markedly altered precipitation morphology across the entire PpH range. Samples remained floating at the liquid surface and displayed an open, multi-porous gel-like structure regardless of PpH. Urea can directly disrupt internal hydrogen bonds within polysaccharide chains, destabilizing their native helical conformations and promoting chain unfolding (Diener et al., 2019; P´erez et al., 2000; Zdunek et al., 2021). It also interacts with water molecules, reducing their structural organization and hydration capability, while improving the solvation of non-polar moieties (Jing et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2019). Gu-HCl, a stronger chaotropic agent, competes for hydrogen bond acceptor sites and simultaneously reduces hydrophobic associations, thereby enhancing compatibility of pectin with water ( et al., 2022; Sun & Arntfield, 2012). 

Solubility assays (Fig. 7A) revealed that both urea and Gu-HCl treatments increased pectin solubility across all PpH levels, with GuHCl showing slightly greater efficacy. The consistent improvement indicates that the solubility decline at high PpH is primarily driven by intramolecular hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions, which promote irreversible aggregation during thermal drying. The partial and pH-dependent effects of NaCl further support that electrostatic forces are secondary contributors in this system, in agreement with zeta potential findings.

## _3.3.4. Thermal stability analysis: DSC_ 

Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) was used to evaluate the influence of PpH on the thermal stability of RGI-rich pectin. As shown in the thermograms (Fig. 9A), all samples exhibited endothermic peaks within the range of 140–155[◦] C. These transitions are generally attributed to the presence of hydrophilic groups and bound water within the pectin molecules, as well as conformational rearrangements of galacturonic acid residues. In particular, the observed thermal events are likely associated with the conversion of the stable[4] C1 chair conformation of polygalacturonic acid units into the less stable[4] C1 chair form via a boat-like intermediate, which corresponds to a higher free energy state (Wang et al., 2016; Williams et al., 2007). 

With increasing PpH, both the enthalpy change (ΔH) and peak temperature (Tp) of the thermal transitions increased (Fig. 9B). Specifically, ΔH rose from 154.9 J/g at PpH = 1 to 180.3 J/g at PpH = 7, while Tp shifted from 145.0[◦] C to 152.6[◦] C over the same pH range. This upward shift in the endothermic peak suggests an enhancement in thermal stability, which may be attributed to stronger intermolecular hydrogen bonding and the resulting compact molecular aggregation structure (Einhorn-Stoll & Kunzek, 2009b; Shi et al., 2024). Although improved thermal stability is typically desirable, in this case, the increased aggregation leads to reduced solubility, which is detrimental to downstream applications (Stoll et al., 2014). Additionally, partial de-esterification of pectin under higher pH conditions during precipitation may also contribute to changes in thermal behavior (Einhorn-Stoll & Kunzek, 2009a). These results support the hypothesis that higher PpH promotes more ordered and tightly bound structures, consistent with the observed reduction in solubility.
