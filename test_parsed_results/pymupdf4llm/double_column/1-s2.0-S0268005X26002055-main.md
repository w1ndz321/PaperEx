## **1. Introduction** 

Monitoring the quality and safety of perishable products, such as meat and seafood, is a critical challenge in the globalized food supply chain, with significant implications for public health and economic sustainability (H. Zhang, Noykaew, et al., 2025). According to data from the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), roughly one-third of the world's food annually is lost or wasted before reaching consumers, with a significant proportion of these losses attributed to inadequate storage conditions at the retail level (Bos-Brouwers, 2025; Xue et al., 2017). Fresh meat, as a protein-rich matrix, offers an ideal environment for microbial growth. Under suboptimal storage conditions, proteins are broken down through the combined action of endogenous enzymes and microorganisms (such as Pseudomonas and Enterobacteriaceae), pro— ducing Total Volatile Basic Nitrogen (TVB-N) mainly ammonia, dimethylamine, and trimethylamine (Gomes et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2021). These metabolites serve as reliable indicators of spoilage and exhibit some toxicity (Chang et al., 2019). 

Consumers typically use subjective sensory evaluations to assess the freshness of meat in both retail and domestic environments. However, by the time visible spoilage or off-odors are detected, the TVB-N content 

and total bacterial count in the meat have usually surpassed food safety limits (Bekhit et al., 2021; Tahir et al., 2022). Moreover, laboratory-based detection methods, which involve destructive sampling, extended processing times, and complex procedures, are unsuitable for real-time monitoring (Cao et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2023; Yanu & Jakmunee, 2017). Consequently, "intelligent packaging" technology, capable of providing a visual representation of microenvironmental changes inside the packaging in real time, has become increasingly critical (Yao et al., 2024). 

To enable real-time visualization of the microenvironment within food packaging, colorimetric indicators have attracted substantial research interest in recent years (Luo et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2022; C. Ma et al., 2025). Their operating principle relies on functional dyes that are highly sensitive either to spoilage metabolites, such as ammonia, biogenic amines, and hydrogen sulfide, or to associated pH shifts (Balbinot-Alfaro et al., 2019; Zia et al., 2021). This sensitivity triggers discernible color transitions, thereby providing an intuitive, visual indication of food freshness. Current indicator systems are broadly categorized into two classes: synthetic dyes and natural pigments. Synthetic dyes, including bromothymol blue (D. Yang et al., 2023), methyl red (P. Lu et al., 2020), and polydiacetylene (S. Kim et al., 2017),

exhibit exceptional sensitivity. Nevertheless, their potential cytotoxicity and carcinogenicity severely restrict the application of these dyes in food packaging. By contrast, natural pigments such as curcumin and anthocyanins are increasingly favored owing to their wide availability, biocompatibility, and established safety for consumption (Li et al., 2022). Notably, anthocyanins display a red hue at pH 4 but undergo a rapid, distinct color shift to bluish-purple or blue within the pH 8-10 range, yielding a color difference (ΔE) that can exceed 40 (Guo et al., 2025). 

Numerous studies have incorporated natural dyes into synthetic polymer films, such as polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) (Xia et al., 2025; Xiaowei et al., 2023), polylactic acid (PLA) (Gravier-Rodríguez et al., 2023; S. Zhang, Noykaew, et al., 2025), and polyethylene (PE) (Shin et al., 2023), or biopolymer films (J. Liu et al., 2018). However, in these conventional dense matrices, the highly entangled polymer chains significantly obstruct the diffusion pathways of gas molecules. During the initial phase of meat spoilage, the concentration of released TVB-N (primarily ammonia) is typically below 50 ppm. In contrast, such dense films typically exhibit porosities of less than 10% and gas permeability coefficients as low as 10[−][12 ] to 10[−][14 ] cm[2 ] s[−][1 ] (Tanis-Kanbur et al., 2021). This structural configuration impedes the rapid diffusion of ammonia molecules to the dye's active sites within the film. Consequently, the indicators suffer from prolonged response times ( _>_ 1 min), a relatively high detection limit ( _>_ 50 ppm), and an overall sensitivity that falls substantially short of practical requirements (Alizadeh-Sani et al., 2021). These intrinsic mass transfer limitations critically undermine the reliability and early-warning efficacy of intelligent indicators under real shelf-life conditions. 

Aerogels represent a class of porous materials distinguished by their high porosity and extensive specific surface area (H. B. Yang et al., 2024). Within their interconnected pore networks, both Knudsen and molecular diffusion mechanisms operate concurrently (Szmyt et al., 2021). This dual-diffusion character endows the material with a gas diffusion coefficient that is three to five orders of magnitude higher than that of conventional dense polymer membranes. Thus, target gases can permeate the matrix and access active sites anchored on the skeletal framework within seconds. However, conventional inorganic aerogels such as silica aerogels fracture under compressive strains as low as 10-20% (M. Liu et al., 2025). Likewise, most synthetic polymeric aerogels, including those based on polyimide or polyurethane, exhibit substantial permanent deformation, with rates reaching 60-90% after repeated compression (X. Zhang et al., 2020). These inherent mechanical shortcomings severely compromise their practical durability. In contrast, cellulose, which is the most abundant natural polymer on earth, features a high density of hydroxyl groups along its molecular chains (X. Lu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025; W. Zhang et al. 2023). These groups not only impart pronounced hydrophilicity and biocompatibility but also provide abundant chemical sites for covalent grafting of functional molecules (Ding, et al., 2023, 2025). Cellulose aerogels can self-assemble via hydrogen bonding into a resilient, mechanically robust “skeleton-network” architecture (J. Kim et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2016). This unique structure simultaneously delivers ultrahigh gas permeability and intrinsic sustainability, thereby establishing cellulose aerogels as an ideal carrier material for high-sensitivity food freshness indicator labels. 

This study successfully developed a non-toxic and sustainable cellulose aerogel indicator label (ARCCP-x) for real-time monitoring of meat freshness. Distinct from previously reported anthocyanin-based indicators, this work integrated a foam-derived load-bearing porous architecture with high cyclic compressive resilience, enabling rapid ammonia diffusion at low concentrations while maintaining structural stability. The internal microstructure and mechanical properties of the aerogel were systematically characterized. The ΔE of aerogels containing varying amounts of blueberry anthocyanins was quantified after 30 s of exposure to 2000 ppm ammonia. The color-responsive behavior of the aerogel under different humidity conditions was also evaluated. 

Furthermore, the indicator label was validated by storing it with fresh meat in a package at room temperature for 64 h. A correlation between the ΔE and both the pH and TVB-N content of the meat was established, confirming the reliability of the colorimetric approach for freshness monitoring. 

## **2. Materials and methods** 

## _2.1. Materials_ 

Bamboo cellulose pulp board was provided by Tianjin University of Science and Technology Pulp and Paper Laboratory. Polyvinyl alcohol type 1788 (alcoholysis: 87%-89% (mol/mol)) and rhamnolipid (≥95 wt %) were purchased from Shanghai Yien Chemical Technology Co., LTD. Citric acid (99.5 wt%) was obtained from Shanghai Jizhi Biochemical Technology Co., LTD. Sodium hypophosphite (analytically pure) was bought by Tianjin Fuchen Chemical Reagent Co., LTD. Blueberry anthocyanins were purchased from Shanghai Maclellan Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd. 

## _2.2. Fabrication of RCCP and ARCCP-x aerogels_ 

The synthesis protocol for rhamnolipid-modified cellulose aerogel (RCCP) has been reported previously (Song et al., 2024). 5 g of dry bamboo pulp, 70 g of polyvinyl alcohol solution (PVA, 5 wt%), 5 g of citric acid (CA), 2.5 g of sodium hypophosphite (NaH2PO2), and 58.5 g of deionized water were thoroughly mixed. The mixture was then reacted in an oven at 150[◦] C. The curing temperature (150[◦] C) was selected to promote citric-acid-mediated esterification between carboxyl groups of CA and hydroxyl groups on cellulose/PVA, thereby forming covalent crosslinks; sodium hypophosphite facilitated this thermally induced crosslinking process. Subsequently, different concentrations of blueberry anthocyanin and 2.5 g of rhamnolipid were added under stirring to induce foaming. The introduction of rhamnolipid stabilized the gas-liquid interface during foaming and enhanced the interfacial strength of the polymer network, thereby facilitating the formation of a stable porous structure after freeze-drying. The resulting foam was transferred into a mold and freeze-dried for 48 h to obtain a pH-responsive cellulose aerogel, designated as ARCCP-x, where x represents the mass of blueberry anthocyanin added (Fig. 1).

The mass of each sample was recorded using an analytical balance with an accuracy of ±0.1 mg. The bulk density ( _ρb_ ) was calculated as the ratio of mass to geometric volume.

## _2.3.2. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)_ 

SEM imaging was conducted using a JEOL JSM-IT300LV Schottky field-emission scanning electron microscope, operated under highvacuum conditions. All samples were sputter-coated with a 10-nm layer of platinum-palladium prior to imaging to ensure sufficient conductivity. 

## _2.3.3. Pore size distribution analysis_ 

The pore size distribution of the aerogels was analyzed based on SEM images. Representative SEM micrographs were selected, and pore diameters were statistically measured using ImageJ software. Prior to measurement, the scale bar of each image was calibrated within the software. The pore size was determined by measuring the equivalent circular diameter of each pore. 

## _2.3.4. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR)_ 

FTIR spectroscopy was performed on a Nicolet iS5 FTIR spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Spectra were acquired at room temperature over the range of 4000-400 cm[−][1] . All samples were prepared as potassium bromide (KBr) pellets, and background spectra were collected under identical conditions prior to each sample measurement. 

## _2.3.5. X-ray diffraction (XRD)_ 

XRD patterns were acquired on a Rigaku Ultima IV diffractometer over the 2θ range of 10-40[◦] with a step size of 0.02[◦] and a scanning rate of 2[◦] min[−][1] . 

## _2.3.6. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS)_ 

XPS analysis was performed to determine the surface chemical composition and bonding states using a Kratos AXIS Ultra DLD spectrometer (Shimadzu, Japan). The measurements were conducted under ultra-high vacuum ( _<_ 5 × 10[−][9 ] Torr). 

## _2.3.7. Mechanical properties_ 

The compressive mechanical properties of the aerogel samples were evaluated using an electronic universal testing machine (Lishi, LD23.53, China). All tests were conducted at room temperature and at a constant strain rate of 5 mm/min. To assess the resilience, cyclic compression tests were performed by loading the samples to a preset strain of 50% – and then unloading to zero force; this loading unloading cycle was repeated 500 times. Each type of test was performed on at least five replicate samples to ensure statistical reliability. 

## _2.3.8. Ammonia gas response performance_ 

Ammonia gas at concentrations of 40, 400, 1200, and 2000 ppm was introduced into a 500-mL light-proof sealed container. The container was then moderately heated for 30 min to promote ammonia evaporation and ensure uniform gas distribution. Meanwhile, the pH-responsive aerogel loaded with blueberry anthocyanins was cut into circular pieces (0.5 cm in diameter). These pieces were strung together with a thread and suspended at the center of the container. After exposure for 30 s, the samples were removed for analysis.

## **3. Results and discussion** 

Pork samples were placed in a transparent glass Petri dish at room temperature together with pH-responsive aerogel slices as freshness indicators. Images were taken every 8 h. The colorimetric response of the aerogel was quantified by calculating the ΔE of the L, a, and b values measured at the beginning and end of the monitoring period. 

## _2.5. pH measurement_ 

At 8-h intervals, three small tissue sections were excised from the pork samples and individually minced using a meat grinder. The pH of the resulting meat juice was then measured with a pH probe, and the mean of the three readings was determined. 

## _2.6. Determination of total volatile basic nitrogen (TVB-N) content_ 

At 8-h intervals, three small tissue sections were excised from the pork samples. These sections were then analyzed for TVB-N content using an automated Kjeldahl nitrogen analyzer (Kjeltec 8400, Denmark). The analysis followed the Chinese national standard method GB/T 5009.44, and the results from the triplicate measurements were averaged and expressed in mg/100g. 

## _2.7. Correlation analysis between ΔE and TVB-N and pH changes_ 

To assess the reliability of ΔE as an indicator of pork freshness, linear regression analysis was employed to examine the quantitative relationships between ΔE and both TVB-N and pH. The regression models were fitted using the ordinary least squares method. All statistical analyses were conducted with SPSS 26.0. 

## _3.1. Characterization of RCCP, and ARCCP-x aerogels_ 

Fig. 2A delineates the structural formation mechanism of pHresponsive cellulose aerogel (ARCCP-x), along with the concomitant reaction of blueberry anthocyanins integrated into the matrix. The formation of the aerogel's regular porous structure is driven by two key components. Citric acid (CA) acts as a multifunctional cross-linker by forming ester bonds with hydroxyl groups on both cellulose and PVA chains during thermal curing, thereby constructing an interpenetrated polymer network that stabilizes the aerogel skeleton. These synergistic processes together impart the final architecture. Moreover, blueberry anthocyanins establish robust hydrogen bonds with both cellulose and PVA. As a consequence of these interactions, the anthocyanins are homogeneously and stably incorporated into the aerogel's internal framework. 

Fig. 2B displays the macroscopic morphology and microstructure of the control sample RCCP (without blueberry anthocyanins) and ARCCPx samples containing different anthocyanin concentrations. Macroscopically, all samples maintain a well-defined cylindrical shape with smooth surfaces, demonstrating that the addition of blueberry anthocyanins does not cause deformation or surface defects in the aerogel matrix. With increasing anthocyanin content from 0.125 g to 1.0 g, the sample color shifts gradually from light pink to a deeper shade. This color gradient offers direct visual confirmation of the successful incorporation and uniform dispersion of anthocyanins throughout the aerogel structure. SEM images further reveal the internal porous architecture of all samples. Such architecture is characteristic of ice-templated freezedrying processes, where the sublimation of ice crystals leaves behind continuous diffusion pathways. The XRD patterns in Fig. 3B show that both RCCP and ARCCP-0.75 display characteristic peaks at the same diffraction angles, which correspond to the 110, 020, and 040 crystal planes of cellulose I (Hernandez-Varela et al., 2021´ ). This indicates that the addition of anthocyanins does not modify the crystalline structure of the plant-derived cellulose in the system. 

Fig. 3C and D display the XPS spectra of RCCP and ARCCP-0.75, respectively. Spectral analysis reveals a slight shift in the C–O/C=O bond peaks, signifying that anthocyanin incorporation alters the local chemical environment of the carbon atoms. This increase in binding energy is commonly attributed to hydrogen bond formation (Chen et al., 2025). The underlying mechanism involves the formation of O–H⋅⋅⋅O hydrogen bonds between the phenolic hydroxyls of anthocyanins and the hydroxyl groups in the aerogel matrix. This reduces the electron cloud density around the oxygen atom acting as the hydrogen bond donor. This decrease weakens the shielding effect on the oxygen atom's core electrons, resulting in an increased binding energy. The observed blueshift in the XPS data thus corroborates the redshift of the – OH peak observed in FTIR. Collectively, these results confirm that blueberry anthocyanins form strong hydrogen bonds with the cellulose/PVA network, ensuring a stable and integrated structure.

## _3.3. Color response performance of aerogel_ 

Given that the spoilage of fresh meat releases ammonia gas, a series of pH-responsive aerogels (ARCCP-x) are initially screened using 2000 

ppm ammonia gas prior to application testing. Fig. 5A shows color comparison images of different ARCCP-x samples taken before and after 30 s of exposure, along with their corresponding color difference values (ΔE). All samples exhibit visible color changes to the naked eye, and the Δ E generally rises with increasing anthocyanin content. Specifically, the ΔE is 57.12 for ARCCP-1 and 56.68 for ARCCP-0.75, with a difference of only 0.44. According to chromaticity standards, a ΔE above 3.5 is considered perceptible. Therefore, at the perceptual level, there is no substantial difference in the ammonia-induced color change between ARCCP-0.75 and ARCCP-1. In addition, ARCCP-0.75 maintains a more balanced combination of strong color response and structural integrity, whereas further increasing the anthocyanin loading provides only marginal improvement in ΔE. Given their comparable performance and the lower material cost of ARCCP-0.75, this sample is selected for subsequent investigation of pH-responsive behavior. This relatively concentrated pore size distribution indicates a uniform and interconnected porous architecture. Such a pore structure, characterized by a high proportion of interconnected macropores, is favorable for efficient ammonia diffusion and contributes to the rapid colorimetric

## _3.4. Application in meat packaging_ 

To assess the response performance and reliability of ARCCP-0.75 under real food spoilage monitoring conditions, the aerogel samples are sealed in transparent Petri dishes alongside fresh pork and stored at room temperature (Fig. 6A). ΔE of the aerogel is recorded at 8-h intervals, while the pH and total volatile basic nitrogen (TVB-N) content of the pork are measured simultaneously. With prolonged storage, the white fatty portion of the pork gradually yellows, accompanied by a noticeable color shift in ARCCP-0.75. After 16 h of storage, a visible color change is observed in the aerogel, with ΔE reaching 13.93. The color variation continues to intensify thereafter, demonstrating that ΔE can effectively indicate real-time changes in pork freshness. 

The mechanism of this coloration behavior stems from the specific response of the aerogel to the characteristic gases of spoilage. As illustrated in the schematic diagram of Fig. 6B, as the protein in pork degrades, alkaline volatile gases mainly composed of ammonia (NH3) are released and accumulated in a closed space. Upon contact with ammonia, the phenolic hydroxyl groups of the blueberry anthocyanins loaded in ARCCP-0.75 undergo deprotonation. This structural change results in a redshift of the maximum absorption wavelength, which macroscopically corresponds to a color shift from pink to blue or purple. Initially, the reaction occurs predominantly on the aerogel surface. As ammonia concentration increases, the gas molecules diffuse inward through the material's uniformly interconnected porous network, reacting with additional anthocyanin molecules. This leads to a pronounced and uniform color change throughout the material. Collectively, these processes demonstrate the capacity of ARCCP-0.75 to convert imperceptible spoilage gas signals into visually discernible color changes. 

To establish an objective indicator of spoilage progression, the ΔE was correlated with physicochemical spoilage markers (pH and TVB-N) (Fig. 6C). The Chinese national standard "GB 2707-2016 Food Safety National Standard for Fresh (Frozen) Livestock and Poultry Products" sets the TVB-N limit for fresh pork at ≤15 mg/100g, and the literature consensus indicates that the initial spoilage stage of fresh meat corresponds to a pH threshold typically above 6.2 (Gonzalez-Blanco et al., ´ 2023). ## **4. Conclusion** 

A rapid-response aerogel colorimetric indicator (ARCCP-0.75) composed of blueberry anthocyanins and cellulose is successfully fabricated. The material features a well-defined load-bearing pore structure, demonstrating enhanced mechanical resilience and structural stability. It exhibits a sensitive colorimetric response to ammonia gas, with a maximum ΔE of 56.68, along with a broad concentration detection range and favorable humidity resistance. When applied in monitoring pork freshness, the aerogel displays distinct visual color changes (ΔE = 14-16) during the initial stage of spoilage (16-20 h). Moreover, the ΔE shows strong linear correlations with both TVB-N content and pH ( _R >_ 0.92). These results highlight the material's capability to convert imperceptible spoilage markers into visible signals, offering a promising approach for intelligent monitoring packaging. Future work will further examine long-term stability under prolonged high-humidity storage to support practical application.
