## 24 **Abstract** 

25 Emulsion gels with tunable textures are promising carriers for hydrophobic food 26 ingredients and have been widely used in the food industry. However, the fabrication 27 of emulsion gels frequently depends on isolated food components, such as 28 polysaccharides and proteins, or on chemically or physically modified derivatives. 29 These methods typically involve labor-intensive extraction, purification, or 30 modification steps, which limit their scalability and practical application. Herein, a 

- 31 straightforward and scalable strategy was developed to produce property-tailored 32 emulsion gels by ball-milling a mixture of _Spirulina_ , water, and oil. The effects of 33 _Spirulina_ biomass content and water-to-oil volume ratio (S) on the microstructure, 34 stability, and rheological behavior were investigated. Emulsion gels with an S of 0.3 35 demonstrated the highest level of stability, while the biomass content of _Spirulina_ 36 exerted an insignificant effect on the properties. During ball milling, the cellular 37 structure of _Spirulina_ is disrupted, resulting in the release of phycocyanin and 38 polysaccharides. The polysaccharide-induced depletion effect promotes the enrichment 39 of phycocyanin at the oil–water interface, thereby facilitating emulsion gel formation. 40 The emulsion gel could be used as a bio-ink for 3D printing, and optimal printability 41 was achieved at S = 0.3. In addition, the emulsion gels were used for sesamol delivery, 42 and their bioaccessibility was almost twofold that of the bulk oil. Moreover, the ball43 milling strategy was effective for stabilizing a range of vegetable oils, indicating its 44 broad applicability. Overall, the whole- _Spirulina_ biomass approach provides a 45 straightforward, universal, and scalable route for designing functional emulsion gels for 46 advanced food applications.

## 50 **1. Introduction** 

51 Oil-in-water emulsion gels (O/W) are soft, solid–like structures in which oil 52 droplets are immobilized either within a three-dimensional crosslinked polymer 53 network (emulsion-filled gels) or within a network formed by aggregated oil droplets 54 themselves (particulate emulsion gels) (Niu, Zhang, Mujumdar, & Li, 2026; Shu, 55 McClements, Luo, Liu, & Ye, 2025). Emulsion gels have attracted considerable 56 attention in the field of food science due to their ability to encapsulate, protect, and 

57 control the release of bioactive compounds (Song, Li, Shang, Wang, & Tan, 2023; H. 58 T. Wang, Y. D. Sun, Y. T. Hou, & M. Q. Tan, 2025). The preparation of emulsion gels, 59 however, typically depends on isolated food components, such as polysaccharides or 60 proteins, or on chemically or physically modified derivatives. These approaches often 61 require labor-intensive extraction, purification, or modification processes, which limit 62 their scalability and practical application. Importantly, the growing consumer demand 63 for sustainable, clean-label foods has spurred significant interest in emulsion gels made 64 from natural ingredients with minimal processing, underscoring the need to develop 65 novel biopolymer gelation strategies.

Proteins in _Spirulina_ , particularly the 70 water-soluble phycocyanin, have demonstrated strong emulsifying ability and have 71 been extensively employed to stabilize emulsions. On this basis, _Spirulina_ protein72 based emulsion gels have been developed for applications such as fat replacement (J.

- 74 Gu, & Meng, 2024). In addition, emulsion gels constructed from _Spirulina_ protein 

- 75 isolates and carboxymethyl chitosan have been designed for curcumin delivery (Mao, 

- 76 et al., 2025), while _Spirulina_ protein-chitosan complexes have been reported to stabilize 

- 77 Pickering emulsions for astaxanthin encapsulation (R. G. Liu, Li, Zhou, & Tan, 2023). 

- 78 However, these approaches typically require protein isolation, chemical modification, 

- 79 or cross-linking reactions involving non-food-grade reagents, which limit their 

3 

- 80 scalability and broader application in food products. Recently, the direct utilization of 81 whole biomass has emerged as a promising strategy for emulsion and emulsion gel 82 preparation, offering a more sustainable and simplified alternative. For example, whole 83 _Gracilaria_ biomass has been directly employed as an emulsifier to fabricate emulsions 84 for astaxanthin delivery and fat replacement (Shao, et al., 2026), highlighting the 85 potential of whole-biomass-based approaches for the development of clean-label and 86 sustainable emulsion gels. 

- 87 Proteins and polysaccharides are the most widely used food-grade biopolymers for 88 the construction of emulsion gels (Zhi, et al., 2023). Conventional emulsion gel 89 preparation procedures involve separately adding proteins or polysaccharides. In 90 contrast, whole-biomass-based approaches involve the simultaneous release of cellular 91 compounds, mainly proteins and polysaccharides, during high-energy processing, 92 which enables their direct participation in emulsion gel formation. Proteins function as 93 emulsifiers due to their high surface activity. In contrast, polysaccharides typically 94 contribute indirectly to the emulsion gel formation process by increasing continuous95 phase viscosity or inducing depletion attraction. Recent results have demonstrated that 96 cooperative interactions between proteins and polysaccharides can substantially 97 enhance emulsion stability and promote gel network formation (Kim, et al., 2017; Tong, 98 et al., 2023), particularly in particulate emulsion gels. In the case of _Spirulina_ , the whole 99 biomass has been employed in the preparation of emulsion gels. Depletion attraction 

- 100 has been proposed as a potential underlying mechanism; however, experimental 101 evidence supporting it remains limited (Feng, et al., 2023a). As a result, the specific 102 mechanisms governing protein–polysaccharide cooperation, particularly when both 103 components originate from a single natural source, remain to be fully elucidated. 

- 104 Ball milling is a straightforward and scalable method for processing various food 

- 105 formulations. The freeze–thaw stability of κ-carrageenan-based emulsion gels could be 

- 106 improved by ball milling (Xu, et al., 2025). It is important to note that by adjusting the 

- 107 ratios of the raw materials, the characteristics of food formulations prepared via ball 

- 108 milling can be easily controlled, indicating that the ball milling method offers a 

- 109 straightforward approach to modifying product properties. The rheological properties 

4 

- 110 of oleogels prepared by ball milling can be effectively tuned by regulating the water 111 content of the initial formulation (G.-S. Wang, et al., 2022). Furthermore, the high shear 112 forces generated during ball milling enabled the one-step preparation of emulsion gels 113 from soy protein isolate (SPI) hydrogels, wherein gel formation was regulated by the 

- 114 depletion-induced attraction effect mediated by konjac glucomannan (Z. Yang, et al., 115 2025). In recent years, the development of 3D-printable emulsion gels for functional 116 ingredient delivery has attracted growing interest (Niu, et al., 2026), creating demand 

- 117 for processing methods that are both scalable and controllable. Compared with other 118 methods such as high-pressure homogenization or ultrasonication, ball milling offers a 119 practical approach that meets these requirements because it can be readily scaled for 120 larger production volumes. In addition, key processing parameters, such as milling time 121 and rotation speed, can be easily adjusted to control the properties of the resulting 122 emulsion gels. Despite these advances, systematic investigation into the preparation of 123 emulsion gels via ball milling whole biomass, particularly those that simultaneously 124 enable 3D printability and functional compound delivery, remains limited and merits 125 further study.

- 293 3.1 Microstructure of emulsion gels 

294 Emulsion gels were prepared by ball milling _Spirulina_ powder with oil and water 295 (Fig. 1A). The visual appearance of the mixtures after ball milling was strongly 296 influenced by the water-oil ratio (S). Formulations with S values between 0.1 and 0.2 297 displayed fluid-like behavior and flowed upon tube inversion (Fig. 1B), indicating the 298 absence of a continuous gel network. Whereas when the S increased to the range of 299 0.3–0.5, the fluid mixture transformed into self-standing emulsion gels, suggesting a 300 self-supporting three-dimensional network was formed (S. Wang, et al., 2020). 301 However, a further increase in S to 0.6 led to viscous flow, which may be attributed to 302 excessive dilution of the interfacial stabilizer in the aqueous phase, thereby weakening 303 the gel network (Feng, et al., 2023b). Optical microscopy revealed no obvious oil 304 droplets at S values between 0.1 and 0.2 (Fig. 1C). Furthermore, these samples 305 exhibited fluid-like behavior upon tube inversion (Fig. 1B), which precludes the 306 formation of a bi-continuous emulsion gel at these concentrations. Taken together, these 307 results suggest that a structured emulsion gel was not formed at S values below 0.3. In 308 contrast, well-defined oil droplets were observed in samples with S values between 0.3 309 and 0.6. Moreover, the average oil droplet diameter in emulsion gels increased from 310 1.9±0.55 μm to 6.5±2.14 μm as S increased from 0.3 to 0.6 (Fig. 1C and Fig. 1D).

- 314 Simultaneously, dilution of _Spirulina_ -derived stabilizers at the oil–water interface 315 promoted droplet coalescence, highlighting that both continuous-phase viscosity and 

- 316 stabilizer availability govern droplet size in _Spirulina_ -based emulsion gels. Emulsion 

- 317 gels can be classified into emulsion-filled gels and particulate gels. In emulsion-filled 

- 318 gels, a continuous gel matrix forms the primary load-bearing network, with emulsion 

- 319 droplets dispersed within it. In contrast, particulate gels are formed when the emulsion 

- 320 droplets themselves act as the structural units (Dickinson, 2012). Since ball milling does 

12 

321 not convert _Spirulina_ into a continuous gel (Fig. S1), the resulting emulsion gels in this 

322 case is considered a particulate gel.

Conclusions** 602 In conclusion, a simple and scalable strategy was established to produce property603 tailored emulsion gels by ball-milling a mixture of _Spirulina_ , water, and oil. The water604 to-oil ratio (S) was the main factor influencing the properties of the prepared emulsion 605 gel. Rheological and microstructure results indicate that an increase in S from 0.3 to 0.6 

- 606 resulted in a reduction in the viscosity of the emulsion gel, an increase in the size of the 

- 607 oil droplets, and eventual disruption of the gel structure of the emulsion gel when S 

- 608 reached 0.6. The emulsion gel with S = 0.3 was the most stable, retaining its structure 

- 609 for over 100 days. Mechanistic studies shown that ball milling leads to the release of 

- 610 phycocyanin and polysaccharides from _Spirulina_ cells, which is key to the formation 

- 611 of emulsion gels. Polysaccharides promote the adsorption of phycocyanin at the oil- 

- 612 water interface through the depletion effect and promote the formation of a three- 

25 

- 613 dimensional gel network with high viscosity, high elasticity and structural integrity. _In_ 614 _vitro_ digestion demonstrated that the emulsion gel supported sustained release of 615 sesamol and increased its bioaccessibility by more than twofold. This approach 616 provides a scalable, whole-biomass strategy for designing sustainable functional 617 emulsion gels with tunable properties.

## **Highlights** 

- Emulsion gels formed via one-step ball milling of _Spirulina_ , oil, and water. 

- Ball milling–released proteins and polysaccharides drove emulsion gel formation. 

- _Spirulina_ polysaccharides facilitated gel formation via depletion attraction. 

- Emulsion gels with a water-to-oil ratio of 0.3 showed the highest stability. 

- The emulsion gels were 3D printable and enhanced sesamol bioaccessibility.
