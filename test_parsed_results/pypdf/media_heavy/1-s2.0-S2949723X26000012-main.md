ABSTRACT
Background: Calcaneal fractures are among the most disabling and costly orthopaedic injuries, frequently
requiring surgical fixation via open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) or minimally invasive fixation (MIF).
Finite element (FE) analysis is an increasingly critical in silico tool for optimizing implant designs and comparing
fixation constructs, yet the technical modelling strategies and limitations specific to calcaneal fracture biome -
chanics have not been systematically mapped.
Technology: The FE modelling pipeline comprises sequential technical domains: (1) medical image-based ge -
ometry reconstruction and mesh generation; (2) material assignment (elastic modulus, Poisson's ratio, consti -
tutive laws for bone, cartilage, and ligaments); (3) virtual fracture creation (gap width specification, Sanders
classification implementation); (4) implant insertion (plate/screw/nail geometry, contact definition); (5)
boundary conditions (standing/gait loading protocols, constraint schemes, muscle force incorporation); (6)
solver configuration (static/quasi-static analysis); and (7) outcome extraction (von Mises stress, displacement,
construct stiffness, micromotion metrics, etc.). This systematic scoping review followed JBI methodology and
PRISMA-ScR guidelines to identify FE studies from PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and IEEE Xplore. Meth -
odological quality was evaluated using the MQSSFE instrument for computational orthopaedic models.
Results: Twenty-three studies were included, predominantly using single-subject CT models with artificially
created Sanders type II – III intra-articular fractures. Most employed calcaneus-only geometries, linear elastic
isotropic bone properties, tetrahedral meshes, and quasi-static stance loading. Locking plates, hybrid plate – screw
constructs, screw-only MIF, and intramedullary nails were compared via stress distribution and construct stiff -
ness. Several studies introduced topology-optimized plates and micromotion-based fracture gap metrics,
demonstrating that MIF with supplementary percutaneous screws can achieve biomechanical stability compa -
rable to ORIF. However, verification (mesh convergence) and validation procedures were inconsistently re -
ported, dynamic loading and multi-patient cohorts were rare, and interfragmentary strain – based healing criteria
were largely absent. This delineation of the current technical design space highlights priorities for more phys -
iologically realistic and methodologically robust in silico studies.

1. Introduction
Calcaneal fractures are among the most debilitating injuries in or -
thopaedic trauma [ 1 ] with an annual incidence estimated at 11.5 per
100,000 person-years in the overall population [ 2 ]. Calcaneal fractures
most commonly result from falls from height, motor vehicle accidents
and sports-related injuries [ 3 ]. The injury pattern demonstrates a
marked occupational association, particularly in men at working age
[ 2 ].
Severe compound fractures are notably common, with over two-
thirds classified as displaced intra-articular and more than a quarter
identified as compound fractures [ 4 , 5 ]. These injuries pose significant
management challenges and are associated with poor clinical outcomes,
including heightened postoperative pain and an increased need for
secondary surgical procedures [ 6 , 7 ]. Calcaneal fractures rank among the
costliest of all fracture types, according to insurance data [ 8 ]. These
injuries often result in long-term, sometimes irreversible disability,
particularly affecting individuals of working age and highlighting their
profound economic and social consequences [ 9 ].
Treatment controversies and evolving surgical paradigms may
contribute to the poor prognosis of calcaneal fractures [ 10 ]. Historically,
conservative management was the standard approach for displaced
intra-articular calcaneal fractures, with many clinicians favouring
non-surgical treatment to avoid the risks linked to operative interven -
tion [ 10 ]. However, this approach frequently led to complications such
as malunion, residual deformity, and post-traumatic arthritis, under -
scoring the limitations of non-operative care [ 10 ]. In recent decades, the
treatment paradigm for calcaneal fractures has shifted, with open
reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) via the extensile lateral approach
becoming the gold standard, superior to conservative management [ 11 ].
This technique offers excellent visualization of the fracture site, enabling
precise anatomical reduction of the posterior facet of the subtalar joint.
Stabilization is typically achieved using calcaneal locking plates and
multiple screws. Multiple studies reported good-to-excellent radiolog -
ical and functional outcomes [ 12 , 13 ]. However, the large incision and
extensive dissection associated with this approach contribute to a sig -
nificant complication profile, most notably wound-related and healing
issues [ 14 ], as well as deep infections that may necessitate implant
removal [ 15 ].
Minimally invasive fixation (MIF) has emerged as a research focus in
the field [ 16 ] and a promising alternative to ORIF to address the high
wound complication rates [ 11 , 17 ]. It encompasses several distinct ap -
proaches, such as screw or pin-only fixation, small plates, and intra -
medullary nail with significantly smaller incision and reduced tissue
disruption [ 18 , 19 ]. However, MIFs are associated with poorer reduction
quality and long-term fixation stability in complex fractures. A 6%
malunion rate was reported and the risk was higher in patients with
Sanders IV fracture, in addition to those with delayed surgical treat -
ments [ 20 ]. Abdelazeem et al. [ 21 ] reported concerns about secondary
loss of reduction with screws fixations. A gradual loss of reduction in

Advancements in biomechanics have played a pivotal role in
addressing the challenge of achieving optimal fixation stability while
preserving a MIF surgical approach for calcaneal fracture fixation.
Recent innovations in implant design have focused on creating low-
profile constructs with minimal size, without compromising essential
fixation strength [ 23 , 24 ]. The integration of computational biome -
chanics, particularly finite element (FE) analysis, has significantly
transformed the implant design process. FE analysis provides a rapid,
ethical, and theoretically rigorous method for simulating and refining
implant performance under various loading conditions. This approach
shortens the design cycle and reduces reliance on cadaveric studies and
high-risk clinical trials.
Although FE analysis has become an established tool in
musculoskeletal biomechanics, most existing literature reviews remain
broad in scope and do not directly address the specific biomechanical
challenges of calcaneal fracture fixation. Comprehensive reviews have
primarily discussed general technical aspects of model and materials,
providing methodological insights [ 25 – 29 ]. Others have catalogued
various foot and ankle pathologies [ 30 – 32 ] or focused on other foot
pathologies [ 33 , 34 ], while some focused on auxiliary applications, such
as footwear [ 35 – 37 ] and orthotics [ 38 , 39 ].
By systematically mapping the current landscape of FE studies on
calcaneal fracture fixation, the objective of this review is to illuminate
how computational modelling has advanced biomechanical under -
standing and optimization of calcaneal fixation, while also critically
evaluating the methodological approaches, model settings, and limita -
tions reported in the literature.
2. Methods
2.1. Search strategy
This scoping review was conducted in accordance with the Joanna
Briggs Institute (JBI) methodology for scoping reviews [ 40 ] and adhered
to the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews
and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) guidelines [ 41 ]. A
comprehensive literature search was performed by the first author on
July 2, 2025, without restriction on publication year, limited to articles
published in English. The following electronic databases were system -
atically searched: PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and IEEE Xplore
Digital Library. Search strategies combined groups of keywords related
to “ finite element ” , “ calcaneus ” , and “ fracture ” using Boolean logic. The
main search string was: ( “ finite element ” OR “ numerical model* ” OR
“ computer model* ” OR “ computational model* ” OR “ biomechanical
model* ” OR “ biomechanical simulation* ” OR “ FEM ” OR “ FEA ” ) AND
( “ calcane* ” OR “ heel ” ) AND ( “ Sanders ” OR “ fracture* ” ). Detailed search
terms, queries, and the number of records retrieved from each database
are provided in the Supplementary Table S1 .
2.2. Screening and selection process
All identified references were managed using Endnote v20 (Clar -
ivate, Philadelphia, United States). The screening and selection process
was conducted independently by the first and second authors, with any
disagreements resolved through consensus among all authors.
Eligibility was restricted to studies employing FE models to evaluate
the biomechanics of calcaneal fracture fixation. Inclusion criteria
covered English-language original research articles published in jour -
nals (including preprints and in-press articles) and full conference pa -
pers. Exclusion criteria included ineligible article types (such as
literature reviews, commentaries, and book chapters), retracted papers,
studies that did not simulate a calcaneal model (e.g., those simulating
only the implant), studies not involving a human calcaneal model
(excluding animal models but including human cadaveric models), and
studies that did not report critical information about the model partic -
ipant (specifically, age and gender).
Screening was performed in a stepwise manner. Initially, duplicates
were identified and removed using Endnote and then manually verified.
The first level of screening involved the exclusion of retracted articles
and ineligible article types, as well as articles that were clearly irrelevant
based on title, abstract, and keywords (e.g., those focused on unrelated
geographical or manufacturing topics, or not addressing calcaneal
fracture, fixation, or FE analysis). In the second level, full-text articles
were reviewed to further exclude studies that violated the predefined
criteria, such as those involving non-human models, lacking participant
important demographic information, or simulating only the implant
without the bone model.

2.3. Data extraction and analysis framework
The data extraction and synthesis framework for this study, based on
established scoping review methodologies, was explicitly structured
around the participant-context-concept (PCC) model [ 40 ]. For each
included study, we synthesized and mapped key information into the
following domains: participants (and models), contextual framework
(encompassing technical modelling details), and conceptual context
(focusing on outcomes and key thematic findings). Where appropriate,
evidence maps were generated to illustrate the distribution of modelling
approaches and key themes, consistent with practices in other reviews of
FE models [ 42 , 43 ].
2.4. Quality assessment
The methodological quality of the included studies was evaluated
using the Methodological Quality Assessment of Single-Subject Finite
Element Analysis Used in Computational Orthopaedics (MQSSFE) [ 44 ].
This tool is specifically designed to address the unique challenges and
requirements of FE analysis in biomechanical research. MQSSFE com -
prises 37 items distributed across six domains: study design and pre -
sentation, subject recruitment, model reconstruction and configuration,
boundary and loading conditions, simulation, and model verification
and validity. Each item is scored as either “ Yes ” or “ No, ” providing a
structured and comprehensive assessment of methodological rigor. The
assessment was conducted independently by the two authors, with any
disagreements resolved through consensus among all authors.
In applying the MQSSFE tool, we supplemented the following criteria
after discussion among authors to address recurring methodological
ambiguities. For Item 5 on timepoint of models, we categorized all
studies as “ Yes ” since it was currently not applicable. For Item 7, studies
were marked as not clearly describing key findings if they failed to
specify the type of stress reported. For item 16, a study was only rated as
having clear methodology only if the method for differentiating
trabecular and cortical bone segmentation was explicitly stated. For
item 17, adequate reporting of component orientation and placement
was accepted if studies referenced implant manufacturer protocols,
direct virtual surgical positioning by surgeons, or specific anatomical

3. Search results
As shown in Fig. 1 , we initially identified 445 records across multiple
databases. After removing 182 duplicate records, 261 records under -
went title and abstract screening. During the screening phase, 217 re -
cords were excluded based on predefined criteria including retracted
articles ( n = 2), ineligible article types ( n = 5), irrelevant content
( n = 128), non-calcaneal fracture studies ( n = 57), studies without
fracture fixation focus ( n = 22), and studies without FE modelling
( n = 3). Forty-four full-text articles were retrieved for detailed eligibility
assessment, with 21 additional studies excluded due to non-calcaneal
fracture focus ( n = 10), studies without FE modelling ( n = 4), missing
important participant information ( n = 6), absence of fracture fixation
( n = 1), non-human studies ( n = 1), studies lacking a calcaneal model
( n = 1). The systematic selection process ultimately identified 23 studies
for qualitative synthesis [ 45 – 67 ].
4. Participants and models
4.1. Model participants
The review encompassed 23 studies, reconstructing foot models from
a total of 26 participants. Notably, 22 studies created a model based on a
single participant, while one study reconstructed models from 4 par -
ticipants. Of these 23 studies, 18 studies utilized male participant
models, and 6 studies employed female models. The age range of the
modelled participants spanned from 24 to 69 years, with a mean age of
40.1 years, excluding one cadaveric study with a mean age of 87 over 4
participants [ 47 ]. Right-sided calcaneal fractures were more frequently
modelled ( n = 15) compared to left-sided fractures ( n = 7), and one
study included bilateral models. The available anthropometric data
showed participants with height ranging from 156 to 178 cm and
weights from 55 to 75 kg.
Importantly, all 23 studies utilized computational models derived
from intact healthy calcanei rather than patient-specific fractured
calcanei, with fracture patterns artificially created. All studies except
one employed single-subject models, with the exception being Delmon
et al. [ 47 ], which utilized 8 feet from 4 cadaveric participants. Three
studies specifically applied cadaveric specimens [ 47 , 54 , 55 ], while the
remaining 20 studies reconstructed computational models from living
participants imaging data. Table 1 summarizes the basic model partic -
ipants, simulated fractures and fixations information. Supplementary
Table S2 included more detailed information on the implants and fixa -
tion approaches.

4.3. Fracture fixations
The reviewed literature highlights the central role of both locking
and nonlocking plate systems in the fixation of calcaneal fractures, as
shown in Table 1 . Contoured locking plates are frequently investigated
( n = 17), with studies systematically comparing constructs utilizing all
locking screws, all nonlocking screws, and various hybrid configurations
that combine both types. For instance, Chen et al. [ 45 ] conducted
comprehensive biomechanical analyses to evaluate the influence of
screw type and configuration. He et al. [ 48 ] further compared contoured
locking plates (10 screws) with contoured nonlocking plates (12 screws).
There was also a trend toward hybrid fixation that combines minimally
invasive plates with percutaneous screw for better biomechanical
performance [ 48 , 49 , 52 ].
Several studies have explored advanced plate design and optimiza -
tion. Ouyang et al. [ 56 ] compared flat branch-like locking plates, full
plate locking designs, and topologically optimized configurations. Lv
et al. [ 51 ] examined multi-point fixation by comparing 2-point volar
distal plate fixation (locking screws of 2.4 to 2.7 mm) with 3-point
calcaneal locking plate systems (12 screws, 3.5 mm). Other studies
also evaluated implant stress across various plate designs, including
comparisons between frame-shaped and Y-shaped plates [ 65 ], as well as
plates from different manufacturers featuring distinct branching struc -
tures [ 66 ].
In addition to minimally invasive plate fixation, our review dem -
onstrates that minimally invasive internal fixation also includes screw-
only techniques and intramedullary nail systems. Screw-only strategies
utilized cannulated or absorbable screws arranged in specific configu -
rations, such as transverse or longitudinal placements, and were some -
times compared with plate fixation methods [ 49 , 54 , 60 , 61 , 63 ].
Intramedullary nail systems, including the Calcanail, C-nail, and
self-designed Ni-nail, were also evaluated, with some studies comparing
these nails to plate fixation or assessing the effect of supplemental
screws on construct stability [ 51 , 55 , 58 , 64 ]. Fig. 3 provides a visual
overview of the primary fixation techniques, including plating,
screw-only, and intramedullary nail systems.

model reconstruction was Mimics (Materialise NV, Leuven, Belgium).

differentiate between trabecular and cortical bone, modelling the

However, 17 studies reported segmentation of cortical and trabecular
components. While several studies did not detail their segmentation
methodology, the majority implemented a pre-specified thickness
approach, most commonly used 2 mm is the cortical shell thickness.

studies employed Hypermesh (Altair Engineering Inc., Troy, United

ments) [ 47 , 50 , 53 ]. An additional two studies reported the use of C3D10
elements specifically for encapsulated soft tissue components [ 45 , 46 ].
The selection of higher-order elements provides better solution accuracy
at increased computational cost [ 42 ].

5.3. Loading scheme
The studies summarized in Table 4 reveal considerable heterogeneity
in loading schemes, boundary conditions, and simulation strategies. This
diversity stems primarily from differences in how physiological loads
are conceptualized, both in their simplification of real-world mechanics
and in the classification of use-case scenarios, such as balanced standing,
single-stance standing, and walking stance. Furthermore, loading ap -
proaches included both the top-down modality (proximal body weight
transfer) and the bottom-up modality (ground reaction force input), as
shown in Fig. 5 .
In simulations of balanced and single-stance standing, almost all
studies prescribed axial loading as a percentage of body weight, most
commonly representing either partial (approximately 50% BW) or full
(100% BW) support [ 45 , 46 , 49 , 55 , 58 – 61 , 64 , 67 ]. When a talus was
included in the model, the axial load was transmitted through the talar
dome [ 48 , 49 , 59 ]; otherwise, the load was imposed directly on the
subtalar joint's articular surface of the calcaneus. To achieve global
equilibrium in these configurations, the base of the calcaneus was
typically fully constrained. Both top-down approaches — where load is
transmitted from proximal elements — and bottom-up approaches — -
where the load is applied as a ground reaction force at the plantar sur -
face — were observed. Several studies further introduced an Achilles
tendon force [ 45 , 46 , 50 , 60 , 61 ], generally set at about half the ground
reaction force, to better simulate the antagonistic muscle activity
required for maintaining standing equilibrium. In addition, some ana -
lyses varied the magnitude of applied axial loads, either to mimic
higher-impact activities such as jumping [ 65 ], or to conduct sensitivity
analyses assessing the effect of incremental loading [ 56 , 66 ].
A subset of studies departing from purely axial loadings implemented
multidirectional constraints or load distributions. In these models, not
only was the base of the calcaneus fixed, but additional constraints were
applied at distal joints (notably the calcaneocuboid joint) [ 48 , 49 , 52 , 53 ,
59 , 65 ], often along the anterior-posterior direction. Certain analyses
distributed the applied force across multiple calcaneal articular surfaces
in unequal ratios to better reflect anatomical loading paths [ 48 , 57 ].
However, in several cases, the rationale and distribution specifics were
not clearly detailed; some studies simply labelled their approach as
“ neutral" without further explanation.

5.4. Model verification and validation
Nearly half of the studies ( n = 11) did not perform model verifica -
tion, and among those that did, many failed to report sufficient meth -
odological details. Among the studies that conducted model verification,
ten employed mesh convergence testing to assess discretization errors in
FE approximations. This methodology involves reducing the element
size from coarse to fine until the results converge, indicating that the
discretization error has been minimized to an acceptable level. How -
ever, only four studies provided complete transparency regarding both
their convergence metrics and specific procedures used [ 46 , 54 , 62 , 64 ].
The convergence metrics varied considerably across studies and
included measures, such as displacement of bone, von Mises stress on
implants, peak plantar pressure, and construct stiffness. Additionally, Yu
et al. [ 66 ] examined total strain energy, a parameter that has been used
in other FE studies to assess the screw-bone interface [ 72 ]. Interestingly,
Su et al. [ 62 ] conducted their convergence test using the peak
compressive principal stress of the first metatarsal shaft, which is un -
usual, as this site is not the primary focus in the context of calcaneal
fracture studies.
Where convergence criteria were explicitly stated, the acceptable
deviation ranges varied from 2% to 10%, with 2% being the most
commonly adopted threshold. Two studies employed mesh quality
assessment as an alternative verification method, utilizing software-
integrated quality checks. These assessments typically evaluate the
aspect ratio of elements, element skewness, and element shape quality
via internal angle distribution.

the physical reality of interest and assesses whether the model is suffi -
ciently credible for its intended application [ 73 , 74 ]. Among the

6. Conceptual thematic analysis
6.1. Outcome measures or metrics
Outcome measures include radiological parameters like B €ohler and
Gissane angles, mechanical metrics such as construct stiffness and
displacement at bone fragments, joint facets, and implants, along with
fracture gap analyses. Stress evaluation commonly uses von Mises stress
complemented by principal stresses. Fig. 4 provides a framework for
outcome measures applied in the reviewed studies.
Radiological parameters, particularly the B €ohler and Gissane angles,
are key outcome measures for assessing the anatomic restoration ach -
ieved by various fixation techniques in calcaneal fracture management.
Five studies [ 45 , 46 , 51 , 64 , 67 ] evaluated improvement using the B €ohler
angle, which is particularly sensitive to changes in calcaneal height and
overall compression, thus reflecting the effectiveness of the fixation in
re-establishing the native calcaneal structure. The Gissane angle,
assessed in three studies [ 51 , 64 , 67 ], specifically monitors restoration of
the articular surface, with its normalization indicating successful
re-alignment and integrity of the subtalar joint.
Distance-based metrics were frequently employed to evaluate the
effectiveness of fixation strategies. Construct stiffness, assessed in five
studies [ 54 – 56 , 64 , 66 ], served as a simplified yet crucial indicator of
mechanical integrity and resistance to deformation under load, which is
also an important validation metric as previously described.
Displacement remained an important outcome metric, though its
relevance and interpretation depend heavily on the scope monitored.
Ten studies reported the overall displacement of calcaneus [ 49 , 51 , 53 ,
56 , 58 – 60 , 62 , 64 , 67 ], identifying the maximum translations (and thus
deformation) under load. Three studies examined the displacement of
individual bone fragments [ 52 , 61 , 63 ], aiming to analysis interfrag -
mentary misplacement, which might correlate to fracture site stability.
Another three studies focused on the displacement at the joint facet,
reflecting local congruity following fixation [ 47 , 51 , 64 ]. Additionally,
five studies monitored displacement in implant, aiming to detect po -
tential screw loosening or implant migration [ 45 , 49 , 53 , 60 , 61 ].
A commonly held assumption in the literature associates displace -
ment, particularly interfragmentary or hardware movement, with me -
chanical instability or an increased risk of compromised union.
However, this interpretation requires contextual refinement. While
displacement typically reflects construct deformation under loading
(often with one end fixed and the other end loaded), a higher absolute
displacement does not necessarily imply greater instability. For
example, both sides of a fracture site may deform in a coordinated
manner with minimal relative motion, or the plate and screws may move
as a unified structure rather than exhibiting decoupled motion, with
high displacement values in these cases not necessarily indicating a risk
of malunion or hardware loosening, respectively. In these contexts,
some studies have investigated relative displacement to analyse true
local instability [ 48 , 52 ], or developed specific metrics to assess the
fracture gap.
Fracture gap analysis is a critical yet inconsistently defined outcome
metric, with studies employing geometrical definitions, node-pairing
strategies, or surrogate modelling to approximate interfragmentary
behaviour. Yao et al. [ 65 ] quantified the fracture gap by directly
measuring medial and lateral distances at predefined anatomical loca -
tions along the fracture line. He et al. [ 48 ] introduced a node-based
method to calculate relative displacement between paired nodes in
unloaded and loaded states. Building on this approach, Ni et al. [ 54 ]
developed the 'fracture micromotion' metric, which was later adapted by
Xu et al. [ 64 ] and Zhang et al. [ 67 ] for the posterior joint facet and

metric, representing the average inter-nodal distance and capturing
interfragmentary separation along the articular surface, was calculated
by selecting node pairs along the fracture interface (16 pairs in Ni's
study) and computing the average Euclidean distance between paired
nodes under both unloaded and loaded conditions. Mathematically, the

6.2. Key findings and implications
As shown in Fig. 6 , Supplementary Tables S2 and S5 , the compara -
tive analyses in the literature can be broadly categorized into studies
that compare ORIF (with locking or nonlocking plates) versus MIF (with
minimally invasive plates, pins, or intramedullary nails) ( n = 9), those
analysing different setups or types within ORIF ( n = 9) and within MIF

ative element ( n = 2). All but one study investigated moderate-to-severe
intra-articular fractures (Sanders Type II and III); one study focused on
extra-articular (avulsion) fractures, as shown in Fig. 6 .

configuration (115.83 MPa vs 52.43 MPa and 21.02 MPa). He et al. [ 48 ]

Comparative analyses within ORIF approaches have demonstrated
the significance of screw configuration optimization and plate design
characteristics in determining biomechanical performance. Chen et al.
[ 45 ] systematic evaluation of hybrid locking and nonlocking screw
configurations revealed that the optimal construct employed four lock -

showed that additional screw placement reduced both stress concen -

multi-directional fixation strategies. Ouyang et al. [ 56 ] topology opti -

achieved a 12.3% volume reduction while maintaining construct stiff -

branch-like designs, highlighting the potential for improved implant
efficiency through reduced material volume without compromising
mechanical performance. The reduced implant bulk can also potentially
minimize soft tissue irritation.
Comparative analyses within MIF approaches demonstrate the
importance of supplementary screw augmentation in optimizing
biomechanical performance. Song et al. [ 61 ] investigation revealed that
minimally invasive plate fixation with one medial support screw
(0.29 mm) achieved superior displacement control compared to isolated
plate (0.42 mm) or screw-only techniques (0.41 mm), while Xu et al.
[ 64 ] comparison of C-nail versus Ni-nail systems showed similar per -

screw augmentation significantly reduced relative displacement (0.2 –
4.0 μ m vs 1.0 – 4.3 μ m) at fracture sites across different fracture classes.

nuanced impacts on calcaneal fixation stability. Qiang et al. [ 59 ]'s

7. Methodological Quality Assessment
The MQSSFE total scores ranged from 14 to 33 out of a possible 37
points. The average score across all studies was 23.2 (63%), with a
median score of 22 (59%). Only four studies (17%) achieved a score
above 75% of the total possible score (i.e., ≥ 28 points), indicating high
methodological quality. As shown in Fig. 7 , across the six domains, the
“ study design and presentation ” domain had the highest average score,
achieving 80% of the possible points. In contrast, the “ model verification
and validation ” domain scored the lowest, with an average of 47% of
possible points. A more detailed analysis of this “ weak ” domain identi -
fied three main deficiencies: Q34 (limitations of model validation dis -
cussed), Q35 (limitations on external validity and single-subject design
discussed), and Q36 (improvement or discussion of internal validation,
such as mesh convergence testing, uncertainty, and variability analysis).
These items were sufficiently addressed in only 35% (8/23), 43% (10/
23), and 48% (11/23) of studies, respectively. This pattern reflects an
ongoing challenge in the field, as discussions on the limitations of model
validation, internal validity measures (such as mesh convergence
testing), and external validity remain underemphasized.
Item 9 (characteristics of model subject clearly described) was
consistently scored “ Yes ” across all studies, while item 5 was not
applicable (also scored all “ Yes ” ), as noted in the Methods. Conversely,
Item 11 (model subject is representative of the population) was score
“ No ” in all studies, as all relied on health-intact model surrogates to
represent patients.
8. Discussion
The extensive FE analyses of calcaneal fracture fixation have pro -
vided crucial biomechanical insights that improve our understanding of
optimal treatment strategies for moderate-to-severe (Sanders Type II
and III) fractures. ORIF approaches demonstrated superior mechanical
stability with enhanced stiffness and fracture control, but generated
higher stress compared to MIF. Minimally invasive approaches, partic -
ularly when augmented with strategic supplementary screw configura -
tions, emerge as biomechanically viable alternatives, with hybrid
strategies outperforming isolated methods. Most significantly, emerging
topological optimization techniques enable computationally-optimized
constructs that achieve substantial volume reduction while maintain -
ing or enhancing mechanical performance. This topology-optimized
methodology could support the evolution toward personalized fixation
strategies that optimize mechanical performance while minimizing
surgical invasiveness. Economically, FE analysis reduces design time
and cost by enabling rapid, iterative optimization and minimizing the
need for expensive physical prototypes and tests [ 77 ]. Clinically, FE
analysis enhances safety and performance by serving as a “ digital twin, ”
capable of simulating complex loading scenarios and identifying po -
tential failure modes early in the implant design process [ 78 ]. While
finite element analysis has historically been viewed as a preclinical
modelling stage, the contemporary orthopaedic device roadmap
increasingly integrates computational evidence (including in-silico
clinical trials) throughout the entire lifecycle, from design verification
and regulatory submission to complementing traditional clinical trials
and supporting market release and post-market surveillance [ 79 ].
As illustrated in Fig. 5 , the current landscape of finite element (FE)
analysis in calcaneal fracture fixation research predominantly focuses on
moderate-to-severe intra-articular fractures, particularly Sanders Type II
and III. Most studies investigated these types using open reduction and
internal fixation (ORIF) with locking or nonlocking plates, as well as
minimally invasive plating techniques. Only one study addressed an
extra-articular (avulsion) fracture, and none examined Sanders Type I or
IV fractures. This underrepresentation reflects typical clinical

management: mild (Type I) fractures are usually minimally displaced
and treated conservatively or with simple percutaneous fixation [ 1 , 80 ],
while severe (Type IV) fractures often require arthrodesis [ 1 ]. Both ex -
tremes of the injury spectrum are less attractive for FE analysis, even
though arthrodesis itself has been modelled for other conditions [ 81 ,
82 ].
Our review of the FE literature reveals a notable prevalence of
Sanders subtypes IIB and IIIAB. The focus on subtype IIB can be attrib -
uted to its distinct clinical and modelling characteristics: its fracture line
crosses the central, primary weight-bearing region of the posterior facet,
often causing more significant articular displacement than subtypes IIA
or IIC and thus presenting a stronger indication for surgical repair [ 69 ].
The subtype IIIAB is preferentially selected for computational simula -
tions due to its distinct fragment morphology. This pattern preserves the
sustentaculum tali as a large, intact block, simplifying segmentation
[ 69 ]. Conversely, the IIIBC subtype has been less frequently studied,
likely owing to the complexities associated with modelling its unstable
sustentacular fragment and establishing reliable boundary conditions
for analysis. The IIIAC subtype, while also having a stable medial col -
umn, is less frequently modelled because it lacks the challenges of
combined central depression and lateral blowout that makes IIIAB a
more meaningful test for fixation techniques. It is important to note,
however, that there is a lack of epidemiological studies quantifying the
prevalence of specific subtypes. One study suggested that subtype IIA,
despite its lower representation in FE literature, may be more prevalent
among all type II fractures [ 83 ].
The modelling approaches in the reviewed studies present several
key limitations. A predominant issue is the widespread use of simplified,
partial-foot models (often calcaneus-only), which inadequately
represent the complex biomechanical environment of the foot. These
models typically apply simplified boundary conditions, such as pure
compressive forces on the subtalar joint, counteracted at the plantar
surface. This approach omits the critical influence of surrounding soft
tissues and the complex loading dynamics generated by muscle forces
and shifts in the centre of pressure, which create significant moment
loads. The consequences of such simplifications are not trivial. For
instance, one reviewed study that utilized a comprehensive full-foot
model identified stress concentrations at the plantar fascia or ligament
junctions [ 67 ], a finding different from the stress patterns reported in
calcaneus-only models. The omission of soft tissue constraints and
physiological muscle forces likely leads to a significant underestimation
of the true loads experienced by the calcaneus. In fact, research by
Giddings et al. [ 84 ] which focused on estimating in-vivo calcaneal loads
during walking stance, suggested they can reach over 5 times body
weight, a magnitude that far exceeds the simplified loading conditions
applied in most of the FE studies we reviewed.
A significant methodological limitation across all reviewed studies is
the exclusive reliance on static or quasi-static simulations. The most
common loading scenarios modelled were balanced standing or single-
leg stance, with a few studies examining specific instants of the gait
cycle. This preference for static methodologies using pure loading con -
ditions is largely driven by established protocols for the mechanical
testing of orthopaedic implants, such as those outlined by the ASTM
astm:F2502 standard for plate-and-screw fixation [ 85 ], despite that
they primarily govern the mechanical performance or failure but not
biological outcome. Adhering to such standards facilitates not only
regulatory compliance and inter-study comparison but also simplifies
the process of experimental validation using cadavers, sawbones, or

3D-printed surrogates. However, this approach fails to capture the dy -

Critical, high-impact scenarios—such as heel-strike during gait, lunge
during sport manoeuvre, landing from a height, or unanticipated
movements like a misstep—impose complex, transient loads that are
more likely to challenge implant stability and lead to failure. While
other FE domains simulated such dynamic events [ 86–89 ], their absence
in the context of calcaneal fracture fixation is a considerable gap.
Furthermore, the lack of sensitivity analyses investigating the effects of
variable loading rates and impact velocities represents a significant
methodological oversight in the current literature. In addition, most
reviewed studies modelled bone as a linear elastic and isotropic mate -
rial, neglecting the time-dependent viscoelastic properties and potential
changes in bone stiffness during the healing process. This common
simplification may limit the physiological relevance of simulation re -
sults, as dynamic loading scenarios and fracture healing are influenced
by nonlinear and temporally varying mechanical properties.
Displacement, global stiffness (construct stiffness), and von Mises
stress are among the most commonly reported mechanical outcome
measures in the reviewed literature. While these metrics are useful for
comparative mechanical testing and for assessing risks such as potential
implant failure (e.g., high von Mises stress), their direct association with
clinically meaningful outcomes, such as bone healing, remains limited.
For example, as previously noted, a higher absolute displacement does
not necessarily indicate instability; instead, it may reflect a more flexible
fixation construct that could support secondary bone healing. In fact, the
local mechanical environment within the fracture gap provides impor -
tant indicators for osteogenesis. According to Perren's strain theory and
subsequent mechano-regulation models, interfragmentary strain serves
as a critical biomechanical stimulus that governs the mode of fracture
healing [ 90–92 ]. an interfragmentary strain of 2% to 10% is considered
optimal for robust secondary bone healing. Strain below 2% provides
insufficient mechanical stimulation for significant callus formation,
while strain exceeding 10% risks excessive motion, tissue damage, and
non-union [ 92 ]. Consequently, several FE studies of other bones have
reported related metrics, such as interfragmentary stress fields and
strain energy density adjacent to the implant, to better capture the
biological implications of fixation [ 72 , 75 ]. Future research should
continue to incorporate these localized interfragmentary metrics to
improve the predictive value for bone healing outcomes. Nevertheless,
determining the optimal range for such measures remains challenging,
as the relationship is not simply linear or monotonic.
Current FE studies relied on single intact healthy foot models to
simulate fractures. While this provided consistent baseline for compar -
ison, artificial fractures may fail to sufficiently represent reality. Real
calcaneal fractures exhibit complex morphology with curved lines, inter-
fragmentary cracks, variable gap distances, and bone defects, which
cannot be captured by simplified sharp geometric cuts. One of our
reviewed articles demonstrated that bone defect substantially influences
fixation stability and stress distributions [ 62 ]. To address this limitation,
we propose implementing sensitivity testing using multiple
patient-specific fractured models rather than single idealized geome -
tries. This approach would leverage augmented reality (AR) technology
for surgeon-guided virtual reduction [ 93–95 ], potentially enhanced by
deep learning algorithms to assist with fragment alignment and reduc -
tion optimization [ 96 , 97 ]. After virtual reduction, probabilistic fracture
mapping can quantify the distribution of observed special features and
fracture line/surface. Sensitivity analyses can be performed by boot -
strapping across these features or by leveraging statistical geometrical
models or generative diffusion models to produce variations in fracture
morphology [ 98–101 ].
Our opposition to clinical validation of FE models is supported by
literature evidence [ 102 , 103 ] and reinforced by problematic mapping of
clinical validation metrics in our review, despite acknowledging that
other forms of model validation remain essential. Our position is
grounded in the irreconcilable epistemological differences between
theoretical FE models and clinical trial methodologies, the intended
preclinical function of these models rather than clinical routine appli -
cation, and the inherent biological limitations that prevent the models
form accounting for complex physiological processes. We believe that FE
models and clinical studies fundamentally adopt different philosophical
approaches to knowledge generation that create inherent in -
compatibilities and distinct assumptions when applied to the same
research question [ 104 ].
FE modelling produces predictable and reproducible outputs
through theory-driven, deterministic mechanistic understanding of
material behaviour and structural mechanics. Our review found that all
studies used intact healthy bone models to create virtual fractures and
test various fixation strategies. This uniformity ensures that differences
in biomechanical metrics arise from implant design rather than
anatomical variability, enhancing the internal validity of biomechanical
comparisons within FE analysis. In contrast, clinical studies seek to
establish empirical relationships through statistical inference method -
ologies that account for stochastic confounding factors and random
variability inherent in biological systems and patient populations (i.e.,
external validity).
While some studies have argued for the necessity of clinical valida -
tion for clinical translation [ 105 , 106 ], we believe that FE models are
primarily intended for preclinical purposes, including implant design
optimization, understanding biomechanical principles, and comparative
analysis of different fixation strategies under controlled conditions
rather than for rigorous clinical decision-making or patient-specific
diagnosis. FE models cannot output concrete biological outcomes and
fundamentally cannot account for non-mechanical factors such as
healing responses, patient compliance, and behavioural variations that
are critical for direct clinical translation [ 107 ]. Model validation should
be conducted with the intended context of use and associated risks as
specified by the ASME V&V40 standard [ 74 ], focusing on mechanical
quantities in this case rather than clinical correlation. Recognizing the
limitations of mechanical models in biological contexts highlights why
clinical validation is inappropriate and potentially misleading for pre -
clinical FE studies, as it conflates engineering predictions with phe -
nomena beyond the scope of modelling [ 108 ]. While we oppose clinical
validation of computational models, we believe clinical studies, some -
times referred to as 'ultimate validation', shall still be conducted on the
introduced implants, procedures, or decisions [ 78 ]. However, these
should be post-hoc evaluations separate from model validation, pre -
serving appropriate epistemological boundaries.
Beyond comparative analysis, FE modelling has demonstrated suc -
cessful impact in implant optimization, particularly in advancing the
shift from ORIF to MIF while striving to maintain comparable fixation
stiffness or stability, as illustrated in this review. A concrete scenario is
exemplified by the work of Ni's team, who initially investigated sup -
plementary screws on existing Calcanail system [ 55 ] and in another
study, adapted smaller radial plates to repair calcaneal fractures [ 51 ].
Their experience culminated in the development of proprietary Ni-nail,
an intramedullary nail incorporating knowledge on sustentaculum tali
screw and supplementary longitudinal screws to enhance fixation
strength within a minimally invasive setting [ 64 ]. Another successful
application involves the topology optimization of an ORIF implant,
which reduces it volume by 12.3%, maintaining sufficient fixation
strength, potentially transforming it into a MIF [ 56 ]. While this review
primarily focuses on upstream biomechanical research and FE studies in
calcaneal fracture fixation have not yet incorporated clinical endpoints,
we acknowledge that clinical trials remain crucial for verifying
design-level and decision-level findings prior to clinical deployment,
despite our previous argument that platform-level clinical validation
may not be necessary. Unfortunately, we did not find direct clinical
evaluation research of FE-driven designs specifically for calcaneal frac -
ture fixation. Nevertheless, the utility of FE-driven designs in improving
clinical and manufacturing outcomes has been demonstrated in other
orthopaedic areas. For instance, FE-guided implant design has been

This literature review has several limitations. Standard concerns
include potential publication bias, limited database and language se -
lection. Notably, we did not assess how each reviewed paper imple -
mented indirect validation, such as benchmarking against previously
published numerical or experimental results. Furthermore, there are
frequent methodological ambiguities, for example, reporting “ stress ”
without clarifying if this refers to von Mises, principal or other stress
types. In addition, we made pragmatic assumption based on context or
prevailing protocol, for example, classifying uncategorized plate fixa -
tion as locking when not specified ( n = 3), which could introduce bias or
misclassification. Another limitation was that we did not perform a
quantitative meta-analysis or a statistically meaningful subgroup com -
parison of key biomechanical indicators (e.g., structural stiffness, peak
stress) across different fixation methods or fracture types. This decision
was driven by the pervasive methodological heterogeneity and the na -
ture of the models reviewed. Specifically, nearly all included studies
utilized single-subject models, meaning that any observed differences in
results are confounded by patient-specific anatomy and bone quality,
making statistical comparison across studies unreliable. Furthermore,
the variability in model parameters, including different levels of model
simplification (e.g., calcaneus-only vs. whole-foot models), diverse
implant brands and materials, and non-standardized surgical simulation
techniques (e.g., screw placement and orientation), introduces too much
noise for a robust quantitative synthesis and might lead to misleading
and potentially spurious results. Finally, we extracted detailed protocols
and quantitative modelling data from existing studies. We aim to pro -
vide a clear methodological framework for future investigators and a
data resource that can support subsequent indirect validation within the
field.
9. Concluding remarks
This systematic scoping review has comprehensively mapped the
current landscape of finite element analysis in calcaneal fracture fixa -
tion, revealing an evolving paradigm where MIF techniques, particularly
when augmented with supplementary screws, demonstrate potential to
achieve comparable biomechanical performance to the established gold
standard of ORIF with locking plate systems. Computational modelling
has evolved from descriptive stress distribution analyses of individual
implants to comparative implant evaluations, and now toward implant
design optimization using topology optimization approaches in FE
analysis.
However, critical limitations constrain clinical translation, including
the overwhelming reliance on single-subject models, which lacks
generalizability; surrogate model designs that artificially create frac -
tures on intact healthy models, ignoring the complexity of real fracture
patterns; and simplified loading conditions that may not adequately
challenge fixation safety. Future studies should prioritize developing
population-level models, such as bootstrapping approaches, statistical
shape models, or generative diffusion models based on fracture patterns
and characteristics. Dynamic loading scenarios with sensitivity testing of
loading rates, such as heel-strike, lunge, and fall conditions, should also
be implemented.
Despite the clear utility of FEA in comparative biomechanical
assessment and design optimization, a significant challenge remains in
establishing definitive clinical thresholds or indications for biome -
chanical variables. The inherent difficulty in translating FEA output into
a clinical prognosis stems from the biological complexity of fracture
healing and the lack of consensus standards for reporting and validation.
Moving forward, the clinical utility of FEA will be maximized by the
development of consensus standards that can bridge the gap between
computational metrics and patient-specific clinical outcomes.
